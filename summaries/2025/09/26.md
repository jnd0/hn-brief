## [Open Social](https://overreacted.io/open-social/)
**Score:** 991 | **Comments:** 388 | **ID:** 45388021

> **Article:** The article "The Open Social Stack" by Dan Abramov argues that the current social media landscape is dominated by closed, siloed platforms, but a new "open social" movement is emerging. The author contrasts two key protocols: ActivityPub (powering Mastodon) and AT Protocol (powering Bluesky). The core argument is that while ActivityPub creates a federated network of servers that don't have a global view, AT Protocol enables true global aggregation. This allows any application built on the protocol to have a consistent, real-time view of the entire network, breaking down the instance-based fragmentation seen in Mastodon. The article presents this as a path to building competitive, open social products that can rival closed giants like Twitter and Facebook.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion reveals a mix of optimism and skepticism about the future of open social protocols. Key points include:

*   **Interoperability vs. Centralization:** Users debate whether Bluesky's approach, which allows for global aggregation, is truly decentralized. Some argue it's a form of centralization, while others see it as a necessary step to compete with closed platforms and avoid the fragmented user experience of Mastodon.
*   **User Experience:** There is a strong consensus that for open social to succeed, the underlying complexity must be invisible to the average user. The focus should be on delivering a great product, not on educating users about protocols.
*   **Adoption and Apathy:** A major concern is the general public's apathy towards concepts like data ownership and interoperability. Many users are content with the convenience of closed platforms (e.g., TikTok, Instagram) and won't switch for abstract principles. The challenge is to make open social not just a "niche hobby."
*   **Technical and Economic Hurdles:** The discussion touches on practical challenges, such as the performance of highly popular accounts (e.g., Justin Bieber), the cost of running infrastructure (like DNS), and the difficulty of bootstrapping new networks against powerful network effects.
*   **Historical Precedent:** Some commenters point to past failed attempts at open social standards (like OpenSocial in 2007) as a warning, while others argue that new protocols like AT Protocol have different goals and technical underpinnings.

## [Fast UDP I/O for Firefox in Rust](https://max-inden.de/post/fast-udp-io-in-firefox/)
**Score:** 367 | **Comments:** 108 | **ID:** 45387462

> **Article:** The article details Mozilla's effort to improve Firefox's UDP I/O performance, specifically for its HTTP/3 and QUIC networking stack. The initial implementation was performing poorly, with a bottleneck limiting throughput to around 1 Gbit/s. By analyzing system calls and collaborating with a reporter to reproduce a hardware-specific issue, the team identified that the generic `sendmsg`/`recvmsg` system calls were the cause.

The solution was to adopt modern, platform-specific APIs that allow for batched I/O operations:
- **Linux:** `sendmmsg`/`recvmmsg`
- **macOS:** `sendmsg_x`/`recvmsg_x`
- **Windows:** `WSASendMsg`

The article also discusses the challenges of using "Generic Segmentation Offload" (GSO) across different operating systems. The result of these changes was a significant performance improvement, increasing UDP throughput from under 1 Gbit/s to over 4 Gbit/s in CPU-bound scenarios, which also reduces CPU usage and improves power efficiency.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion focused on several key themes:

*   **Performance and Bottlenecks:** Users were impressed by the 400% throughput increase and its implications for power efficiency. However, one commenter argued that 4 Gbit/s is still relatively slow for modern hardware, suggesting underlying inefficiencies in the network stack or QUIC's encryption overhead, which was countered by others pointing to the costs of system calls and state management.

*   **Platform API Maturity:** There was surprise that `sendmmsg`/`recvmmsg` are considered "modern" given their age. A technical sub-thread clarified that `io_uring` on Linux doesn't offer a direct equivalent for batching UDP datagrams, making GSO/GRO and the older `mmsg` calls the preferred methods for high-performance UDP.

*   **Platform-Specific Issues:** Commenters noted the author's mention of bugs in the GSO/GRO implementations on Windows and macOS, expressing frustration with the quality of network stacks on major operating systems.

*   **Firefox-Specific Topics:** A tangential discussion arose about Firefox's policy on self-signed certificates for HTTP/3. A Mozilla developer clarified that this is a security policy decision, not a technical limitation tied to the new UDP I/O library.

*   **Open Source Collaboration:** The article's use of the `quinn-udp` library was praised, leading to a brief debate about Mozilla's responsibility to financially support the open-source projects it depends on.

## [Investigating a Forged PDF](https://mjg59.dreamwidth.org/73317.html)
**Score:** 319 | **Comments:** 50 | **ID:** 45381010

> **Article:** The article, by security developer Matthew Garrett, details his experience investigating a forged PDF. After his landlord failed to return a security deposit, the property management agency provided a PDF of a lease addendum, allegedly signed by the landlord, which supposedly waived the deposit. Garrett suspected forgery. By examining the document's metadata, he discovered it had been created using RightSignature (a digital signing service) *after* the date it was allegedly signed. He proved this by accessing the document's certification page on the RightSignature website, which showed a "draft" version was uploaded and signed, not the final document. The agency had simply edited the PDF to add the fraudulent addendum after the legitimate signing process was complete, and the digital signature on the certification page only validated the original, unaltered document.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion centered on the technical, legal, and societal aspects of the forgery. Key points include:
*   **Technical Solutions:** Commenters noted that standard PDF specifications support robust digital signatures that can detect tampering, and services like DocuSign implement this correctly. The failure here was that RightSignature's system only provided a web link for verification, not an embedded, verifiable signature.
*   **Legal Advice:** Many users advised Garrett to pursue legal action, suggesting he contact law enforcement (FBI, local financial crimes units) for criminal fraud charges and hire a lawyer on a contingency basis to sue for the deposit and damages. Some suggested a class-action lawsuit might be possible if the agency has a pattern of this behavior.
*   **Societal Impact:** A prominent discussion thread, featuring user 'gwern', used this case as an example of how easy and common PDF forgery is. They argued that despite the existence of technical solutions, proving forgery remains a difficult, time-consuming process that requires significant expertise, making it an effective tool for low-level fraud.
*   **Prevention:** A commenter shared a similar personal experience and discussed preventative measures, such as maintaining multiple signed copies of all documents (physical or digital) at the time of signing.
