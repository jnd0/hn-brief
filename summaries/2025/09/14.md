## [OCSP Service Has Reached End of Life](https://letsencrypt.org/2025/08/06/ocsp-service-has-reached-end-of-life)
**Score:** 217 | **Comments:** 101 | **ID:** 45242591

> **Article:** Let's Encrypt announced that its Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP) service has reached end-of-life and will be shut down. OCSP is a protocol used by browsers to check if a website's TLS certificate has been revoked. Let's Encrypt is ceasing this service because its certificates have very short lifespans (90 days, soon to be 47), making real-time revocation checks less critical. The organization will instead rely on Certificate Revocation Lists (CRLs) and is encouraging the industry to adopt more modern revocation strategies.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is largely in favor of Let's Encrypt's decision, with many commenters viewing OCSP as a flawed and obsolete technology. The consensus is that OCSP is a "fail-open" system (if the check fails, the browser often proceeds anyway), creates privacy risks by leaking user browsing habits to CAs, and adds unnecessary complexity.

Key points of debate and information include:
*   **The Rise of Modern Revocation:** Commenters note that browsers have already moved away from OCSP. Firefox uses CRLite, a system that aggregates revocation data into a downloadable list, and Chrome uses CRLSets, a similar curated list pushed from Google. These systems solve the privacy and reliability issues of traditional OCSP.
*   **Short-Lived Certificates as the Solution:** A major theme is that the need for real-time revocation is greatly diminished when certificates only last 90 days. Re-issuing a certificate is now a trivial, automated process, making it a more effective revocation mechanism than a complex protocol.
*   **OCSP Stapling as a Historical Compromise:** Some users argue that OCSP stapling was a decent solution in an era of long-lived certificates (5-10 years), as it solved the privacy issue by having the server provide the status. However, with short-lived certs, even this is becoming unnecessary.
*   **Non-Browser Use Cases:** A few dissenting voices point out that OCSP is still critical in non-TLS contexts (like secure boot or enterprise systems) where certificates are valid for many years and automated re-issuance isn't feasible. They worry that the web's move away from OCSP will harm these other ecosystems.
*   **Misconceptions about Browsers:** One thread corrects a user's surprise at Chrome's CRLSets, with others clarifying that this is now the industry standard and a more effective approach than traditional OCSP.
