## [Hosting a website on a disposable vape](https://bogdanthegeek.github.io/blog/projects/vapeserver/)
**Score:** 1415 | **Comments:** 472 | **ID:** 45252817

> **Article:** The article by BogdanTheGeek details a project to repurpose a disposable vape into a functional web server. By reverse-engineering the device's pinout and using the YUBA embedded framework, they successfully loaded a lightweight TCP/IP stack and a simple web server onto the vape. The project serves as a proof-of-concept, demonstrating the surprising computational power hidden within single-use consumer electronics and offering a guide for others to salvage and re-purpose these components.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion surrounding the project was multifaceted, touching on the technical implementation, the ethics of disposable electronics, and the social context of vaping.

A significant portion of the conversation focused on the immense technological waste inherent in disposable vapes. Commenters expressed astonishment that devices containing microcontrollers, and in some cases color screens and Bluetooth radios, are designed to be thrown away. This sparked a broader debate on the definition of "disposable," with users noting that many devices with rechargeable batteries are still marketed as single-use, possibly to circumvent regulations. The sheer cheapness of these components was also highlighted as a marvel of modern manufacturing.

Several technical questions were raised. Users inquired about the author's networking setup for exposing the home-hosted server to the internet, leading to discussions on security best practices like using a VPS as a secure proxy. The author also clarified that they had to place the server behind a more robust nginx proxy to handle the load from HN readers, humorously noting their router couldn't handle the traffic directly.

The discussion also took a more personal turn. One commenter, in a moment of vulnerability, shared their struggle with nicotine addiction and feeling like a "pathetic" person for craving a vape, which prompted supportive replies encouraging quitting. Finally, there was a practical interest in salvaging these devices for other DIY projects, such as building automatic cat feeders, and a debate over whether the author should have named the specific vape brand to help others identify salvageable units.

## [Language models pack billions of concepts into 12k dimensions](https://nickyoder.com/johnson-lindenstrauss/)
**Score:** 368 | **Comments:** 142 | **ID:** 45245948

> **Article:** The article, referencing a YouTube video, explains the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma to argue that language models can efficiently pack a vast number of concepts into relatively low-dimensional spaces (e.g., 12,000 dimensions). The core idea is that any set of high-dimensional data points can be projected into a lower-dimensional space while approximately preserving the distances between them. This mathematical principle allows models to represent billions of concepts without requiring an impractically large number of dimensions, making them computationally feasible.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion offers a mix of clarification, technical depth, and skepticism regarding the article's claims. A central thread involved users correcting each other's interpretations of "orthogonality" in the context of binary vectors, with the consensus being that it relates to the XOR operation in a finite field, not just non-overlapping bits.

Several users questioned the technical details, such as the use of a ReLU in the loss function and the interpretation of constants in the lemma's formula. The most substantive contributions explored the limitations of the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma. One user argued that while the lemma preserves pairwise distances, it may fail to preserve the underlying "topology" or manifold structure of the data. This could lead to "folding" artifacts where points that are far apart in the original space become close after projection, potentially explaining some of the strange behaviors observed in LLMs. Another user cautioned that the real challenge isn't keeping orthogonal vectors separate, but maintaining the correct distance *ordering* between non-orthogonal vectors, suggesting the article's optimism about representational capacity might be overstated.

Finally, there was a notable side-discussion about the article's writing style, with several commenters finding its "ChatGPT vibe" distracting and overly formal, a point that was itself made ironically by a commenter who used similar phrasing.

## [Folks, we have the best π](https://lcamtuf.substack.com/p/folks-we-have-the-best)

The standard Euclidean metric (n=2) corresponds to the familiar Pi (~3.14). The author shows that if you define distance using the Lp-norm (where distance from the origin is |x|^n + |y|^n = 1), the calculated value of Pi changes depending on n.
