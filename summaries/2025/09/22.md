## [Cap'n Web: a new RPC system for browsers and web servers](https://blog.cloudflare.com/capnweb-javascript-rpc-library/)
**Score:** 643 | **Comments:** 298 | **ID:** 45332883

> **Article:** The article introduces Cap'n Web, a new open-source RPC library for browser-to-server and server-to-server communication, created by Kenton Varda (author of Cap'n Proto). It is designed to be a simplified, more powerful alternative to existing solutions like tRPC, GraphQL, and JSON-RPC.

Key features include:
*   **Zero Round-Trip Time (RTT) Promise Pipelining:** The client can chain multiple calls together (e.g., `api.hello(api.getMyName())`) and send them in a single batch. The server executes the entire chain without waiting for intermediate results, sending back a single final response. This avoids network latency for dependent calls.
*   **Schemaless by Default:** It doesn't require a separate schema definition step, making it feel like a simple library, but it is strongly typed at runtime.
*   **Capability-Based Security:** Access is controlled by passing capability objects, not just by endpoint URLs.
*   **Advanced Data Fetching:** It includes a `.map()` function that allows the client to describe a computation to be performed on the server over a list of items, avoiding the N+1 query problem common in naive GraphQL implementations.

The library is available on npm and is designed to work in any JavaScript environment, including browsers, Node.js, and Cloudflare Workers.
>
> **Discussion:** The HN discussion was highly positive, with the original author (kentonv) actively participating. Key points of discussion were:

*   **Comparison to OCapN:** A user compared Cap'n Web to the OCapN standard, noting that Cap'n Web lacks "sturdyrefs" (out-of-band capability URIs) and "third-party handoffs." The author explained that these were omitted for simplicity and because their implementation is highly platform-dependent, suggesting OAuth as a potential web standard for the concept.
*   **Understanding "One Round Trip":** Users clarified the "one RTT" claim. It means the server only responds once, but the client can send multiple pipelined messages in that single request. The server executes the entire dependency chain on its side before responding.
*   **Potential for Broader Adoption:** A user asked about backend support for other languages like Rust. The author suggested a future proxy that could translate between Cap'n Web and the original Cap'n Proto, allowing type-safe backends to interoperate.
*   **Practical Use Cases:** Commenters saw value in simplifying complex connections like browser-to-worker or browser-to-iframe communication, and some expressed interest in using it as a replacement for tRPC.
*   **Minor Issues:** A user pointed out a copy-paste error in the article's code sample, which the author quickly fixed.

## [Why haven't local-first apps become popular?](https://marcobambini.substack.com/p/why-local-first-apps-havent-become)
**Score:** 505 | **Comments:** 485 | **ID:** 45333021

> **Article:** The article argues that local-first applications (apps that store data primarily on the user's device and sync peer-to-peer or to a personal cloud) have failed to become popular due to economic, not technical, reasons.

The author contends that the SaaS (Software as a Service) model is dominant because it provides a more reliable revenue stream (subscriptions) and allows for easier marketing and distribution. This model also acts as a form of DRM, ensuring developers retain control over their code and user data. While technical challenges like data synchronization exist, the author believes they are solvable, but the lack of a strong business incentive to solve them is the primary barrier to adoption.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely agrees with the article's conclusion but explores the nuances from multiple angles.

*   **Economic Incentives are Key:** The most upvoted comments argue that the SaaS business model (subscriptions, control, lack of DRM) is the primary driver, not technical feasibility. Cloud services have more money for marketing and polish, creating a powerful network effect.
*   **Technical Challenges are Real:** Several users push back on the idea that technical hurdles are trivial. They point to the inherent difficulty of distributed systems, the "lie" of a linear log of changes, and the CAP theorem. However, the consensus is that these are solvable problems if there were enough financial incentive.
*   **User Experience and Behavior:** A major theme is that users don't care about local-first principles until they face a problem (like privacy violations or data loss). Furthermore, users have become accustomed to the convenience of cloud sync and are unwilling to manage their own data or deal with complex conflict resolution.
*   **Collaboration vs. Control:** The debate highlights a core tension. Cloud apps (like Office 365) often provide a superior collaborative experience by avoiding sync conflicts ("conflicted copy" issues). Local-first advocates argue for data ownership and offline access, while cloud proponents prioritize seamless, real-time collaboration.
*   **Historical Context:** Commenters noted that local-first was the original paradigm (desktop apps) and that "local-first" is essentially a return to that model but with modern sync capabilities. Git was mentioned as a successful example of decentralized collaboration, though its user interface is not mainstream-friendly.

## [UK Millionaire exodus did not occur, study reveals](https://taxjustice.net/press/millionaire-exodus-did-not-occur-study-reveals/)
**Score:** 253 | **Comments:** 321 | **ID:** 45335135

> **Article:** A study by the Tax Justice Network refutes the widely reported 2024 "millionaire exodus" from the UK. The original claim, originating from Henley & Partners, stated that 9,500 millionaires left the country. The new study argues this figure represents only 0.3% of the UK's total millionaire population (3.06 million), making it a "near-0%" event and not a significant exodus. The study also notes that the Henley report uses a narrower definition of "millionaire" (liquid assets only), which further reduces the percentage of the population affected. The article concludes that the narrative of a mass exodus was exaggerated and used to influence tax policy decisions.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is highly skeptical of the article's conclusions and focuses on methodological and political biases. Key points of the debate include:

*   **Source Bias:** Many commenters immediately flagged the study's publishers (Tax Justice Network, Patriotic Millionaires UK) as biased interest groups, comparing their work to a study on gun violence conducted by the NRA. They argue the study is designed to support a pro-taxation agenda.

*   **Wrong Denominator:** A prominent critique is that the article uses the total number of UK millionaires as its denominator. Critics argue the relevant group for tax policy is the highly mobile, ultra-wealthy with liquid assets (who can actually leave), not the much larger population of "asset-rich, cash-poor" individuals (e.g., homeowners in London whose property value makes them millionaires on paper but who cannot easily relocate).

*   **Timing and Causality:** Some argued that the exodus concerns were about future policy (e.g., capital gains tax changes in 2025), so analyzing 2024 data is premature and misses the point.

*   **The "Margin" Argument:** A counter-argument suggested that even a small percentage of people leaving (on the margin) can have a significant negative impact, especially if they are high-tax-payers. This was compared to the slow decline of Detroit, where things seem stable until a tipping point is reached and the situation becomes irreversible.

*   **Media and Wealth Definitions:** The discussion touched on how media ownership and a conflation of "wealth" (house value) vs. "liquid assets" contribute to public misunderstanding of the issue.
