*   **User Frustration:** Many commenters express frustration with YouTube's "enshittification," viewing this as a hostile move against users. ## [How AWS S3 serves 1 petabyte per second on top of slow HDDs](https://bigdata.2minutestreaming.com/p/how-aws-s3-scales-with-tens-of-millions-of-hard-drives)

> **Article:** The article explains how Amazon S3 achieves massive throughput (1 petabyte per second) by using software-level parallelism to overcome the inherent speed limitations of individual hard disk drives (HDDs). Instead of relying on fast media, S3 distributes data across tens of millions of HDDs. When a request is made, the system reads from or writes to many drives simultaneously. This parallelism effectively aggregates the bandwidth of countless drives, turning a collection of "slow" hardware into a system capable of serving data at an incredible rate. The architecture relies on erasure coding (data sharding) to ensure durability and availability while maximizing this parallel performance.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion focuses on the scale, architecture, and open-source alternatives to S3. Key points include:

*   **Scale and Comparison:** Commenters estimated S3's total storage to be in the triple-digit exabytes, making it likely the largest storage system on Earth. Some compared its scale to large government data centers.
*   **Open-Source Alternatives:** A user asked about open-source solutions designed for HDDs. Suggestions included Gluster, Ceph, and Garage, with the consensus being that most other systems are optimized for flash storage and that achieving S3-like performance with HDDs requires an enormous scale (many datacenters) that is difficult to replicate.
*   **Technical Corrections and Nuances:** A commenter pointed out a factual inaccuracy in the article regarding the specific 5:9 sharding scheme, noting that S3 uses multiple schemes and that a 5:9 ratio would be too costly for HDDs. *   **Architecture Style:** The discussion touched on S3's architecture, described as a "Systems Oriented Architecture" with a few dozen large services rather than a monolith or thousands of microservices, to minimize network latency.

The flaw allows attackers with administrative access to the Baseboard Management Controller (BMC) to install persistent, "unremovable" malware. Because the compromised BMC can then prevent any subsequent legitimate firmware updates, the malware becomes effectively permanent, surviving reboots and standard OS re-installations. The only known recovery method is low-level hardware reprogramming of the flash chip.
