## [Functional programming and reliability: ADTs, safety, critical infrastructure](https://blog.rastrian.dev/post/why-reliability-demands-functional-programming-adts-safety-and-critical-infrastructure)
**Score:** 140 | **Comments:** 146 | **ID:** 46406901

> **Article:** The article argues that functional programming (FP) features, particularly Algebraic Data Types (ADTs) and strong static type systems (as found in languages like OCaml and Haskell), are essential for building highly reliable software in critical infrastructure like banking and telecom. The author posits that most production incidents are caused by the code entering states that should be impossible. By using ADTs to "make illegal states unrepresentable," developers can leverage the compiler to enforce correctness, ensuring that functions can only handle valid data and state transitions. This approach, combined with FP principles like immutability and pure functions, creates a "correct-by-construction" development style that significantly enhances reliability.
>
> **Discussion:** The HN discussion offers a nuanced and often critical perspective on the article's claims. Key points of debate include:

*   **FP vs. Strong Typing:** Several commenters argue that the article conflates functional programming with strong static typing. They contend that the primary benefits of reliability (immutability, pure functions) can be achieved in dynamically typed languages like JavaScript or Racket, and that the focus on types overlooks the broader principles of FP.
*   **Correctness vs. Fault Tolerance:** A significant point is that real-world critical systems (like payments and telecom) prioritize fault tolerance and reconciliation over perfect, bug-free code. However, the author and others counter that making illegal states unrepresentable is still crucial for ensuring that fault-tolerance mechanisms (like retries) don't introduce new failure modes.
*   **Practicality and Complexity:** Critics argue that handling all possible errors in a purely functional way can become impractical in large, complex systems. There's also a concern that FP can lead to "write-only" or incomprehensible code, which is detrimental to reliability.
*   **Evidence and Absolutes:** Commenters challenge the lack of hard evidence for the article's strong claims, calling them "vibes and feels." The discussion is wary of absolute statements and "silver bullet" solutions, with many pointing out that type systems are just one tool among many (like fuzz testing and fault injection) needed for reliability.
*   **Specific Technical Debates:** A minor thread debated the merits of "tagged" unions (ADTs in Haskell) versus "untagged" unions (in TypeScript/Scala 3), with users defending the utility and safety of tagged unions.
