> **Article:** The article "Replacing JavaScript with Just HTML" argues for using modern, native HTML elements to achieve common interactive features, reducing the need for JavaScript. It provides practical examples of replacing JS-heavy components with simpler HTML alternatives, such as:
*   **Accordions:** Using `<details>` and `<summary>` instead of JS-driven toggles.
*   **Tooltips:** Using the `title` attribute or the new `popover` attribute.
*   **Form Validation:** Using built-in attributes like `required`, `pattern`, and `type="email"`.
*   **Autocomplete:** Using `<datalist>` for simple input suggestions.
*   **Dark Mode:** Using CSS media queries (`prefers-color-scheme`) or the `:has()` selector with a checkbox.

The core message is to leverage the platform's capabilities first for better performance, accessibility, and maintainability before adding the complexity of a JavaScript framework.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is largely positive but pragmatic, acknowledging the benefits of HTML-first development while highlighting its real-world limitations.

Key points of the discussion include:
*   **The "It Depends" Reality:** Many commenters argue that while native HTML is great for simple sites, it often falls short for complex, commercial applications where design consistency and custom behavior are required. The styling limitations of elements like `<datalist>` and `<select>` are frequently cited as a major blocker.
*   **Specific Feature Debates:**
    *   **`<details>` / `<summary>`:** Praised for being accessible by default and highly useful, though criticized for lacking native animation support.
    *   **`<datalist>`:** Seen as too limited for real-world autocomplete (e.g., no custom filtering or complex matching), forcing developers back to JS solutions.
    *   **Browser Inconsistency:** A major pain point is that native UI elements (like date pickers or dropdowns) look and behave differently across browsers, making them difficult to style consistently for a branded experience.
*   **The Broader Trend:** The conversation expands into a larger trend of "returning to the server." Commenters mention technologies like HTMX, Phoenix LiveView, and Rails Hotwire as modern solutions that embrace this philosophy—keeping logic on the server and using HTML/CSS for rendering, thus avoiding the heavy JS front-end stack.
*   **Pragmatism over Dogma:** The consensus is not to abandon JavaScript entirely, but to start with the simplest solution (HTML/CSS) and only add JavaScript when necessary for more advanced functionality or a specific user experience.

The traditional view of heredity (Mendelian genetics) is being expanded by evidence that sperm carries more than just DNA; it also contains various RNA molecules that can influence gene expression in the offspring.

The article cites several key studies on mice:
*   **Nicotine:** Fathers exposed to nicotine produced male offspring whose livers were more efficient at metabolizing not only nicotine but also other toxins like cocaine.
*   **Stress:** Fathers subjected to chronic stress produced offspring with altered stress responses.
*   **Obesity:** A father's high-fat diet can influence his son's metabolism and weight.

This form of inheritance is not a change to the DNA sequence itself, but rather a "package" of molecular instructions that can alter how the child's genes are expressed. While the mechanisms are still being actively researched, it suggests that a father's health and choices at the time of conception can have a tangible, lifelong impact on his children's physiology.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion was largely fascinated and speculative, with several key themes emerging:

*   **Lamarckian Inheritance:** Many commenters immediately drew parallels to Lamarckism (the inheritance of acquired characteristics), noting that this research provides a biological mechanism for a concept once considered debunked. The discussion touched on how this complicates the purely Mendelian view of genetics and even has implications for historical psychoanalytic theories that were dismissed for their Lamarckian leanings.

*   **Practical and Personal Implications:** Users explored the real-world consequences. This included questions about how IVF could evolve to screen for sperm RNA quality, and humorous or cautionary speculation about men trying to "optimize" their health before conception (e.g., microdosing nicotine to give children an edge in metabolizing toxins).

*   **Limits of Inheritance:** A nuanced point was raised about the limits of this transmission. While general physiological states like stress, fitness, or metabolic profiles can plausibly be passed down, it's unlikely that specific, complex subjective experiences (like a fear of spiders) could be inherited unless tied to a distinct molecular signature.

*   **Sci-Fi and Speculation:** The conversation sparked creative ideas, such as the concept of "freezing your best self" (banking sperm at peak health) and a sci-fi novella about a society that practices this. There were also references to real-world evidence like the transgenerational effects of trauma (e.g., the Dutch Hunger Winter).

*   **Defense in Depth:** Suggestions were made for overlapping certificates or using multiple algorithms (RSA and ECC) to provide redundancy, though some argued this is overkill compared to simply shortening renewal intervals.
*   **Critique of the Article:** Some commenters felt the article was complaining about "user error" (broken alerting, lack of monitoring) rather than a flaw in the technology itself.
