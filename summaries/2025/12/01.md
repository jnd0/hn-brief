## [Why xor eax, eax?](https://xania.org/202512/01-xor-eax-eax)
**Score:** 576 | **Comments:** 215 | **ID:** 46106556

> **Article:** The linked article, "Why xor eax, eax?", explains the idiomatic assembly instruction used to set a register to zero on x86 architecture. The core reasons are performance and code size. The `xor eax, eax` instruction is shorter (2 bytes) than the equivalent `mov eax, 0` (5 bytes), which was critical for memory-constrained systems. Furthermore, modern CPUs recognize this pattern as a "zeroing idiom," allowing the instruction to be handled efficiently by the front-end and potentially eliminated via register renaming, making it the fastest way to zero a register.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion is a nostalgic and technical deep-dive into x86 assembly, with a consensus that `xor eax, eax` is the canonical way to zero a register due to its historical and modern performance benefits.

Key points of agreement and insight include:
*   **Historical Context:** Commenters confirm the practice dates back to the 8086 era, where saving a single byte per instruction was a significant optimization for memory-limited machines.
*   **Modern CPU Behavior:** It's noted that modern CPUs have dedicated hardware optimizations for this and other "zeroing idioms," making it consistently fast.
*   **x86 vs. RISC Architectures:** A side-discussion compares x86's lack of a dedicated zero register (like RISC-V's `x0`) to its use of a short, canonical instruction. The consensus is that both are valid design choices with their own trade-offs (e.g., RISC-V uses its zero register for other operations like `NOP`).
*   **Technical Nuances:** The conversation clarifies that `xor eax, eax` clears the carry flag, contrary to a mistaken claim about `sub eax, eax`. It also highlights a key 64-bit feature where writing to a 32-bit register (like `eax`) automatically zeroes the upper 32 bits of the 64-bit register, a detail unfamiliar to those only experienced with 32-bit assembly.

Disagreements were minor and mostly irrelevant, such as a brief, unsubstantiated complaint about browser crashes and a downvoted attempt to frame the x86 instruction as a "remnant" of a failed RISC attempt. Overall, the thread serves as a practical lesson in assembly optimization and computer architecture history.
