## [Programmers and software developers lost the plot on naming their tools](https://larr.net/p/namings.html)
**Score:** 422 | **Comments:** 534 | **ID:** 46234806

> **Article:** The article argues that the software industry has abandoned sensible, professional naming conventions in favor of "cutesy" or arbitrary names. The author posits that naming a tool after a mythological creature, random noun, or fictional character is unprofessional "career suicide" in any other technical field (e.g., civil engineering, pharmaceuticals). They advocate for descriptive, purpose-driven names (like early languages: FORTRAN, COBOL) that immediately convey a tool's function, arguing this aids discoverability and reduces cognitive load for new developers. The piece frames the trend of "creative" naming as a vanity project that prioritizes the developer's amusement over the user's utility.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely rejects the article's premise, viewing the author's stance as naive to the realities of software evolution and professional practice. The consensus is that descriptive names are brittle and often become misleading as a tool's scope expands—a phenomenon frequently cited as "scope creep." The community argues that arbitrary or "mythological" names are a pragmatic solution to this, allowing a tool to evolve without requiring expensive renaming efforts or confusing users with an outdated name.

Key points of disagreement and insight include:
*   **The "Scope Creep" Defense:** The most compelling counter-argument is that a descriptive name like "Login-Config-Service" becomes a lie the moment the tool is repurposed for broader authentication. Arbitrary names avoid this trap.
*   **False Equivalency with Other Fields:** Commenters dismantled the comparison to other industries, pointing out that professionals in those fields use systematic model numbers or chemical compounds (e.g., doctors prescribe *sildenafil*, not *Viagra*), while "creative" names are reserved for marketing.
*   **Discoverability vs. Maintenance:** A minority agreed that descriptive names are better for discoverability, but the prevailing view is that the one-time cost of renaming a descriptive tool is preferable to the perpetual confusion of an opaque name.
*   **The "Microsoft" Exception:** Several users noted that the worst naming conventions often come from corporate branding (e.g., Microsoft's cloud products), making the "random noun" approach seem preferable by comparison.

Ultimately, the discussion concludes that naming is a trade-off: descriptive names are ideal for static, well-defined tools, but arbitrary names are a safer bet for the fluid, iterative nature of modern software development.
