## [Python 3.15’s interpreter for Windows x86-64 should hopefully be 15% faster](https://fidget-spinner.github.io/posts/no-longer-sorry.html)
**Score:** 396 | **Comments:** 146 | **ID:** 46384167

> **Article:** The linked article, "No Longer Sorry," details an experimental optimization to the CPython interpreter for Windows x86-64, aiming for a ~15% speedup in Python 3.15. The core technique is replacing the traditional `switch`-based or computed-goto dispatch loop with a **tail-calling interpreter**. The author argues this isn't primarily about register efficiency, but about "resetting compiler heuristics." The massive, single-function loop of the current interpreter (12,000+ lines) confuses MSVC's optimizer, preventing crucial optimizations like function inlining. By breaking the loop into smaller functions that tail-call each other, the compiler can better optimize the code, leading to significant performance gains, especially on MSVC.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is a mix of technical appreciation, historical skepticism, and pragmatic caution. The consensus is that the approach is clever and the performance gains on MSVC are significant, but it's not a silver bullet.

Key insights and disagreements include:

*   **The "Why" is Key:** Commenters debate the source of the speedup. The author and some others suggest it's less about register allocation and more about enabling the optimizer by breaking down a monolithic function that breaks compiler heuristics. This is seen as a more robust way to guide the compiler than hoping for the best with a complex loop.
*   **MSVC is the Weak Link:** There's a recurring concern about relying on specific, potentially undocumented or unstable MSVC optimizer behavior (`[[msvc::musttail]]`). The author acknowledges this risk, stating they will maintain multiple interpreter backends and can fall back if MSVC breaks. This pragmatic approach is generally approved.
*   **Historical Context & Skepticism:** Some users are wary of inflated performance claims, recalling a previous incident with Python 3.14 where an LLVM bug artificially inflated benchmarks. The author is praised for his transparency ("sharing early and making a fool of myself"), which helped catch that earlier bug.
*   **The Bigger Picture:** A few comments put this in context, noting that Python's core team has only recently prioritized performance (post-Guido), and that if raw speed were the ultimate goal, one would use PyPy or a JIT. This is seen as a valuable, but incremental, improvement to CPython.

In short, the discussion views this as a smart, pragmatic hack to solve a specific MSVC performance bottleneck, with the community appreciating the transparency but remaining aware of the potential fragility of relying on compiler-specific features.
