## [What an unprocessed photo looks like](https://maurycyz.com/misc/raw_photo/)
**Score:** 1130 | **Comments:** 207 | **ID:** 46415225

> **Article:** The article "What an unprocessed photo looks like" demystifies the digital photography pipeline by showing that no image is truly "unprocessed." It demonstrates that a RAW file from a camera sensor is not a finished image but a collection of linear light data that requires significant processing to become viewable. The author walks through several key steps: first, the sensor captures light through a Bayer filter, meaning each pixel only records intensity for one color (red, green, or blue). Second, because the sensor's response to light is linear, a direct display of this data would appear extremely dark, necessitating a gamma correction curve to make it look natural to the human eye. Finally, the article shows the crucial step of demosaicing, where the single-color pixel values are interpolated to create a full-color image. The piece concludes by showing the final, polished image after these and other adjustments, illustrating that even a "straight out of the camera" JPEG is the result of a complex series of processing choices.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely affirmed the article's core message, with many commenters expressing that it's a great explanation of a commonly misunderstood process. A central theme was the philosophical debate over what constitutes a "real" or "fake" photo. Several users argued that since all photos, even unedited RAWs, are interpretations of reality through hardware and software choices, the term "fake" is meaningless. Instead, they suggested the real distinction should be based on intent to deceive, not the act of editing. The discussion also delved deeper into the technical reasons for processing, such as how gamma correction isn't just a monitor limitation but also a way to efficiently use limited bit-depth and match the non-linear sensitivity of human vision and film. Commenters also explored the implications of this, from the importance of color science (like why Bayer filters prioritize green) to the growing concern over AI "hallucinating" details in photos, which some see as the next frontier in blurring the line between capture and creation.

A representative from the JDK team provided a detailed explanation, arguing that integrity is crucial for security, performance, and long-term platform stability, and that the change was necessary to prevent libraries from silently breaking platform invariants.
