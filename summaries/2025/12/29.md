## [What an unprocessed photo looks like](https://maurycyz.com/misc/raw_photo/)
**Score:** 370 | **Comments:** 96 | **ID:** 46415225

> **Article:** The article "What an unprocessed photo looks like" demystifies the digital photography pipeline by showing the stages of image creation, from raw sensor data to a final image. It explains that a camera sensor is essentially a grid of light-sensitive elements that only record brightness, not color. A Color Filter Array (most commonly a Bayer filter) is used to make each pixel sensitive to only Red, Green, or Blue light. This results in a "mosaic" image where each pixel has only one color value. This raw data is then linear and appears very dark because human vision is non-linear. To create a viewable image, the data must undergo several processing steps: Demosaicing (interpolating the missing two color values for each pixel), applying a gamma curve to correct for the non-linearity of human vision and displays, and applying white balance, sharpening, and color profiles. The article concludes that every JPEG from a camera is a heavily processed and interpreted version of the raw data, not a "true" representation of the scene.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion centers on the idea that all digital images are interpretations, not objective truths. Key points include:

*   **No "Real" Photos:** Commenters argue that the concept of an "unprocessed" or "original" photo is a myth. Every image, even straight from the camera, involves thousands of processing choices (demosaicing, gamma correction, color profiles, etc.). Therefore, manually editing a photo is simply taking control of this process, not "faking" it. "Fake" is better defined by the *intent to deceive* rather than the act of editing.
*   **Technical Nuances:** Users delve deeper into the technical reasons for processing. Gamma correction isn't just for monitor limitations; it's also a form of data compression that allocates more bit-depth to the shadows where human eyes are more sensitive. The 50% green in a Bayer filter is explained as a way to prioritize luminance (detail), which the human eye is most sensitive to, similar to chroma subsampling in video.
*   **AI and the Future of "Truth":** A concern is raised about AI "hallucinating" details in photos, which pushes the boundary of what is considered a photograph and raises issues for things like evidence in court.
*   **Practical Analogies:** The process is compared to video color grading, where footage is often shot "flat" (logarithmic) to preserve dynamic range for later creative interpretation.
*   **Minor Criticism:** One user felt the example image (a multi-colored, artificial light scene) was a poor choice because it made it hard to understand what the "ground truth" should have been, though another user countered that the article's point is to challenge the very idea of a ground truth.

> **Article:** The article "MongoBleed Explained Simply" describes a critical security vulnerability (CVE-2019-2389) in MongoDB. The bug is a "use-after-free" memory error in the database's internal memory allocator. When a client disconnects, the memory used for its session data is freed, but a pointer to that data remains in a list. If a new client connects immediately, the allocator may reuse that same memory for the new session, causing the new client to inherit sensitive data (like authentication credentials) from the previous session. This could allow an attacker to gain unauthorized access to the database.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion centered on three main themes:
