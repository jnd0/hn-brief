The solver then rigorously checks if the LLM's conclusion logically follows from its premises. It essentially tries to bridge the gap between probabilistic text generation and deterministic logical proof.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is a mix of interest in the methodology and significant skepticism regarding the underlying premise and practical execution. There is no clear consensus, but the debate centers on the reliability of LLMs and the feasibility of "autoformalization" (translating natural language to strict logic).

Key points of the discussion include:

*   **The "Autoformalization" Problem:** Several users, including those working in the field, identify the translation of natural language (like business policies or wiki pages) into formal logic as the primary bottleneck. While the concept is promising for high-stakes domains (finance, legal), the conversion process currently requires significant human oversight to ensure the formal model accurately reflects the source material.
*   **Skepticism of the "Reasoning" Label:** A recurring philosophical debate questions whether LLMs "reason" at all. One faction argues they merely mimic reasoning through statistical pattern matching, and that applying a logic checker post-hoc is just validating whether the random output happened to be logical, not ensuring the process was sound. The counter-argument is that defining "thinking" is notoriously difficult and that dismissing LLM capabilities on this basis is unscientific.
*   **Practical Flaws and Modern Solutions:** A pragmatic critique emerged regarding the implementation. One user pointed out that the project's pipeline naively parses raw text into JSON, ignoring that modern LLM APIs have built-in structured output capabilities that would solve this problem more reliably. This highlights a common gap between academic research and current production best practices.
*   **Performance and Guarantees:** The project's paper reportedly shows a high false-positive rate (51%). However, other users point to newer models and commercial products (like AWS Automated Reasoning Checks) that claim much higher verification accuracy (99%+), suggesting the concept is viable but highly dependent on the quality of the underlying LLM and a rigorous, human-verified formal model.
*   **The Opacity of LLMs:** A tangential but related point was raised about the "black box" nature of LLMs, with users noting that their high-dimensional, organically grown parameters make their internal decision-making processes fundamentally opaque and difficult to debug.

In essence, the community sees the use of theorem provers as a valid and interesting approach to constraining LLMs, but is highly critical of the current implementation's naivety, the project's performance metrics, and the philosophical overreach of claiming the LLM is "thinking."
