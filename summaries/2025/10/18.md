> **Article:** The article, "The IDEs we had 30 years ago and lost," is a piece of software nostalgia that laments the demise of the integrated development environments of the DOS era, specifically Borland's Turbo series (C++, Pascal). It argues that these tools, despite their technical limitations by today's standards, offered a uniquely fast, responsive, and cohesive "flow state" experience that is missing from modern development. The core thesis is that the tight integration of the editor, compiler, and debugger within a single, lightweight executable provided a level of performance and ergonomic simplicity that we have somehow abandoned in our pursuit of more complex, resource-heavy tooling.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion is a predictable mix of nostalgia, practical solutions, and classic programmer debates. The consensus is a shared, almost wistful appreciation for the perceived speed and elegance of the Borland IDEs, with many commenters reminiscing about the "flow state" they enabled on what were then considered underpowered machines.

Key points of the discussion include:
*   **Nostalgia vs. Modernity:** The primary theme is a longing for the simplicity and performance of the past. Commenters note that despite today's vastly more powerful hardware, modern IDEs feel slower and more bloated. This is countered by a pragmatic view that these old tools were "buggy" and lacked the features necessary for modern, complex software development.
*   **Practical Re-creations:** A significant portion of the conversation is dedicated to pointing out modern projects that resurrect the Borland TUI (Text-based User Interface) aesthetic. Projects like `tvision` (a library) and `turbo` (a modern IDE built with it) are highlighted as direct answers to the article's implied question.
*   **The "Bloat" Debate:** A classic tangent emerges about binary sizes in modern languages like Rust and Go. This is framed as a symptom of the same "storage is cheap" mentality that leads to heavy IDEs, though others correctly identify static linking as the primary, practical reason for the large binaries.
*   **Technical Context:** A more insightful comment explains *why* TUIs were so fast on old DOS machines: direct memory-mapped I/O to video hardware, bypassing the overhead of modern terminal emulators and graphics stacks. This provides a technical basis for the "it just felt faster" sentiment.
*   **The Inevitable Contrarian Take:** A dissenting voice argues that TUIs are a pointless exercise in "legacy bloat" and that graphical environments are objectively superior for complex tasks. This is met with the simple rebuttal that modern graphical tools are often demonstrably slower than the 30-year-old TUIs they replaced.

In essence, the community agrees that the *feeling* of the old IDEs was special, but the debate splits on whether that feeling is a result of genuine design elegance or just rose-tinted nostalgia for a simpler time.

## [SQL Anti-Patterns](https://datamethods.substack.com/p/sql-anti-patterns-you-should-avoid)
**Score:** 296 | **Comments:** 219 | **ID:** 45626985

> **Article:** The article is a listicle of common SQL "anti-patterns" aimed at intermediate developers. It covers a grab-bag of well-known pitfalls, including using `SELECT *` (which breaks on schema changes), overusing `DISTINCT` to mask bad joins, applying functions to columns in `WHERE` clauses (killing index usage), and using massive `CASE WHEN` statements for mapping codes. The proposed solutions are equally standard: use lookup tables, create functional indexes, or explicitly list columns. It's a boilerplate guide to avoiding the most common foot-guns in database interaction.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is a mix of agreement on the fundamentals and nitpicking of the article's specifics, with a healthy dose of cynicism about the term "anti-pattern."

**Consensus & Key Insights:**
*   **The "Anti-Pattern" Label is Debated:** A top-level thread argues that these aren't true anti-patterns (which imply a tempting but flawed solution) but simply "bad ideas to avoid." This sparked a meta-discussion on the overuse of the term.
*   **`SELECT *` is a Cardinal Sin:** Commenters strongly agree that `SELECT *` is bad practice, not just for breaking code when schemas evolve, but because it creates a non-local dependency where your application's behavior is tied to the database's structure.
*   **`DISTINCT` is a Red Flag:** Seeing `DISTINCT` is treated as a strong signal that the developer doesn't understand the data model or set theory, and is using a blunt instrument to fix a join problem.
*   **Functional Indexes are a Common Pitfall:** Several developers shared war stories about queries becoming slow because they used a function (like `UPPER()`) on a column, rendering a standard index useless. The solution is either a functional index or a case-insensitive collation.
*   **Complex Queries Need Better Structure:** The article's point about massive nested subqueries resonated. The discussion highlighted that this is a code organization problem, and tools like `dbt` or CTEs can help, though debugging complex pipelines remains a challenge.

**Disagreements & Nuance:**
*   **`CASE WHEN` vs. Lookup Tables:** While using a lookup table is the ideal solution, one commenter pointed out that real-world `CASE WHEN` statements are often more complex than simple key-value mappings, making them difficult to refactor into a simple table.
*   **`SELECT *` Apologism:** One dissenter argued that `SELECT *` shouldn't break your code and that providing more data than needed is only a problem if the system is poorly designed, echoing a "data-oriented" philosophy.

Overall, the discussion is that of experienced engineers: they agree on the core problems but debate the severity and the best way to articulate the solutions, all while acknowledging that these are mistakes everyone makes at some point.

The implementation relies on Analog Devices' "A2B" (Automotive Audio Bus) technology, specifically the AD2428 integrated circuit. The GitHub repository provides the necessary documentation and schematics for others to build compatible hardware, effectively democratizing a high-channel-count, low-cost connection standard.

Users also questioned latency and the wisdom of using an XLR connector, which is ubiquitous for analog audio and could lead to accidental damage from phantom power or misconnection. **2. The Behringer Business Strategy:**
The discussion on Behringer itself is a familiar debate. One side views them as a "Robin Hood" figure, democratizing music production by cloning expensive gear and selling it at a fraction of the cost, making music accessible to amateurs and home studios. There is a general acknowledgment that Behringer has evolved from producing "cheap unusable shit" to making solid, reliable gear that has forced the entire industry to re-evaluate its pricing.

**3. Open Standards vs. Proprietary Lock-in:**
Users appreciate the move to open-source the protocol, contrasting it with the typical industry practice of locking specs behind expensive licensing fees (e.g., Dante, MADI). However, the technical reality is that the protocol is built on top of a proprietary Analog Devices IC. While the *protocol* is open, the *silicon* is not, meaning anyone wanting to build a compatible device must still use Behringer's chosen chip. This is seen as a pragmatic compromise that lowers the barrier to entry without fully giving away the underlying hardware IP.

## [The Unix Executable as a Smalltalk Method [pdf]](https://programmingmadecomplicated.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/onward25-jakubovic.pdf)
**Score:** 148 | **Comments:** 21 | **ID:** 45623917

> **Article:** The linked PDF, "The Unix Executable as a Smalltalk Method," proposes a radical fusion of Unix and Smalltalk. The core idea is to treat the filesystem itself as an object database and individual executable files as methods. In this model, an "object" is a directory containing data files (state) and script files (methods). A "message send" is simply a shell command that invokes a method script, passing the object's directory path and arguments. The paper focuses on making this concept practical and efficient, addressing the notoriously slow performance of such a system by proposing optimizations like caching and avoiding process creation overhead for primitive operations. It's essentially an attempt to build a highly malleable, persistent, and transparent object-oriented environment on top of the familiar, ubiquitous foundation of a standard Unix filesystem.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is largely a mix of appreciation for the concept and a strong sense of déjà vu. There is no significant disagreement on the core idea, but the conversation is heavily colored by historical context and practical concerns.

**Consensus & Key Insights:**
*   **Historical Precedent:** The most prominent theme is that this idea is not new. Multiple commenters immediately identify it as a re-invention of "Woosh" (and its successor "shoo"), a project from the early 2000s that implemented the exact same concept. Others connect it to Stephen Kell's work ("Liberating the Smalltalk lurking in C and Unix") and even Xerox PARC's 1980s efforts to bring their integrated environments to Unix.
*   **The "True" OOP Vision:** Several commenters, invoking Alan Kay, praise the paper for correctly identifying "message passing" as the central tenet of object-oriented programming, rather than the more common class-based implementation.
*   **A Modern Reimagining:** While not novel in concept, the paper is seen as a valuable modern take. It's framed as an attempt to finally realize the "Unix as a Smalltalk environment" dream, potentially with the performance and tooling to make it viable today.
*   **Inspiration for OS Design:** The paper is a catalyst for discussion about more malleable, component-based operating systems, with one commenter detailing their own (stalled) ambitious OS project ("MallowOS") inspired by similar ideas.

**Disagreements & Criticisms:**
*   **Performance Skepticism:** The most pointed critique comes from a developer of "shoo" (kragen), who has firsthand experience with the performance pitfalls. He directly challenges the paper's claim that FUSE can make file-based objects as efficient as in-memory Smalltalk objects, arguing that kernel-level I/O dispatch is fundamentally an order of magnitude slower than a message send. He also notes that his own implementation was "painfully slow."
*   **Practicality:** There's a subtle undercurrent of skepticism about the real-world viability of such a system, with one commenter bluntly stating that while it's an interesting idea, it "still doesn't make the shell into a decent general-purpose programming language."

In essence, the HN community sees the paper less as a groundbreaking discovery and more as a well-reasoned, modern re-imagining of a powerful but historically niche and performance-challenged idea. The discussion is a rich, albeit slightly cynical, acknowledgment of the cyclical nature of programming language and OS design concepts.
