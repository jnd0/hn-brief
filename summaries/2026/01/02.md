## [Bluetooth Headphone Jacking: A Key to Your Phone [video]](https://media.ccc.de/v/39c3-bluetooth-headphone-jacking-a-key-to-your-phone)
**Score:** 442 | **Comments:** 161 | **ID:** 46453204

> **Article:** This article, a presentation from the 39th Chaos Communication Congress (39C3), details a set of critical vulnerabilities in Bluetooth chipsets from the manufacturer Airoha. The flaws (CVE-2025-20700, -20701, -20702) affect a wide range of popular, high-end wireless headphones from brands like Sony, Marshall, and Jabra.

The attack works by an attacker in physical proximity silently connecting to the headphones via Bluetooth. They can then dump the device's flash memory to extract the Bluetooth Link Key—a cryptographic secret that the headphones use to establish trust with a paired phone. Once the attacker has this key, they can impersonate the headphones and connect to the user's phone from a distance, gaining a privileged position as a trusted peripheral. This could potentially lead to complete device compromise and audio surveillance. The researchers also discuss the poor disclosure process with some manufacturers and release tools for users to check their devices and for other researchers to investigate further.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolves around three main themes: the severity of the exploit, the ongoing debate about the removal of the headphone jack, and general frustration with the state of Bluetooth technology.

Many commenters expressed alarm at the vulnerability, with some providing a clear, step-by-step breakdown of how an attacker could steal the link key and impersonate a user's headphones. There was a sense of vindication for those who have long been skeptical of Bluetooth's security, with one user noting that OpenBSD was widely criticized for its refusal to support Bluetooth. The discussion also highlighted the poor security practices of manufacturers, particularly Sony, who were singled out for being slow to respond to the researchers.

A significant portion of the conversation circled back to the removal of the 3.5mm headphone jack. Several users saw this exploit as another "gift that keeps on giving" from that trend, lamenting the loss of a simple, secure, and reliable connection. This led to a debate on the trade-offs: while some argued that the convenience of wireless audio is worth the risks and that USB-C DACs are a fine alternative, others felt that users were forced into a less secure ecosystem for the sake of aesthetics and "courage."

Finally, there was a broader sentiment that Bluetooth is a fundamentally "cursed" and messy technology. Users shared frustrations about the lack of basic diagnostic tools (like signal strength indicators) and the general unreliability. The exploit was seen by some as an inevitable outcome of a "race to the bottom" in a complex and poorly implemented standard.
