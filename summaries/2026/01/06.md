## [I/O is no longer the bottleneck? (2022)](https://stoppels.ch/2022/11/27/io-is-no-longer-the-bottleneck.html)
**Score:** 241 | **Comments:** 120 | **ID:** 46506994

> **Article:** The article "I/O is no longer the bottleneck?" (2022) argues that with the advent of high-speed NVMe SSDs (which can reach speeds of several GB/s), the traditional performance bottleneck of I/O has been significantly reduced for many workloads. The author demonstrates this by benchmarking a custom tool, showing that sequential read speeds from modern SSDs can saturate a single CPU core's ability to process data. The new bottleneck is often the CPU's own memory bandwidth and processing speed, not the storage device itself. This shift challenges long-held optimization assumptions and suggests that software design could evolve to treat persistent storage more like fast, volatile memory.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely validates the article's premise but adds significant nuance. A central theme is that the "I/O bottleneck" was never just about sequential reads; it was historically dominated by the high latency and low throughput of mechanical hard drives (seek times), which made random access particularly painful. While sequential speeds have improved dramatically, random I/O and write latency remain critical concerns, especially for databases and ensuring data persistence (fsync).

The conversation identifies a new primary bottleneck: the "memory wall." As one commenter notes, a single CPU core is often limited by its memory bandwidth to around 6 GB/s, which is now slower than the peak sequential read speed of top-tier NVMe drives. This means the CPU itself can become the bottleneck when feeding data from storage to its processing units. This has led to interest in zero-copy data formats and architectures that blur the line between memory and storage, though practical challenges like the need for explicit persistence (fsync) and OS-level caching trade-offs remain. Ultimately, many commenters agree that beyond hardware limits, poor software design—such as serial operations, inefficient data layouts, and ignoring latency—is a major source of perceived slowness.
