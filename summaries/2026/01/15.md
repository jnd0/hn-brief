## [I’m leaving Redis for SolidQueue](https://www.simplethread.com/redis-solidqueue/)
**Score:** 298 | **Comments:** 124 | **ID:** 46614037

> **Article:** The article "I'm leaving Redis for SolidQueue" argues for replacing the common Redis-based background job system (like Sidekiq) with SolidQueue, a new Rails-native library that uses PostgreSQL or SQLite as its backend. The author's primary motivation is operational simplicity: eliminating the need to manage, monitor, and scale a separate Redis instance. They contend that for the vast majority of applications, the performance of a database-backed queue is more than sufficient, and the convenience of using the existing database is a major win. The article suggests that the complexity of managing Redis is often premature optimization for projects that don't require its extreme throughput or low latency.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion presents a nuanced debate on the trade-offs between database-backed and Redis-based job queues. The central theme is a tension between simplicity and scalability.

Many commenters agree with the article's premise, arguing that for most applications, the operational overhead of running a separate Redis instance is unnecessary. They see using PostgreSQL (via SolidQueue, GoodJob, or Oban) as a significant simplification, especially for smaller teams. However, a key counterpoint is raised about database coupling: some argue that running the job queue on the *same* production database instance can create resource contention and turn the queue into a single point of failure for the entire application. The ideal setup, they suggest, might be a database-backed queue on a *separate* database instance, which offers the simplicity of a single technology stack without the shared-resource risk.

Scalability is the most contentious point. While some believe modern databases can handle the load, others share cautionary tales. One commenter described switching from a PostgreSQL-based worker (Graphile) to Redis+BullMQ after hitting performance bottlenecks at "only" ~5k jobs/minute. This sparked a debate on whether such a bottleneck is a database limitation or a misconfiguration. The performance of similar systems like Oban was also debated; while Oban's benchmark of 1 million jobs/minute was cited as proof of scalability, another user critically broke it down, arguing the benchmark relies on unrealistic batching and that its true transactional throughput is much lower, more in line with the "hundreds per second" range.

Finally, practical considerations were discussed. One user noted that SolidQueue's MySQL support is limited because its developers prioritize PostgreSQL-specific features, making it a poor fit for MySQL shops. Another pointed out a fundamental difference: Redis is in-memory and thus better suited for jobs with large payloads, as storing them in a database could be inefficient. The discussion concluded with a consensus that the choice depends on the specific use case: database queues are excellent for simplicity and for passing primary keys, while Redis remains the superior choice for high-throughput, low-latency, or large-payload scenarios.
