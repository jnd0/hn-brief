The discussion’s nostalgia for `aalib` and `libcaca` underscores a perennial truth in tech: today’s “novel” problem was often solved decades ago, just with less fanfare and more CPU cycles. The real kicker is the suggestion that brute-forcing every character’s bitmap would yield better results—a perfect example of how elegance often trades off against computational cost, a tension that never goes away.

That same pattern of reinvention and trade-offs echoes in the jQuery 4 release. The debate over its 27kB size versus Preact’s 4.7kB misses the point: jQuery’s value was never raw efficiency, but its comprehensive, cross-browser DOM API that worked out of the box. The nostalgia is telling—it’s not about the library itself, but what it represented: a time when web development felt accessible, before the complexity explosion. The fact that it still supports IE11 is a grimly pragmatic nod to the long tail of legacy systems, a reality many engineers would rather ignore.
