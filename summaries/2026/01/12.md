## [Sampling at negative temperature](https://cavendishlabs.org/blog/negative-temperature/)
**Score:** 136 | **Comments:** 43 | **ID:** 46579374

> **Article:** The article from Cavendish Labs explores the concept of sampling from Large Language Models (LLMs) using negative temperatures. The author explains that the standard softmax function used to convert model logits into probabilities can be extended to negative values. While positive temperatures > 1 increase randomness (diversity) and temperatures < 1 (but > 0) make outputs more deterministic (focused on high-probability tokens), negative temperatures invert the probability distribution. This forces the model to sample from the *least* likely tokens. The author demonstrates that as temperature approaches zero from the negative side, the output becomes deterministic but selects the "worst" possible tokens, often resulting in nonsensical or "anomalous" text that sits near the centroid of the model's embedding space.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion was largely enthusiastic, with users analyzing the physics analogy and proposing new applications for the technique.

*   **Clarifying the Physics:** Users debated the validity of the "temperature" analogy. While one user (dnautics) argued it was superficial compared to thermodynamics in molecular dynamics, others (pama, the__alchemist) noted that negative temperature is a valid physical concept in systems like spin lattices where entropy decreases as energy increases beyond a certain point.
*   **Practical Applications and Experiments:** Several users proposed using this for creative generation or problem-solving. Swyx suggested that high temperatures might "unlock additional paths" for solving tricky problems and proposed dynamic temperature adjustments. The author of "min_p" (Der_Einzige) praised the article, arguing that the field currently misunderstands temperature and that exploring ranges beyond the standard 0 to 2 is crucial.

*   **Observations on Output:** Users noted that the examples in the article contained random foreign words (like "Хронологија" and "entferne"), speculating that these might be low-probability tokens that the model associates with "meaningless" or background noise.
