## [Sampling at negative temperature](https://cavendishlabs.org/blog/negative-temperature/)
**Score:** 98 | **Comments:** 36 | **ID:** 46579374

> **Article:** The article from Cavendish Labs explores the concept of "negative temperature" in the context of Large Language Model (LLM) sampling. It explains that the standard softmax function used to convert model logits into probabilities includes a temperature parameter (T). While T is typically positive (0 to infinity), the author demonstrates mathematically and empirically that T can be negative.

The behavior of negative temperature is the inverse of positive temperature:
- **Positive T > 0:** As T increases, the probability distribution flattens (more random). As T approaches 0, the output becomes deterministic (picking the most likely token).
- **Negative T < 0:** As T decreases (becomes more negative), the distribution flattens. As T approaches 0 from the negative side, the output becomes deterministic but picks the *least* likely tokens.

The author samples text using negative temperatures and observes that the output is "anti-semantic"—it consists of high-entropy gibberish that often contains common stopwords or "anomalous" tokens that sit near the centroid of the model's embedding space, effectively representing the "least meaningful" tokens to the model.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion engaged with the article by connecting it to physics, questioning its practical utility, and exploring the underlying mechanics.

Several commenters bridged the gap between LLM sampling and thermodynamics. One user noted that in physics, negative temperature occurs in systems with bounded energy states (like spin lattices) where entropy decreases as energy increases beyond a certain point. However, others argued that this is merely a superficial analogy and that negative temperature in LLMs is just a mathematical trick on the sampling equation, unrelated to physical energy.

Regarding practical application, the conversation touched on the difficulty of evaluating sampling methods and the limitations of standard temperature ranges. The author of the "min_p" sampling method commented that the field misunderstands temperature, suggesting that exploring beyond standard ranges is valuable. A user proposed that high temperatures might help models "unlock" new reasoning paths for difficult problems, though the author of the article countered that negative temperature produces "worse than random" nonsense rather than useful variations.
