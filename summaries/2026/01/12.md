## [Sampling at negative temperature](https://cavendishlabs.org/blog/negative-temperature/)
**Score:** 182 | **Comments:** 54 | **ID:** 46579374

> **Article:** The article from Cavendish Labs explores the concept of "negative temperature" in the context of Large Language Model (LLM) sampling. It explains that standard sampling uses a temperature parameter (T) to adjust the softmax distribution of token logits. Positive T > 1 flattens the distribution (more random), while T < 1 sharpens it (more deterministic).

The author demonstrates that mathematically extending T to negative values inverts the probability distribution. As T approaches 0 from the negative side, the model deterministically outputs the *least likely* tokens. As T becomes a large negative number, the distribution flattens again, but still favors unlikely tokens. The author shows that sampling at negative temperatures produces "anomalous" text—often nonsensical or consisting of tokens that are semantically distant from the model's typical output, effectively probing the "inverse" of the model's learned manifold.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion focused on the theoretical underpinnings of negative temperature, its relationship to physics, and practical applications for LLMs.

Several users with physics backgrounds debated the analogy to thermodynamics. While one user noted the similarity to population inversion in lasers, another argued that negative temperature in LLMs is merely a mathematical trick on the sampling function and not equivalent to the physical state of a system having negative entropy. However, others countered that the concept is valid in statistical mechanics (e.g., spin systems) and serves as a useful phenomenological framework.

Regarding practical utility, the author of the "min_p" sampling method commented that the field misunderstands temperature constraints and that exploring these extremes is valuable engineering. Users discussed potential applications, such as using high (positive) temperatures to explore "alternative paths" for problem-solving or using negative temperature to generate synthetic data, though the author of the article warned that negative temperature sampling produces "worse than random" tokens and is likely not useful for training.

There was also a brief technical thread about the "anomalous" tokens selected by negative sampling, with users speculating that they might be artifacts located near the centroid of the embedding space, lacking strong semantic signal.
