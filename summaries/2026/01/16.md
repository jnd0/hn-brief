Several users argued that the current system, which sets the wholesale price based on the marginal cost of the most expensive generator needed (usually gas), means consumers pay the high gas price even when cheap wind is abundant. This leads to "windfall profits" for renewable generators and fails to pass savings to consumers.

This sparked a counter-argument that this "pay-as-clear" market is economically rational. *   The gas price included carbon taxes, making the underlying fuel cost much lower.
*   The wind price omitted significant system costs, such as grid upgrades and the need for backup power to manage intermittency.
*   The capacity factor assumptions for gas plants in official government reports were unrealistically low, inflating the calculated cost of gas power.

In response, defenders of the wind projects clarified that "backup" often means idling existing gas plants, which still saves significant operational costs. They also argued that including carbon costs in the gas price is a valid way to account for externalities, not an unfair penalty.

Finally, the discussion touched on the practical challenges of integrating intermittent renewables. One user suggested that until large-scale energy storage is viable, intermittent sources should face a price penalty to reflect their lower reliability. However, another user countered that demand-side management (i.e., encouraging consumers to shift usage to off-peak times) is a viable solution that has been successfully implemented elsewhere, and its absence in the UK is a local limitation, not a fundamental human one.

Practical applications and limitations were also debated. Users in the silicon design industry discussed replacing NFS, citing its well-known issues like poor locking and slow performance. However, a FUSE-based solution like JuiceFS was cautioned against for arbitrary workloads due to potential bottlenecks and the inherent limitations of the FUSE interface. A significant concern raised was that JuiceFS writes files to object storage in its own proprietary format; if the metadata store is lost, the data in S3 becomes effectively unrecoverable, which undermines a key benefit of using object storage as a durable backend.
