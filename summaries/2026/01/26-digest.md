The revelation that ICE is using Palantir's ELITE tool to cross-reference Medicaid data isn't a shocking new development; it's the logical, inevitable endpoint of a decade of infrastructure building. The Hacker News discussion captured this perfectly, noting that these systems are designed to be irreversible, consolidating power and finding ever-broader applications. This isn't a bug; it's the feature of a state that has decided data is the ultimate enforcement tool. The same pattern is playing out in Germany, where Deutsche Telekom's throttling isn't a technical failure but a business model—using its de facto monopoly to extract rents by degrading the internet for everyone who doesn't pay tribute, a stark lesson in how infrastructure can be weaponized against its users.

This creeping, systemic control extends to your devices. OnePlus's hardware anti-rollback mechanism, which permanently blows an eFuse to prevent downgrading firmware, is framed as a security measure. But the community skepticism is warranted: it's a kill switch for user autonomy, a way to lock you into their ecosystem and kill the custom ROM scene. The parallel to the FAA's new drone no-fly zones around mobile ICE operations is chilling. The policy's vagueness—moving bubbles of restricted airspace with no public notice—makes compliance impossible, turning every drone operator into a potential felon. It's a perfect example of a law designed not for safety, but for plausible deniability in enforcement, a tool to punish without accountability.
