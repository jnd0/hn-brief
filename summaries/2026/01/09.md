## [A closer look at a BGP anomaly in Venezuela](https://blog.cloudflare.com/bgp-route-leak-venezuela/)
**Score:** 382 | **Comments:** 207 | **ID:** 46538001

> **Article:** The Cloudflare article analyzes a major BGP (Border Gateway Protocol) routing anomaly that occurred in Venezuela on March 19, 2024. During a nationwide power outage, the Venezuelan ISP CANTV (AS8048) accidentally leaked approximately 30,000 IP prefixes to its upstream providers. These routes were then propagated globally, causing international traffic destined for Venezuela to be misrouted to various points of presence (POPs) around the world, including Cloudflare's network.

Cloudflare's analysis concludes that this was almost certainly an accident, not a malicious interception attempt. The key evidence is "AS path prepending," where CANTV added its own AS number multiple times to the route path. This is a common traffic engineering technique to make a route appear less attractive to discourage traffic. A malicious actor trying to intercept traffic (a "man-in-the-middle" attack) would want to make their route appear as short and fast as possible to attract traffic, not deliberately make it look worse. The incident highlights the inherent fragility and trust-based nature of the BGP system, where a single misconfiguration can have global consequences.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolved around three main themes: the technical analysis of the incident, the broader implications of internet infrastructure control, and the nature of BGP security.

Many commenters appreciated the technical depth of Cloudflare's analysis, with several highlighting the AS path prepending as strong evidence against a malicious state-sponsored attack. One user noted that while a "fat finger" misconfiguration is the most likely cause, the incident underscores the systemic problems with BGP, which is a fundamentally trust-based system where even sophisticated operators can make critical errors.

A significant portion of the discussion focused on the power of US-based tech companies like Cloudflare. While some praised the depth of analysis this position enables, others expressed concern about the concentration of internet infrastructure and monitoring in the hands of a few US corporations. This led to a debate about trust, with some users stating they would not believe a US company's analysis if it implicated the US government, and others pointing out that this is a long-standing and global phenomenon of companies being intertwined with their home governments.

Finally, there was a more practical sub-thread for those wanting to learn more, with users advising that understanding this specific topic requires deep networking knowledge far beyond basic socket programming. Others shared their own methodologies for diagnosing routing anomalies, focusing on analyzing path changes, blast radius, and who benefits from the new routes.

**Score:** 150 | **Comments:** 84 | **ID:** 46537983

> **Article:** An article from EMJ Reviews reports on a study where an AI system, analyzing MRI scans, missed nearly one-third (29%) of breast cancers. The study was a retrospective case series, meaning it only included images from patients who were already confirmed to have breast cancer. This design measures the AI's sensitivity (its ability to detect cancer when it is present) but provides no information on its specificity (its ability to correctly identify when cancer is absent), as there were no healthy control subjects. The study also did not compare the AI's performance directly against human radiologists.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion heavily critiques the study's methodology and the article's framing, rather than accepting the headline at face value. The central point is that the study's design is fundamentally flawed for assessing real-world clinical utility. Because it only used images from patients known to have cancer, it can only measure the AI's false negative rate, while the false positive rate—which is critical for a screening tool—is completely unknown. Commenters note that a high false positive rate could lead to unnecessary anxiety and invasive procedures for a large population.

Several key methodological weaknesses were highlighted:
*   **No Control Group:** Without healthy scans, the study cannot determine how often the AI incorrectly flags healthy tissue.
*   **Retrospective Bias:** The study was a "case series," not a comparative trial. It was not designed to test if the AI performs better, worse, or differently than a human radiologist.
*   **Outdated Technology:** The AI model used was from 2021, which is considered ancient in the fast-moving AI field, and may not represent current state-of-the-art performance.
*   **Potential for Bias:** The human follow-up analysis, where radiologists reviewed scans the AI missed, was not blinded and may have been subject to anchoring bias, as they knew they were looking for cancers that were already confirmed to exist.

Beyond the study's flaws, commenters debated the semantics of the headline's use of the monolithic term "AI" and discussed potential use cases. The consensus was that AI should be used as an assistive tool for doctors, not as a fully automated replacement for diagnosis.
