## [Why does a least squares fit appear to have a bias when applied to simple data?](https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/674129/why-does-a-linear-least-squares-fit-appear-to-have-a-bias-when-applied-to-simple)
**Score:** 255 | **Comments:** 69 | **ID:** 46491821

> **Article:** The article (a Cross-Validated question) asks why a standard linear least squares regression line appears visually biased when applied to simple data. The core issue is that ordinary least squares (OLS) is asymmetric: it minimizes the sum of *vertical* squared errors (assuming only the dependent variable, Y, has noise). When you visually assess a "best fit" line, you intuitively consider the shortest perpendicular distance from the data points to the line, which assumes both X and Y have noise. This discrepancy between the vertical-only error minimization and the visual/perpendicular expectation creates the perceived bias.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion centers on explaining the source of this perceived bias and proposing solutions. The key points are:

*   **Asymmetry of OLS:** The fundamental cause is that OLS models noise only in the Y variable. This is contrasted with techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Total Least Squares (TLS), which model noise in both X and Y by minimizing perpendicular distances to the line. A common demonstration of this asymmetry is that regressing Y on X yields a different line than regressing X on Y.

*   **Alternative Regression Methods:** Several commenters suggest alternatives for when both variables have error. Deming Regression is frequently mentioned as a method that can handle errors in both X and Y, provided the ratio of their variances is known. If the variances are unknown, TLS or Reduced Major Axis (RMA) regression are proposed as coordinate-independent methods.

*   **Practical Context:** Users debated the real-world applicability. Some argued that in many scenarios (e.g., time series from a stable clock), the error in the X variable is negligible compared to the Y variable, making OLS perfectly appropriate. Others noted that if you know the relative noise levels, you can adjust your regression model accordingly.

*   **Interpretation vs. Definition:** A few comments clarify that while the line may look biased visually, it is statistically *unbiased* for predicting Y given a specific X, as per the technical definition. The perceived bias is a visual artifact of the model's assumptions, not a statistical error in its predictions.

## [Understanding the bin, sbin, usr/bin, usr/sbin split (2010)](https://lists.busybox.net/pipermail/busybox/2010-December/074114.html)
**Score:** 177 | **Comments:** 138 | **ID:** 46487921

> **Article:** The article, a 2010 mailing list post, explains the historical reasons for the split between directories like `/bin`, `/sbin`, `/usr/bin`, and `/usr/sbin`. The separation originated from the era of expensive, small-capacity storage. It was a practical necessity: `/bin` and `/sbin` contained essential tools needed to boot the system and perform repairs, which were kept on a small, primary disk. In contrast, `/usr/bin` and `/usr/sbin` held the rest of the operating system's applications, which could be located on a separate, larger (and potentially network-mounted) disk. The 's' in `sbin` denotes "system" binaries, typically reserved for administrative use and not needed by regular users.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolves around the tension between historical justification and modern convenience. A central theme is that many of these directory structures persist simply due to historical inertia and standards, rather than current technical necessity. While some commenters defend the original logic—such as separating critical boot files from the main OS for stability or partitioning to prevent user data from crashing the system—others argue that this "legacy cruft" is now overly complex.

This debate is exemplified by the mention of alternative systems like GoboLinux or the use of modern package managers like Homebrew, which create their own directories (e.g., `/opt/homebrew`) to work around the established hierarchy. The conversation also touches upon how modern immutable operating systems and package managers (NixOS, Guix, MicroOS) are, in a way, returning to the historical model of a read-only base system with separate writable locations, bringing the discussion full circle. Ultimately, the thread concludes that while the original reasons were practical, the system's evolution has been a messy amalgamation of decisions, with standards bodies often documenting complexity without simplifying it.
