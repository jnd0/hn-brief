## [Why does a least squares fit appear to have a bias when applied to simple data?](https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/674129/why-does-a-linear-least-squares-fit-appear-to-have-a-bias-when-applied-to-simple)
**Score:** 223 | **Comments:** 54 | **ID:** 46491821

> **Article:** The article (a Cross-Validated question) addresses a common visual confusion: when performing a standard linear least squares regression, the fitted line often appears to be "biased" or skewed. It does not seem to bisect the data cloud as one might intuitively expect, especially when looking at a scatter plot. The question asks why this phenomenon occurs and if it is a genuine error in the fitting process.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion clarifies that this is not a bias or error, but a natural consequence of the assumptions underlying different fitting methods. The core explanation is that Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) minimizes the sum of squared *vertical* distances (errors in Y), implicitly assuming that the X-axis values are precise and only the Y-axis values contain noise. This is often called "Regression Towards the Mean."

In contrast, a human eye trying to fit a line by sight is performing a "Total Least Squares" (TLS) or "Orthogonal Regression," which minimizes the shortest perpendicular distances to the line, assuming both X and Y have noise. This discrepancy between minimizing vertical error (OLS) and minimizing perpendicular error (visual/TLS) is what creates the apparent skew.

Key discussion points include:
*   **Different Loss Functions:** OLS minimizes vertical variance, while Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or TLS minimizes the total orthogonal variance. These are different mathematical goals and produce different lines.
*   **When is OLS Appropriate?** The consensus is that OLS is correct when X is truly independent and controlled (e.g., a fixed experimental setting), and only Y is measured with error. If both variables are noisy measurements (e.g., comparing two imperfect sensors), OLS is conceptually flawed.
*   **Solutions for Noisy X:** If both variables have noise, the community recommends alternatives like **Deming Regression** (which allows specifying the ratio of variances) or **Total Least Squares**.
*   **Visualizing the Fit:** To verify an OLS model, one should check the residuals (difference between actual and predicted Y) to ensure they are symmetrically distributed around zero, rather than judging the line's angle visually.
