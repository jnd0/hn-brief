## [Why does a least squares fit appear to have a bias when applied to simple data?](https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/674129/why-does-a-linear-least-squares-fit-appear-to-have-a-bias-when-applied-to-simple)
**Score:** 291 | **Comments:** 73 | **ID:** 46491821

> **Article:** The article (a Cross-Validated question) asks why a standard linear least squares regression line often appears visually biased when plotted against data. The user observes that the line of best fit for predicting Y from X is different from the line for predicting X from Y, and neither seems to perfectly bisect the data cloud. The core question is why the standard method doesn't produce a line that looks like the "center" of the data.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion clarifies that this "bias" is not a mathematical error but a result of the fundamental assumptions of Ordinary Least Squares (OLS). The consensus is that OLS minimizes the sum of *vertical* squared errors (residuals), implicitly assuming that the X-axis variable is measured perfectly without error and all the noise resides in the Y-axis variable. This asymmetry is why the regression line is unique to the choice of dependent and independent variables.

Several alternative methods and concepts are introduced to address this:
*   **Total Least Squares (TLS):** This is the technique that corresponds to the visual intuition of finding the "best fit" line. It minimizes the *perpendicular* (orthogonal) distance from the data points to the line, effectively treating noise as present in both X and Y.
*   **Deming Regression:** A specific type of TLS that allows for weighting the noise in X and Y differently based on prior knowledge of their relative variances.
*   **Principal Component Analysis (PCA):** The first eigenvector of a PCA is equivalent to the line found by Total Least Squares.

Participants also discuss practical implications. Many note that in real-world scenarios (e.g., sensor data with a stable clock), the noise in Y is often orders of magnitude larger than the noise in X, making the standard OLS assumption of zero error in X a reasonable and useful approximation. The "bias" is only a problem if one's visual expectation is for a coordinate-independent fit, when in fact the statistical model is conditional on X.
