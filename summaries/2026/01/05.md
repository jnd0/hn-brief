**Score:** 178 | **Comments:** 166 | **ID:** 46486780

> **Article:** The 2017 article by Hillel Wayne argues against the dogmatic "comments should only explain 'why' (the business reason)" rule. He posits that this is often impractical because code frequently does things for purely technical, not business, reasons. He gives examples like using a specific algorithm for performance, or handling a quirky API response. In these cases, a comment explaining *what* the code is doing (e.g., "Using a bubble sort because the list is always tiny and it avoids allocating memory") is more valuable than a generic "why" that doesn't explain the technical choice. The article concludes that the goal of comments is to aid understanding, and both 'what' and 'why' comments are valid tools to achieve this.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion reveals a strong consensus against the rigid, prescriptive styles of "Uncle Bob" (Robert C. Martin), with many developers sharing anecdotes of how excessive method extraction and a dogmatic anti-'what' comment stance led to unreadable, over-abstracted code. The central debate then shifts to the nature of comments themselves. While the ideal of keeping comments in sync with code is raised, many argue that for complex business logic or domain-specific rules, comments are indispensable. A key insight is that the 'what' vs. 'why' distinction often breaks down in practice; explaining a business rule (the 'what') is often inseparable from explaining the business context (the 'why'). Ultimately, the prevailing sentiment is pragmatic: comments should be used to explain intent and make the code understandable, regardless of a rigid 'what' or 'why' classification.

## [Understanding the bin, sbin, usr/bin, usr/sbin split (2010)](https://lists.busybox.net/pipermail/busybox/2010-December/074114.html)
**Score:** 168 | **Comments:** 125 | **ID:** 46487921

> **Article:** The linked article is a 2010 email from the BusyBox mailing list that explains the historical reasons for the split between `/bin`, `/sbin`, `/usr/bin`, and `/usr/sbin` directories in Unix and Linux systems. The core reason was hardware limitations. In early Unix, `/bin` and `/sbin` contained essential commands needed to mount the rest of the system, which was often stored on a separate, larger disk or partition mounted at `/usr`. Over time, as hardware became cheaper and more capable, this distinction blurred, but the structure was maintained for backward compatibility and adherence to standards like the Filesystem Hierarchy Standard (FHS).
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolves around the themes of historical legacy, the tension between tradition and modernization, and the nature of standards in technology. Many commenters express frustration with the complexity, humorously referencing the XKCD "Standards" comic and attributing the persistence of the structure to inertia ("because it's always been that way"). However, others provide historical context, explaining that the split was a practical necessity due to expensive and small disks.

The conversation then shifts to modern solutions and alternatives. Users point out that some distributions like Arch Linux have already made moves to consolidate binaries into `/usr/bin`, while others like NixOS and Guix represent a more radical departure by creating entirely new, non-standardized filesystem layouts. The discussion also touches on the purpose of other directories like `/opt` (for self-contained, third-party applications) and `/usr/local` (for locally compiled software), and how these fit into the evolving ecosystem. A key takeaway is that while the original reasons are historical, the structure is now maintained by standards bodies and the need for backward compatibility across a vast ecosystem of software.
