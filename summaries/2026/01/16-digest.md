The Hacker News discussion reflects a grim consensus: the technology itself is often a standard enterprise dashboard, but its application in the hands of the state, supercharged by Silicon Valley's relentless drive for contracts, creates a machine of breathtaking efficiency for human suffering. The debate over whether the fault lies with the engineers or the executives feels increasingly academic when the result is the same—a system that treats communities as targets and data points, with historical parallels that make many deeply uncomfortable. It's a reminder that the most impactful code written today isn't in a startup's repo, but in the contracts that bind big tech to government power.

This theme of institutional decay and misplaced priorities echoes elsewhere.
