## [Bluetooth Headphone Jacking: A Key to Your Phone [video]](https://media.ccc.de/v/39c3-bluetooth-headphone-jacking-a-key-to-your-phone)
**Score:** 390 | **Comments:** 123 | **ID:** 46453204

> **Article:** The linked content is a video presentation from the 39th Chaos Communication Congress (39C3) detailing severe security vulnerabilities in Bluetooth headphones and earbuds that use system-on-chip (SoC) solutions from the manufacturer Airoha. The researchers demonstrate a three-step attack:
1.  **Connect:** An attacker in physical proximity can silently connect to the target headphones via unauthenticated Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) or Classic Bluetooth channels.
2.  **Exfiltrate:** The attacker dumps the device's flash memory, which contains a "connection table."
3.  **Impersonate:** This table holds the Bluetooth Link Key for every device the headphones have ever paired with. Using this key, the attacker can spoof the headphones' identity and connect to a victim's smartphone, gaining a trusted, privileged position to potentially compromise the phone or exfiltrate data. The presentation also discusses the poor disclosure process with manufacturers and releases tooling for users and researchers.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolves around the severity of the vulnerabilities, the inherent insecurity of the Bluetooth ecosystem, and the consequences of removing the headphone jack.

Many commenters expressed alarm at the attack's implications, particularly the ability to steal the Bluetooth Link Key, which allows an attacker to compromise a phone even after the initial interaction with the headphones has ended. This was seen as a significant escalation beyond simple audio eavesdropping. The incident was used to validate long-standing criticisms of Bluetooth's complexity and security, with some users noting that OpenBSD was widely criticized for its refusal to implement Bluetooth support, a decision now seen as prescient. The general sentiment was that the "race to the bottom" in consumer electronics has created a "messy standard" with major security gaps, even in premium devices.

A major tangent of the discussion was the removal of the 3.5mm headphone jack, which many linked directly to the increased reliance on vulnerable Bluetooth technology. This topic was dominated by the initial decision by Apple, which was famously justified with the term "courage." Commenters noted the irony that while Apple's reasoning was widely mocked, the industry followed suit, leaving consumers with few high-end wired options. While some argued that the convenience of wireless is worth the trade-offs and that USB-C DACs are a viable alternative, the consensus in this thread was that the removal of the jack was a negative development that has now been compounded by serious security flaws.

## [Resistance training load does not determine hypertrophy](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP289684)
**Score:** 220 | **Comments:** 307 | **ID:** 46448998

> **Article:** The linked article, published in *The Journal of Physiology*, presents the findings of a 10-week study investigating the effects of different resistance training loads on muscle hypertrophy (growth). The study involved 20 healthy, recreationally active young men who were divided into two groups. One group performed low-load, high-repetition training (30-40% of one-rep max) to failure, while the other performed high-load, low-repetition training (80-85% of one-rep max) to failure. The study's primary finding was that both groups experienced nearly identical increases in muscle size. This suggests that when training is taken to the point of muscular failure, the specific weight used is not a critical determinant for achieving hypertrophy.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion centered on the practical implications of the study's findings, the quality of the research itself, and common misconceptions about strength training.

Many commenters interpreted the core takeaway as "effort is what matters, not the weight," summarizing it as "no pain, no gain." However, this idea was immediately challenged by others who argued that pain is not the goal and that pushing to true failure can be counterproductive or even dangerous, especially for beginners. A key point of agreement was the distinction between muscle growth (hypertrophy) and strength. While the study found no difference in size between the groups, commenters noted that the high-load group would almost certainly have seen greater strength gains, a point the study acknowledged but did not focus on.

A significant portion of the discussion was dedicated to critiquing the study's methodology. Critics pointed out that the 10-week duration is too short to draw meaningful long-term conclusions, especially since the participants were "recreationally active but untrained" individuals who would experience "newbie gains" from almost any new stimulus. The small sample size (20 participants) was also cited as a major weakness, leading some to dismiss the study as "low-quality" or "junk science." The general sentiment was that exercise science is often hampered by these limitations, making it difficult to produce robust, widely applicable findings.

Finally, the conversation touched on practical advice and safety. Several users, including one with personal experience, warned beginners to be careful with the concept of "failure," as improper form or overexertion can lead to serious injury. The consensus was that while the study's conclusion might be technically correct, it's not a green light for everyone to lift to failure; for most people, training close to failure with a focus on safety and consistency is a more sustainable and effective approach.
