The study involved 20 healthy, recreationally active young men. The core finding was that when both groups trained to the point of muscular failure, the resulting muscle growth was essentially the same. This supports the conclusion that training volume and proximity to failure are the primary drivers of hypertrophy, not the specific weight used.

> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely affirms the study's central finding, with a common sentiment being that this is well-understood in fitness circles but not widely known by the general public. The key takeaway for many commenters is that for muscle growth, the crucial factor is taking sets to or near muscular failure, regardless of whether that is achieved with high weight/low reps or low weight/high reps.

However, the conversation quickly expands beyond this single point. Several important nuances and criticisms are raised:

*   **Strength vs. Hypertrophy:** A critical distinction is made between muscle size (hypertrophy) and strength. This highlights that training goals should dictate methodology.

*   **Study Limitations:** Multiple users questioned the study's validity due to its design. Criticisms focused on the short 10-week duration (arguing that "newbie gains" could skew results and longer-term effects are unknown), the small sample size (20 participants), and the use of untrained subjects, whose rapid initial progress may not be representative of trained individuals.
