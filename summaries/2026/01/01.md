## [Resistance training load does not determine hypertrophy](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP289684)
**Score:** 190 | **Comments:** 227 | **ID:** 46448998

> **Article:** The linked scientific article investigates whether the load (weight) used in resistance training affects muscle hypertrophy (growth). The study had 20 healthy, recreationally active young men train for 10 weeks. One group used high loads (heavy weight for 8-12 reps) and the other used low loads (light weight for 25-35 reps). The key condition for both groups was that they trained to the point of muscular failure. The study's conclusion is that when training to failure, the resistance load does not significantly determine the degree of muscle hypertrophy. Both groups saw similar increases in muscle size.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion centered on the implications, limitations, and common misconceptions surrounding the study's findings.

A primary theme was the practical takeaway: for muscle size (hypertrophy), training to muscular failure is the critical factor, not the amount of weight lifted. Many commenters noted this is well-established in exercise science but not widely known by the general public, who often believe heavier is always better for growth. However, several pointed out that heavy weights are still superior for developing absolute strength (one-rep max) and athletic performance, which were not the focus of this study.

The conversation also heavily critiqued the study's methodology. The most common criticism was its short 10-week duration and small sample size (20 participants). Commenters argued that this timeframe primarily captures "newbie gains," where any novel stimulus produces results, and that the findings may not apply to trained individuals who require more sophisticated programming for continued progress. The study's focus on untrained males was seen as a significant limitation for generalizing the results.

Finally, there was a strong counter-narrative to the "no pain, no gain" mentality. While the study required training to failure, many commenters warned against equating this with pushing to the point of injury. They distinguished between productive exhaustion and harmful pain, advising that consistency and listening to one's body are more important for long-term health and progress than recklessly chasing failure in every session.
