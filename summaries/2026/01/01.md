**Score:** 268 | **Comments:** 302 | **ID:** 46444076

> **Article:** Researchers at the University of Helsinki have developed a new material for capturing carbon dioxide directly from the air (Direct Air Capture or DAC). The method uses a material called "calcium-ammonia borate," which binds with CO2 at relatively low temperatures (around 35°C). A key advantage is that the material can be regenerated and reused by heating it to 60°C, which is significantly less energy-intensive than many existing DAC technologies that require much higher temperatures. The researchers claim this process is more efficient and cost-effective than current solvent or sorbent-based systems, potentially making large-scale atmospheric carbon removal more feasible.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion surrounding this article is largely skeptical, focusing on the immense economic, logistical, and physical challenges of Direct Air Capture (DAC). The community's reaction can be broken down into several key themes:

A primary concern is **economics and efficiency**. Commenters question whether any DAC technology can compete with the low cost of natural solutions like planting trees. However, others counter that forests are not a permanent solution, as they can die or burn, releasing their stored carbon, and cannot possibly absorb enough CO2 to offset current emission rates.

There is significant debate about the **"scaling problem."** Many users point out that because atmospheric CO2 is so dilute (around 420 parts per million), capturing it requires moving a massive volume of air. This makes DAC fundamentally different and more difficult than capturing CO2 at the source (like a power plant exhaust), where the concentration is much higher. The sheer amount of energy and infrastructure required to "scrub" the entire atmosphere is seen by many as a near-insurmountable barrier.

Finally, the discussion highlights the **problem of what to do with the captured CO2.** While some see potential uses like creating synthetic fuels or refrigerants, many commenters are wary of the risks associated with long-term storage. The potential for catastrophic leaks from underground reservoirs, similar to the Lake Nyos disaster, is mentioned as a major safety concern. The overall sentiment is that while the scientific advancement is interesting, DAC faces profound practical hurdles and is not a silver bullet for climate change.
