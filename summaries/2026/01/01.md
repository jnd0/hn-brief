## [Bluetooth Headphone Jacking: A Key to Your Phone [video]](https://media.ccc.de/v/39c3-bluetooth-headphone-jacking-a-key-to-your-phone)
**Score:** 301 | **Comments:** 93 | **ID:** 46453204

> **Article:** This article, based on a presentation at the 39th Chaos Communication Congress (39C3), details a series of critical vulnerabilities in Bluetooth chipsets from the manufacturer Airoha. The exploit chain, nicknamed "Bluetooth Headphone Jacking," allows an attacker in physical proximity to take over headphones and other Bluetooth peripherals. The attack works by first gaining an unauthenticated connection to the target device, then using a proprietary protocol (RACE) to dump the device's flash memory. This memory dump contains the Bluetooth Link Keys for all previously paired devices. With this key, an attacker can impersonate the headphones and connect to the user's smartphone, gaining a trusted position from which to launch further attacks or exfiltrate data. The researchers demonstrate this on popular, high-end headphones from brands like Sony, Marshall, and Jabra, and release tooling for users to check their devices and for further research.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion revolves around three main themes: the severity and mechanics of the exploit, the broader context of Bluetooth's inherent insecurity, and the ongoing debate over the removal of the headphone jack.

Many commenters expressed alarm at the vulnerability's implications, with some providing a step-by-step breakdown of the attack, highlighting how the theft of a single cryptographic key allows an attacker to maintain access to the phone even after the headphones are out of range. This led to a wider critique of Bluetooth as a technology, with several users agreeing that it is a "messy standard" and "hacks on hacks," expressing vindication for platforms like OpenBSD that have historically avoided implementing Bluetooth due to its complexity and security risks. The poor state of Bluetooth diagnostics, like the lack of signal strength indicators, was cited as evidence of its opaque and "cursed" nature.

This incident was frequently framed as a bitter consequence of the industry-wide removal of the 3.5mm headphone jack, a move initiated by Apple under the banner of "courage." ## [Resistance training load does not determine hypertrophy](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP289684)
**Score:** 210 | **Comments:** 270 | **ID:** 46448998

> **Article:** The linked scientific article, "Resistance training load does not determine hypertrophy," presents the findings of a 10-week study on 20 healthy, recreationally active young men. The research compared two resistance training protocols taken to the point of muscular failure: one using high-load training (around 75% of 1-repetition maximum for 8-12 reps) and the other using low-load training (around 30% of 1-repetition maximum for 25-35 reps). The study's primary conclusion is that when volume is matched and training is performed to failure, both high-load and low-load protocols produce similar increases in muscle hypertrophy (size). While both groups saw similar muscle growth, the high-load group did experience a greater increase in one-rep max strength, a finding that was expected but not the main focus of the study.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely validates the study's findings, framing them as confirmation of a well-understood but not widely known principle in fitness: muscle growth is primarily driven by effort and proximity to muscular failure, not the specific weight used. Many commenters agree that as long as a set is taken to or near failure, the rep range is secondary for hypertrophy. Practicality is a key theme; while low-rep/heavy-weight and high-rep/light-weight training can both build muscle, the former is often preferred for its time efficiency.

However, the discussion also raises significant caveats and critiques. A major point is the study's limited scope and applicability. Commenters question the relevance of the findings for trained individuals, arguing that "newbie gains" make any stimulus effective for untrained participants, and that the 10-week duration is too short to observe meaningful long-term adaptations. The study's narrow demographic (20 young men) is also highlighted as a limitation for generalizing the results.

Beyond the study's methodology, the conversation branches into related topics. Several users differentiate between goals, noting that while hypertrophy may be similar across rep ranges, strength and endurance adaptations are specific to the training protocol. There are also important practical warnings about training to failure, with one user sharing a personal story of injury and others debating the "no pain, no gain" mantra, suggesting that training should be challenging but not necessarily painful. Ultimately, the community consensus is that while the study adds to the evidence, the core idea—that sufficient effort is the key driver of muscle growth—is a long-established principle.

The law aims to significantly streamline the previously cumbersome process of individually contacting hundreds of data brokers. The service is currently under development, with a planned launch date of August 1, 2026.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is largely positive, viewing the Delete Act as a significant step forward for consumer privacy, with many commenters comparing it to Europe's GDPR but noting its unique centralized infrastructure as a key improvement. The conversation explores several key themes:

*   **Potential Unintended Consequences:** A prominent concern is the "absence of data" paradox. Some worry that having a complete data footprint erased could negatively impact individuals in scenarios that rely on background checks, such as applying for loans or housing, potentially making them appear riskier than those with a verifiable history.
*   **Scope and Definition of "Data Broker":** There was debate over which companies would be affected. The discussion clarified that the law specifically targets businesses that collect and sell data on consumers with whom they have no direct relationship. While this may not apply to giants like Google or Facebook in their primary roles (as they don't typically *sell* raw data), the definition could still have broad implications.
*   **Legislative Strategy and Future Impact:** Some users noted that state-level laws like this can act as a catalyst for federal legislation, as companies may eventually lobby for a single national standard to avoid complying with a patchwork of different state laws.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Commenters also discussed the current status of the service, confirming it is not yet live and is scheduled for a 2026 launch, and noted the enforcement mechanisms, which include daily fines for non-compliance.
