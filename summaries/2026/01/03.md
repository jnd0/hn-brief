## [Publish on your own site, syndicate elsewhere](https://indieweb.org/POSSE#)
**Score:** 763 | **Comments:** 182 | **ID:** 46468600

> **Article:** The article (via the IndieWeb wiki) introduces POSSE, which stands for "Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere." It is a strategy for reclaiming ownership and control over one's online content. Instead of publishing directly to centralized social media platforms (like Twitter, Facebook, or Medium), users should first publish content on their own personal website or blog. They can then syndicate (cross-post) that content to other platforms to reach their audience there. This approach ensures that the user retains the original copy of their work, maintains control over their identity, and is not dependent on the policies or longevity of third-party platforms.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News community largely embraced the POSSE philosophy, viewing it as a principled way to reclaim ownership of one's content and identity from large tech platforms. The discussion revolved around several key themes:

*   **Practical Implementation:** Users shared their personal workflows. One commenter described manually posting to their site and then syndicating links to Mastodon, Bluesky, and Twitter, while another suggested tools like "Posse Party" that can automate this process by using a site's Atom feed.
*   **Historical Context and Idealism:** Many commenters expressed nostalgia for the "old web" and the ideals of the semantic web, federated protocols (like RSS and microformats), and decentralized identity. They lamented the centralization driven by platforms like Facebook and hoped that models like POSSE could help build a more distributed social graph.

Another argued that the desire to return to a pre-centralized internet is a regression, suggesting that digital connection remains vital for many. **Score:** 415 | **Comments:** 821 | **ID:** 46465327

> **Article:** The article from The Register, published on the 30th anniversary of IPv6's specification, examines why the protocol has not fully replaced IPv4. It argues that while IPv6 has not "taken over the world" in the sense of making IPv4 obsolete, it has been a critical success by absorbing the massive growth in internet traffic, particularly in mobile, broadband, and cloud environments. The piece highlights that the original goal of a complete transition has been replaced by a "dual-stack" reality, where the continued viability of IPv4 is largely due to IPv6 taking the pressure off it elsewhere.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals a deep divide between the protocol's large-scale success and its persistent friction for individual users and smaller operators. A central theme is the discrepancy between global adoption statistics (approaching 50%) and personal experience. Several commenters working in large enterprises report that IPv6 now carries the majority (75%+) of their internet traffic, suggesting it has already "taken over" at the network edge, even if invisible to many.

However, this success is contrasted by widespread frustration from individuals, particularly those self-hosting services. Key pain points include:
*   **ISP Unwillingness:** Many residential ISPs, even in 2025, refuse to provide static IPv6 prefixes, forcing users to rely on dynamic DNS scripts or stick with IPv4.
*   **Inconsistent Support:** A lack of native IPv6 support from some ISPs and mobile operators, coupled with incomplete support in certain applications (especially games), creates a fragmented and unreliable experience.
*   **Perceived Complexity and Lack of Incentive:** For many, the added configuration and troubleshooting of a dual-stack environment outweighs the benefits, as IPv4 "just works."

A significant sub-thread debated privacy and security. One commenter argued that IPv6's direct device addressing is a surveillance risk and that IPv4's use of NAT provides "casual anonymity." This was countered by others who pointed out that ISPs can link a household to both an IPv4 and an IPv6 block, and that browser fingerprinting is a far greater privacy threat than the IP address itself. Ultimately, the consensus is that while IPv6 has succeeded in solving the address exhaustion problem for the mobile and cloud internet, it has failed to become the simple, default option for the hobbyist or small-scale user.
