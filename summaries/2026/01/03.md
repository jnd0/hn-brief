## [Recursive Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.24601)
**Score:** 88 | **Comments:** 13 | **ID:** 46475395

> **Article:** The paper "Recursive Language Models" proposes a new architecture to overcome the context window limitations of standard LLMs. The core idea is to treat long prompts and context not as a static input to be processed, but as an external "environment" that the model can interact with. Instead of feeding a massive prompt directly into the transformer, the model uses a recursive process: it can issue "symbolic" queries to this environment to retrieve relevant information. This retrieved information is then fed into the LLM's context. The process can repeat, with the model making further queries based on what it has already retrieved, effectively allowing it to "reason" over a much larger information space than its native context window would allow. This makes the model itself responsible for the retrieval mechanism, rather than relying on a separate, pre-defined retrieval step.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion centered on how this concept relates to existing techniques and whether it represents a genuine innovation. The primary comparison was to Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). While some initially saw it as just RAG, others pointed out a key distinction: in traditional RAG, the retrieval logic is fixed by the developer, whereas in this model, the LLM itself *decides* what to search for and when, making it more "agentic."

Several commenters argued the idea was not new, equating it to the use of "sub-agents" (e.g., having one LLM call another to read a file) or noting that similar recursive agent frameworks were attempted in 2023 but failed due to the limitations of the models at the time. A point of clarification was made that true recursion would require sub-agents that can themselves call further sub-agents. There was also some skepticism about the novelty, with one user linking to a similar recent paper. On a practical note, one commenter expressed a wish for major AI providers to expose hooks that would allow such techniques to be implemented as custom "compaction" strategies for their tools.
