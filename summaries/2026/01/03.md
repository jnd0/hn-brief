## [Publish on your own site, syndicate elsewhere](https://indieweb.org/POSSE#)
**Score:** 703 | **Comments:** 174 | **ID:** 46468600

> **Article:** The article (via the IndieWeb wiki) introduces the POSSE (Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere) strategy. This philosophy advocates for individuals to maintain ownership of their content by publishing it on a personal website they control first, and then sharing or cross-posting (syndicating) it to third-party social media platforms like Twitter, Mastodon, or Substack. The goal is to reclaim ownership of one's digital identity and content from the "silos" of large tech companies, ensuring the personal site remains the primary archive and source of truth.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News community largely embraced the POSSE philosophy, viewing it as a return to the original, decentralized ideals of the web. The discussion centered on several key themes:

*   **Ownership and Control:** The primary motivation cited was reclaiming ownership of one's content and audience from platforms that monetize user data and can arbitrarily ban users. Commenters contrasted POSSE with the more common PESOS (Publish Elsewhere, Syndicate to your Own Site) model, noting that POSSE ensures the personal site is the single source of truth.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Several users shared their personal workflows. One user described manually posting to their site and then syndicating to Mastodon, Bluesky, and Twitter, while another mentioned using tools like "Posse Party" to automate the process via RSS feeds. The difficulty of automating for platforms with restrictive APIs (like Twitter and LinkedIn) was also noted.
*   **Historical Context and Idealism:** Many commenters lamented the loss of older, open web standards like RSS, microformats, and semantic web technologies, which they believe could have created a distributed social graph similar to email. The conversation was framed as a pushback against the centralized, walled-garden model of modern social media.
*   **Counterpoints and Nuance:** While the sentiment was overwhelmingly positive, a few dissenting voices emerged. One user argued that the desire to return to personal blogs and decentralized platforms is a nostalgic reaction to the current internet landscape (including LLMs and platform concentration), suggesting a broader return to non-digital life. Another commenter pointed out the irony of discussing this on Hacker News, a centralized platform itself, though others noted that HN's model is less predatory than social media's.

## [IPv6 just turned 30 and still hasn't taken over the world](https://www.theregister.com/2025/12/31/ipv6_at_30/)
**Score:** 388 | **Comments:** 758 | **ID:** 46465327

> **Article:** The article from The Register, published on the cusp of 2026, marks the 30th anniversary of the creation of the IPv6 protocol. It frames the discussion around the long-predicted "IPv4 exhaustion" and the slow, incomplete transition to IPv6. The article argues that while IPv6 has not "taken over the world" in the way early proponents hoped, it has been a silent success story in the sectors that needed it most. Specifically, it highlights that IPv6's primary achievement was absorbing the massive growth in mobile networks, broadband, and cloud infrastructure, thereby relieving the pressure on the finite supply of IPv4 addresses and keeping the old protocol viable for legacy and residential use. The piece concludes that IPv6 is a qualified success, even if it hasn't replaced IPv4 entirely and remains invisible to most end-users.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals a deep and persistent divide between the theoretical necessity of IPv6 and its practical implementation. The consensus is that while IPv6 adoption is significant—especially in mobile and large-scale enterprise networks—its failure to become universal stems from a combination of technical inertia, poor ISP support, and user experience friction.

Several key themes emerge from the comments:

*   **The "Invisible Success" vs. User Frustration:** Many engineers confirm that a majority of internet traffic (some estimate over 75% in their environments) is now IPv6, particularly from mobile devices. However, this success is largely invisible to users who still rely on IPv4 for many services. For individual users, hobbyists, and self-hosters, IPv6 is often a source of frustration. Commenters report that their ISPs either don't offer it, refuse to provide static prefixes for residential plans, or have broken implementations, forcing them to stick with IPv4 or rely on complex workarounds like dynamic DNS or tunnels.

*   **The Knowledge and Compatibility Gap:** A recurring point is that the networking industry suffers from a significant knowledge gap; many engineers simply don't understand IPv6. This lack of expertise contributes to poor implementation and support. Furthermore, compatibility remains a major hurdle. Several commenters noted that specific software, particularly game servers, and even some mobile carriers still fail to handle IPv6 correctly, making a pure-IPv6 setup unreliable for general use.

*   **The Privacy and Security Debate:** A prominent sub-thread debated the privacy implications of IPv6. One commenter argued that IPv6's design for end-to-end transparency is a liability in an age of surveillance, making it easier to track individual devices compared to the "casual anonymity" of IPv4 behind NAT. The counter-argument was that ISPs can already identify households via IPv4 assignments and that modern tracking relies more on browser fingerprinting than IP addresses. Others noted that IPv6 privacy extensions exist and that users can still implement their own NAT-like behavior if desired.

*   **The Economic Argument:** A compelling point raised is the emerging market for IPv4 addresses. As they become scarcer, they are being treated like a financial asset—bought, sold, and leased on a secondary market. This creates a direct economic incentive for businesses and individuals to finally make the switch to IPv6, where addresses are free and plentiful, rather than perpetually renting the legacy ones.

## [The rsync algorithm (1996) [pdf]](https://www.andrew.cmu.edu/course/15-749/READINGS/required/cas/tridgell96.pdf)
**Score:** 148 | **Comments:** 20 | **ID:** 46466734

> **Article:** The article is the 1996 paper by Andrew Tridgell detailing the "rsync algorithm." It presents a novel method for efficiently transferring and synchronizing files between two locations across a network. The core innovation is that instead of transferring the entire file, the algorithm calculates rolling checksums for fixed-size blocks of the file on both the source and destination. It then exchanges these checksums to identify which blocks are already present on the destination side. Only the missing or changed blocks are transferred, drastically reducing the amount of data sent, especially for files with small modifications. This approach made remote backups and file synchronization significantly faster and more bandwidth-efficient.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion is a mix of appreciation for the paper's classic computer science principles and personal anecdotes about the enduring utility of rsync. A prominent theme is a lament for the perceived shift in software engineering focus from solving fundamental efficiency problems (like rsync) to managing the complexities of modern frameworks and patching security vulnerabilities. One user countered this by noting that early systems had terrible security, a trade-off for the focus on efficiency.

Several users shared practical applications of rsync, such as efficiently managing large ISO images for virtual machines and performing remote backups of mail servers decades ago. A technical note was raised about rsync's default reliance on file size and modification time, which can fail in reproducible build environments (like Nix) where timestamps are static, requiring the use of the `--checksum` flag for accuracy.

The conversation also highlighted the impressive legacy of rsync's author, Andrew Tridgell. He is credited with reverse-engineering the SMB protocol to create Samba and writing a free BitKeeper client, which were pivotal events that helped lead to the creation of Git. Finally, users mentioned alternative implementations like the permissively licensed openrsync and discussed other tools like Google's CDC, which claims to be significantly faster than rsync in some scenarios.
