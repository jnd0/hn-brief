## [Finding and fixing Ghostty's largest memory leak](https://mitchellh.com/writing/ghostty-memory-leak-fix)
**Score:** 303 | **Comments:** 65 | **ID:** 46568794

> **Article:** The article by Ghostty terminal developer Mitchellh details the process of finding and fixing a significant memory leak in the terminal's scrollback buffer. The leak was triggered when the terminal handled "non-standard" page sizes, which occurred when using certain UTF-8 characters or when the terminal was resized to non-standard dimensions. The root cause was a flaw in the memory management system's logic: it would attempt to reuse a memory block that had been allocated with a non-standard size, but the re-initialization process assumed a standard size. This mismatch caused the memory to be freed incorrectly, leading to a leak. The fix was simple and elegant: if the oldest memory block to be pruned is of a non-standard size, it is simply discarded rather than being added to the reuse pool, ensuring all reused blocks have a predictable, standard size.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion was overwhelmingly positive, praising the author for the clear, accessible write-up and for fixing the bug. A key theme was the importance of a reliable reproduction case, which was instrumental in solving the issue. Users noted that the problem had been present for years but was recently triggered more frequently by the use of AI coding tools like Claude Code.

The developer, Mitchellh, actively participated, clarifying several points:
*   He defended his initial cautious stance on the bug report, explaining that without a reliable reproduction, it was difficult to diagnose, and its impact was limited despite being a large leak in magnitude.
*   He addressed a user's suggestion for an alternative fix, explaining that the chosen solution was simple and aligned with the existing architecture, while other approaches would require more complex changes without sufficient empirical data to justify them.
*   He also discussed his use of AI to generate visualizations for the blog post, viewing it as a practical tool for one-off, non-maintained assets.

Other comments included users sharing similar experiences, questions about the technical stack, and alternative implementation ideas, which the developer and others addressed by explaining the trade-offs between simplicity and performance.

## [I got paid minimum wage to solve an impossible problem](https://tiespetersen.substack.com/p/i-got-paid-minimum-wage-to-solve)
**Score:** 85 | **Comments:** 35 | **ID:** 46564618

> **Article:** The article "I got paid minimum wage to solve an impossible problem" is a personal narrative by a CS student who took a job as a cleaner at a large grocery store. The "impossible problem" was to find the most efficient path to sweep the entire store every night. The author initially approached this as a pure optimization problem, attempting to model it as a variation of the Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP) to find the shortest path. However, he discovered that a purely mathematically optimal path was impractical for a human. It involved too many tight turns, ignored the physical constraints of a broom and cart, and was mentally taxing to follow. The key insight was that the "cost function" he was optimizing for (pure distance) was wrong. The real-world problem required optimizing for human factors like ease of execution, mental load, and physical constraints, not just raw efficiency. The author concludes that technical correctness is worthless if you're solving the wrong problem, and the most critical step is defining what you should be optimizing for in the first place.
>
> **Discussion:** The HN discussion largely validated the author's core insight, using it as a springboard to explore broader themes in computer science, business, and systems design. The central point of agreement was that defining the correct objective function (the "cost function") is often more difficult and more important than the algorithmic optimization itself. Commenters noted that optimizing for "human satisfaction" is an "NP-hard" problem in itself, as preferences are subjective and varied.

Several distinct themes emerged from the conversation:

*   **Problem Classification:** Some users immediately tried to classify the problem, identifying it as a Hamiltonian cycle or a variation of the TSP, and noted its NP-hard nature. The counterpoint was made that even for NP-hard problems, useful approximate solutions (heuristics) are often sufficient and practical, and for a fixed, small number of locations (like in a store), the complexity becomes a non-issue.
*   **The "Right Problem" Fallacy:** The article's conclusion was widely praised as a crucial lesson for tech professionals. Commenters related this to common industry pitfalls, such as cargo-culting optimization practices from large tech companies (FAANG) without considering if a smaller-scale application actually needs them. The discussion included examples showing that many businesses operate at a scale where complex optimization is premature.
*   **Human vs. Machine Logic:** A key sub-thread focused on the gap between a theoretical solution and a practical, human-centric one. One commenter used the analogy of a Roomba, which cleans differently than a human, to highlight that different agents have different "cost functions." Another pointed out that for tasks like mowing a lawn or sweeping, the penalty for turns is often much higher than for distance, a nuance a simple algorithm might miss.
*   **Broader Systems Thinking:** Some users elevated the discussion to a philosophical level, suggesting that optimizing for metrics that are merely "easy to measure" rather than what is "genuinely needed" is a root cause of inefficiency and even "oppression" in complex systems.
*   **Practical Advice:** On a practical note, one commenter suggested that the author's approach of trying to model the problem from scratch was unnecessary, as a dynamics model for a trajectory planner would have been a more appropriate tool from the start.

## [Overdose deaths are falling in America because of a 'supply shock': study](https://www.economist.com/united-states/2026/01/08/why-overdose-deaths-are-falling-in-america)
**Score:** 83 | **Comments:** 64 | **ID:** 46569312

> **Article:** An article in *The Economist* (Jan 8, 2026) cites a study in *Science* suggesting that the recent decline in US overdose deaths is primarily due to a "supply shock" in the illicit fentanyl market. The study argues that a crackdown on precursor chemicals in China, which began in 2023, severely disrupted the production of fentanyl in Mexico. This resulted in a decrease in the purity of fentanyl products available in the US, leading to fewer fatal overdoses. The article contrasts this supply-side explanation with other theories, such as increased availability of Narcan or the end of the COVID-19 pandemic.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals significant skepticism and debate regarding the "supply shock" theory. While some commenters confirm that China did indeed crack down on precursor chemicals in 2023, often attributing it to Biden-era diplomatic cooperation, others challenge the study's conclusions.

A central point of contention is the timeline of events. Several users point out that overdose deaths began to decline in mid-2023, months before any reported changes in fentanyl purity occurred in 2024. This chronological mismatch leads many to question the supply shock as the primary cause. Alternative explanations are frequently proposed:

*   **Increased Harm Reduction:** The widespread availability and adoption of Narcan (naloxone) is cited as a major factor, with its over-the-counter approval in March 2023 coinciding with the peak in deaths.
*   **Market Adaptation:** Some argue that the theory of users consuming less due to lower purity is flawed, suggesting they would simply take more to achieve the same effect. An alternative view is that less potent fentanyl means less of the deadly drug is being laced into other substances, reducing accidental overdoses.
*   **Population Depletion:** A recurring, grim hypothesis is that the most vulnerable population has already been "decimated," and the decline reflects a natural tapering of the crisis rather than a supply-side intervention.
*   **Distrust of Institutions:** The discussion includes anecdotes about the unethical practices of some methadone clinics, fueling a broader skepticism of official narratives and institutional solutions to the opioid crisis.

Overall, the community is divided. Some accept the study's data on purity drops but debate its causal link to mortality, while others point to conflicting data and alternative evidence (such as fentanyl production shifting to domestic labs in Mexico) to argue the supply shock narrative is either incorrect or incomplete.
