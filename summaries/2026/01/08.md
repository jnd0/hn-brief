## [“Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead” (2016)](https://lbstanza.org/purpose_of_programming_languages.html)
**Score:** 220 | **Comments:** 202 | **ID:** 46525640

> **Article:** The 2016 article "Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead" argues that developers should focus on creating libraries within existing, mature, general-purpose languages rather than inventing new, specialized programming languages. The author posits that a powerful library in a language like Ruby can provide a Domain-Specific Language (DSL) that is just as expressive and effective as a standalone language, but without the high adoption cost of a new toolchain, ecosystem, and syntax.

The core thesis is that a language's features directly enable or constrain its libraries. Using Ruby on Rails as a prime example, the author argues that Rails' success is fundamentally tied to Ruby's specific features like powerful metaprogramming, blocks (first-class functions), and a flexible object system. These features allow Rails to create an intuitive DSL for web development that abstracts away complexity for non-expert programmers. The author contends that trying to replicate such a framework in a less powerful language like Java would be impossible or result in a clunky, inferior product. Therefore, the effort is better spent on libraries that elevate the capabilities of established, accessible languages.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals a mix of agreement, disagreement, and meta-commentary on the article's premise. A key point of contention, raised early on, was that the title was presented without quotation marks, which slightly altered its meaning and likely influenced initial reactions.

Several commenters supported the article's core idea, emphasizing the practical benefits of libraries over new languages. They noted that libraries allow developers to stay within their preferred ecosystems and avoid the steep cost of adopting a new language, which includes new tooling and hiring challenges. This camp often distinguished between "tool libraries" that they control and "frameworks" that dictate application structure, expressing a preference for the former.

Conversely, some users argued that the article's premise is an oversimplification. They championed the "Weak Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis"—that the language you use fundamentally shapes how you think about and solve problems. From this perspective, a well-designed language offers unique conceptual advantages that libraries in other languages cannot fully replicate. This debate was exemplified by a lengthy counter-argument from a Ruby developer who defended the language's intrinsic merits and unique design, arguing that its value isn't just as a vehicle for Rails and that its decline is overstated.

Other notable themes included:
*   **Meta-critique of HN:** A user humorously predicted that many would comment based on the title alone, a sentiment echoed by others who discussed the role of titles as "ice breakers" for discussion.
*   **Modern Context:** A commenter suggested that with the rise of LLMs, building a DSL as a Python library might be more effective than creating a new language, as models are already highly proficient in Python.
*   **Nuance:** A few voices advocated for a more pragmatic, less dogmatic approach, suggesting that the right choice depends on the specific problem, and that sometimes a language is indeed the better tool.
