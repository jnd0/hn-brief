## [A4 Paper Stories](https://susam.net/a4-paper-stories.html)
**Score:** 303 | **Comments:** 148 | **ID:** 46525888

> **Article:** The article "A4 Paper Stories" explores the mathematical elegance and practical utility of the international standard A-series paper sizes. The author explains that the system is based on a single principle: the ratio of a sheet's length to its width is the square root of 2 (1:√2). This ensures that when a sheet is cut in half, the resulting halves retain the same aspect ratio. The article details how this ratio allows for an infinite series of sizes (A0, A1, A2, etc.), where each size is exactly half the area of the previous one. The author then demonstrates how these properties can be used for practical, on-the-fly measurements, such as using the known dimensions of an A4 sheet as a convenient, non-standardized ruler for everyday objects.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals a mix of appreciation for the article's geeky charm and debate over its practical utility. The core topic of A-series paper's mathematical properties was widely enjoyed, with several users highlighting the elegance of the 1:√2 ratio and its benefits for paper production (minimizing waste) and logistics (calculating weight via GSM).

Key discussion points included:

*   **Practicality vs. Novelty:** A recurring theme was skepticism about the "Measuring Stuff" section. Several commenters argued that using a piece of paper as a ruler is a common, intuitive act, and that the article's method essentially requires memorizing the exact dimensions of A4 paper, rather than cleverly applying the √2 ratio. Others defended the article as a fun and interesting exploration of a familiar object.
*   **The Metric System's Elegance:** The most praised practical tip was the relationship between paper size, density (GSM), and weight. One user explained that because A0 is defined as 1 square meter, the weight of a single sheet of A4 is simply its GSM divided by 16, a calculation that simplifies postage and logistics.
*   **Cultural and Standard Comparisons:** American users contrasted the A-series with the "8.5 x 11" US Letter standard. One argued that the simple, rational dimensions of US Letter are easier to work with, while another countered that the A-series' primary advantage is for manufacturers, not consumers.
*   **Humor and Sarcasm:** A few comments offered satirical takes, such as a user "inventing" a system of string and scissors for measurement, or another sarcastically questioning if using a known object to measure makes one a "hacker."

## [“Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead” (2016)](https://lbstanza.org/purpose_of_programming_languages.html)
**Score:** 227 | **Comments:** 203 | **ID:** 46525640

> **Article:** The 2016 article "Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead" argues that developers should focus on creating libraries within existing, powerful general-purpose languages rather than inventing new, standalone Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs). The author posits that a well-designed general-purpose language (using Ruby and Scheme as examples) is itself the ultimate tool for creating DSLs. By building a library, you provide an API that allows users to construct an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) or express domain logic, effectively giving them a DSL without forcing them to adopt a new, often immature, language ecosystem. The core benefit is that this approach leverages the mature tooling, libraries, and developer base of the host language, making the solution more practical and easier to adopt.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion on Hacker News shows a mix of agreement, nuanced debate, and some tangential conversations. A significant portion of the comments focused on the article's title, with some users admitting they commented without reading the piece, a phenomenon one user called out directly.

The main points of agreement centered on the practical challenges of adopting new languages versus libraries. Commenters noted that new languages require a full switch of tooling and are risky to adopt due to hiring difficulties, whereas libraries integrate into existing, preferred environments. This led to a distinction between "tool libraries" (which the author prefers) and "frameworks" (which often dictate control flow).

A key counterargument, articulated in a lengthy comment, is the "Weak Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis": the idea that a language's features fundamentally shape how you think about and solve problems. While a library can emulate a DSL, the underlying language's power (e.g., Ruby's first-class functions and metaprogramming) determines how elegant and effective that emulation can be. This user argued that a superior language leads to superior libraries and that dismissing language design ignores this fundamental impact.

Other threads emerged:
*   **The Definition of a DSL:** One user questioned the term's fuzzy boundaries, suggesting that a library that parses a new syntax (like a JSON library) blurs the line between a library and a language.
*   **Modern Alternatives:** A user seeking a typed scripting language to replace Bash sparked a discussion on options like Python (with type checkers), Elixir, and Haskell.
*   **Ruby's Relevance:** A debate erupted over Ruby's popularity, with one user declaring it's on an "extinction path" like Perl, while another countered that it remains a massive, healthy ecosystem underpinning major websites like Shopify and GitHub.
