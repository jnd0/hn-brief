## [A4 Paper Stories](https://susam.net/a4-paper-stories.html)
**Score:** 332 | **Comments:** 152 | **ID:** 46525888

> **Article:** The article "A4 Paper Stories" explores the mathematical elegance and practical utility of the A-series paper standard. The author begins by explaining the core principle of the A-series: the aspect ratio is the square root of 2 (≈1.414). This unique ratio ensures that when a sheet is cut in half, the resulting smaller sheets retain the exact same proportions. The article details how this allows the entire series (A0, A1, A2, etc.) to be derived from a single definition: A0 has an area of exactly one square meter, and each subsequent size is half the area of the previous one. The author then demonstrates how these properties can be used for practical, real-world measurement, using a sheet of A4 paper as an improvised ruler to measure objects by comparing their dimensions to the known lengths of the paper's sides and diagonals.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion was largely positive, with many users appreciating the article for its blend of mathematical trivia and practical life-hacks, viewing it as the kind of interesting, non-critical content that makes the platform enjoyable.

Key points of discussion included:

*   **Practicality and Life-Hacks:** The most popular and detailed comment highlighted a practical use of the A-series standard: calculating paper weight. Because A0 paper has an area of 1 square meter, the paper's density in grams per square meter (GSM) directly corresponds to the weight of a single A0 sheet. This allows for simple integer arithmetic to determine the weight of multiple sheets (e.g., an A4 sheet is 1/16th of an A0, so 80 GSM paper means each A4 sheet weighs 5 grams), which is useful for postage and logistics.

*   **Comparison to US Letter:** Several users from North America compared the A4 standard to the US Letter format (8.5" x 11"). Some Americans found the A4 ratio (involving √2) to be less intuitive than the simple, non-proportional dimensions of Letter paper. Conversely, Europeans living in the US expressed a "cognitive dissonance," finding the US Letter format to appear "squat" or "stubby" after being accustomed to the slimmer A4.

*   **Skepticism and Nitpicking:** A few commenters were more critical. One user argued that the "Measuring Stuff" section was not particularly insightful, as it essentially boils down to using a known object as a ruler—a common practice. Others pointed out minor typos in the original article.

*   **Historical and Technical Context:** The discussion also included interesting ancillary information, such as the historical origin of the √2 ratio (credited to Lichtenberg in 1786), the existence of B and C series sizes (which are useful as envelope sizes), and the industrial advantage of the A-series for paper producers who can minimize waste when cutting large sheets.

## [“Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead” (2016)](https://lbstanza.org/purpose_of_programming_languages.html)
**Score:** 242 | **Comments:** 229 | **ID:** 46525640

> **Article:** The 2016 article "Stop Designing Languages. Write Libraries Instead" argues that developers should focus on creating libraries within existing, powerful general-purpose languages rather than inventing new Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs). The author contends that a well-designed library in a language like Ruby can provide a DSL-like experience through its API, offering the same benefits without the high cost of forcing users to adopt an entirely new toolchain and ecosystem. The piece uses Ruby on Rails as a prime example, explaining that its success is due to Ruby's powerful features (like first-class functions and dynamic metaproxying) which allow it to create an expressive, high-level DSL for web development. The core thesis is that a language's power directly enables the quality and usability of its libraries, and that computer scientists should prioritize improving these general-purpose languages and their libraries to solve domain-specific problems more effectively.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion on Hacker News is multifaceted, with users engaging with the article's title, its core arguments, and the broader philosophy of language and library design. A recurring meta-comment, made preemptively by some, is that many HN users likely commented based on the provocative title alone without reading the article's nuanced content.

Several key themes emerge from the conversation:

*   **The Blurring Line Between Languages and Libraries:** Many commenters challenge the article's premise by arguing that the distinction is not always clear. One user posits that a sufficiently advanced library (like a JSON parser) can function as a language, while another suggests that the debate is a false dichotomy and the two concepts are complementary. The idea that libraries and frameworks are different, with frameworks often imposing control flow, was also raised.

*   **Practicality and Adoption:** A significant portion of the discussion focuses on the real-world challenges of adopting new languages versus new libraries. Users agree that introducing a library is far less disruptive than a new language, which requires changes to the entire environment, tooling, and hiring practices. This practicality is seen as a major advantage for the library-centric approach.

*   **The Role of Language Features (Sapir-Whorf):** Several users supported the article's implicit point about the "Weak Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis"—that a language's features shape how developers think about and solve problems. They argued that powerful language features are essential for creating elegant, high-level libraries, and that a language's design directly influences what its libraries can achieve.

*   **Counterarguments and Nuance:** The discussion wasn't purely in agreement. Some users defended the creation of new languages, pointing out that mature ecosystems can be difficult to extend and that a new language can be purpose-built for a domain in ways a library cannot. Others pushed back on the article's specific examples, arguing that DSLs and frameworks are possible in other languages (like Java) and that the benefits of Ruby on Rails were overstated or dated. The conversation also included practical examples of using modern languages for scripting, demonstrating a real-world desire for the kind of power the article advocates for.
