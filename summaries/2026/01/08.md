## [A4 Paper Stories](https://susam.net/a4-paper-stories.html)
**Score:** 277 | **Comments:** 136 | **ID:** 46525888

> **Article:** The article "A4 Paper Stories" explores the mathematical elegance and practical utility of the A-series paper standard. The author explains that the A-series is based on a single principle: the ratio of the long side to the short side is the square root of 2 (1:√2). This ensures that when a sheet is cut in half, the resulting pieces have the same aspect ratio. The article also details how A0 is defined as having an area of exactly one square meter, which makes the dimensions of all other sizes (A1, A2, A4, etc.) easily calculable. The author then demonstrates how this standard can be used for practical "hacking," such as using a sheet of A4 paper as an improvised ruler by marking its dimensions, and using the paper's weight (in GSM) and area properties to calculate postage without a scale.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion was largely positive, with many users appreciating the article for its interesting trivia and elegant mathematical concepts. Several commenters shared their own real-world applications, such as using a phone app for foot measurement that relies on an A4 sheet for scale, or the practical life hack of calculating postage by knowing that a standard A4 sheet weighs 5 grams (derived from 80gsm paper and its 1/16th relationship to a 1 square meter A0 sheet).

A central theme of the discussion was a comparison between the A-series and the American "Letter" (8.5" x 11") standard. While some American users expressed curiosity about the "special properties" of the A-series, others defended the simplicity of the US system. It was clarified that the primary advantage of the A-series is for paper producers, as it minimizes waste when cutting large sheets. The conversation also touched on the history of the standard, with one user noting its origin with Lichtenberg in 1786, and the nature of the metric system itself, with a user pointing out that the A-series' practicality is a happy coincidence of its definition, much like how the kilogram became the standard unit of mass despite the gram being the base unit.
