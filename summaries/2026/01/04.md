## [Cardiovascular disease is a solved problem](https://totalhealthoptimization.com/2025/09/27/cardiovascular-disease-is-a-solved-problem/)
**Score:** 82 | **Comments:** 74 | **ID:** 46478398

> **Article:** The article "Cardiovascular disease is a solved problem" argues that from a purely biomedical and scientific standpoint, we have the tools to prevent and potentially reverse heart disease. The author contends that we possess accurate biomarkers (ApoB, Lp(a)), effective risk models, and powerful new drugs (PCSK9 inhibitors, lepodisiran). The piece suggests the problem isn't a lack of scientific knowledge, but rather a failure in healthcare delivery, including misaligned financial incentives, a lack of access to specialists, and the immense difficulty of implementing preventive care at scale.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is highly skeptical of the article's premise, with commenters arguing that cardiovascular disease is far from a "solved problem." The key points of contention revolve around three main themes:

First, many challenged the article's optimism by pointing to the realities of medical practice. Several users, including some claiming familiarity with the field, stated that cardiologists would strongly disagree with the title. They highlighted that the medical field is still researching fundamental causes and that the problem is not solved for the hundreds of millions of people who already have existing plaque and other conditions.

Second, the discussion questioned the article's core assumptions. A major point of debate was the causal link between LDL cholesterol and heart disease. One commenter argued that while statins (which lower LDL) are effective, other drugs that lower LDL even more are not, suggesting LDL is a correlation, not the direct cause. Others pointed out that the article ignores non-dietary causes of heart disease, such as genetic factors (Lp(a)), valve failure, and electrical issues.

Finally, commenters focused on the immense practical and social barriers that prevent a "solution" from being implemented. The consensus was that even if the science were perfect, the problem remains unsolved due to human behavior (obesity, poor diet), systemic issues (lack of cardiologists in many counties, misaligned financial incentives), and patient-side challenges (side effects from medication like statins). The article was ultimately seen as a provocative but overly simplistic take that ignores the complexities of real-world medicine and human health.

The goal was to create a low-cost, self-hosted server. The technical stack involves installing a Linux environment (Andronix) on the non-rooted Android device, running Nginx to serve the site, and using a Cloudflare Tunnel to expose the server to the internet securely. The author highlights using "vibe coding" with Claude Code to assist in writing the website content and configuring the system via the terminal, though they still performed manual troubleshooting.

Regarding the hardware, a user argued that the title was misleading because the phone is subsidized by a carrier plan.
