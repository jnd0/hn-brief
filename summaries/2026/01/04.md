## [Understanding the bin, sbin, usr/bin, usr/sbin split (2010)](https://lists.busybox.net/pipermail/busybox/2010-December/074114.html)
**Score:** 150 | **Comments:** 114 | **ID:** 46487921

> **Article:** The linked article is a 2010 email from the BusyBox mailing list titled "Understanding the bin, sbin, usr/bin, usr/sbin split." It explains the historical reasons for the separation of directories in Unix-like filesystems. The core logic stems from a time when disk space was extremely expensive and limited. The split was a practical solution to manage storage:

*   **`/bin` and `/sbin`**: Contained essential binaries needed to boot the system and perform basic repairs. These were kept on the root filesystem, which was often small.
*   **`/usr`**: Originally stood for "user" and housed the rest of the operating system and user applications. It could be on a separate, larger (and potentially cheaper) disk partition.
*   **`sbin` vs. `bin`**: The 's' in `sbin` stands for "system," indicating these binaries are typically only run by the root administrator, not regular users.
*   **`/usr/local`**: A designated place for software installed by the system administrator, separate from the OS distribution's files in `/usr`, to prevent conflicts during system updates.

The article frames this structure not as an arbitrary design, but as a solution born from the hardware and operational constraints of early Unix systems.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion is largely a nostalgic and philosophical reflection on the legacy structures explained in the article. The central theme is the tension between historical necessity and modern convenience.

Many commenters express frustration with the complexity, humorously referencing the XKCD "Standards" comic and suggesting the structure persists simply because "it's always been that way." There's a consensus that the original reasons (like separate physical disks) are now obsolete, yet the structure remains, maintained by standards bodies and historical inertia.
