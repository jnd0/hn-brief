The system uses self-certifying identities and stable repository IDs to verify content regardless of the source, enabling untrusted parties to host data without compromising integrity.
>
> **Discussion:** The discussion centers on Radicle's architecture, its comparison to other decentralized projects, and the inherent challenges of P2P systems.

**Architectural Comparisons**
Users compared Radicle to Tangled and Forgejo. Tangled is described as federated, relying on servers ("knots") and a central AppView, whereas Radicle is strictly P2P with no servers or clients—only nodes. Forgejo is working on federation via ActivityPub (similar to Mastodon), which maintains server instances and databases, while Radicle uses self-certifying identities without central authority.

**Trust and Identity**
A key debate involved how Radicle solves the trust problem of verifying repository authenticity in a P2P network. The protocol uses cryptographic identities (DIDs) to sign repository delegates. While this doesn't solve the initial "zero-to-some" trust problem (how to trust a stranger), it secures the "some-to-more" trust (verifying known entities). Trust is established socially, similar to real-world interactions.

**Privacy, Deletion, and Safety**
Several comments highlighted the difficulty of handling mistakes or illegal content in an immutable P2P network. Users asked about deleting accidentally posted personal info or content that becomes illegal. The Radicle representative (endiangroup) acknowledged this is an active area of development, aiming to introduce "forgetting" features and safer defaults, though they noted perfection is unlikely. Others argued centralized systems offer no guarantee of deletion either.

**Technical Feasibility**
Technical questions focused on network support. Users confirmed Radicle works over IPv6, Tor, and alternative networks like Yggdrasil, addressing concerns about NAT and ISP blocking.

**General Sentiment**
The community views Radicle as a promising future for software forges, particularly valuing its censorship resistance. However, some users remain hesitant to switch from GitHub as their primary platform due to the learning curve or maturity of the ecosystem.
