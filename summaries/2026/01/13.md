## [LLVM: The bad parts](https://www.npopov.com/2026/01/11/LLVM-The-bad-parts.html)
**Score:** 266 | **Comments:** 52 | **ID:** 46588837

> **Article:** The article "LLVM: The bad parts" by Nikita Popov critiques the LLVM compiler infrastructure, focusing on its significant drawbacks despite its widespread adoption. Key issues highlighted include:

*   **Compilation Time:** LLVM is notoriously slow, both for debug builds (O0) and optimized builds. This is a major pain point for languages like Rust and PHP that rely on it.
*   **Complexity and Instability:** The internal APIs are complex and frequently change, making it difficult for frontend developers to maintain their code. While the LLVM IR itself is relatively stable, the surrounding tooling is not.
*   **Bugs and Optimization Failures:** The optimizer can be fragile, sometimes producing incorrect code or degrading performance due to issues like over-aggressive loop-invariant code motion (LICM) causing register pressure.
*   **Poor Documentation:** Key components, especially for backend development (like SelectionDAG and GlobalISel), are poorly documented, making it difficult to build or maintain custom targets.
*   **Organizational Issues:** The project suffers from a lack of comprehensive testing (especially for backends) and a code review culture that can be a bottleneck, as reviews don't offer immediate value to contributors.

The article concludes that while LLVM is a powerful tool, it is far from perfect and presents significant challenges for those who build upon it.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely validates the article's points, with contributors and users sharing their own experiences with LLVM's shortcomings. The conversation centers on a few key themes:

*   **Compilation Speed:** This is a major point of frustration. Users note that Rust's compile times are a "real PITA" due to LLVM. Some defend the choice of Go to use its own backend instead of LLVM, citing better tooling speed. However, others point to ongoing efforts to improve this, such as a new fast O0 backend and alternative code generators like Cranelift.
*   **Complexity and Stability:** Several commenters, including those who maintain LLVM-based projects, confirm that the C API is not as stable as claimed, with certain parts like the Orc JIT API changing "wildly." This makes life difficult for frontend developers.
*   **Testing and Auditing:** A significant concern is the difficulty in auditing and testing LLVM. One commenter highlights the lack of a comprehensive executable test suite starting from LLVM IR, which makes developing custom backends a guessing game. The general consensus is that optimizing compilers are nearly impossible to fully audit, though tools like `alive2` can help.
*   **Specific Technical Flaws:** The discussion delves into the technical details of optimization bugs. The issue of LICM causing register pressure is acknowledged, with debate on whether the fix belongs in the optimizer or the register allocator. The general view is that the backend has less visibility and struggles to undo bad decisions made earlier in the pipeline.
*   **Organizational Health:** The article's point about poor code review culture resonates with commenters, who note it's a problem even within companies. The lack of credit for reviews is seen as a key demotivator.

Overall, the community agrees with the article's diagnosis of LLVM's problems, viewing them as deep-seated issues related to the project's scale, complexity, and development model.
