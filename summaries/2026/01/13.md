## [LLVM: The bad parts](https://www.npopov.com/2026/01/11/LLVM-The-bad-parts.html)
**Score:** 279 | **Comments:** 55 | **ID:** 46588837

> **Article:** The article "LLVM: The bad parts" by Nikita Popov (author of PHP's JIT) critiques the LLVM compiler infrastructure, focusing on its instability, complexity, and performance issues. He argues that while LLVM is a powerful tool, it presents significant challenges for developers. Key criticisms include:

*   **Instability:** The C++ APIs are highly unstable, making it difficult to maintain frontends and passes across LLVM versions. While the C API is more stable, it is incomplete and not always consistent.
*   **Documentation & Complexity:** The documentation is poor, especially for backend components like SelectionDAG. The system is incredibly complex, with many undocumented, semi-generic rules and "magic" that make it hard to understand and extend correctly.
*   **Compilation Speed:** Compile times are notoriously slow, a problem that has worsened over time and affects developer productivity, particularly in languages like Rust.
*   **Code Quality & Review:** The quality of patches is inconsistent, and the code review process is a significant bottleneck, as reviewers often lack incentive to perform this work.
*   **Bugs:** The optimizer is a source of subtle and hard-to-diagnose bugs. The article provides a specific example where the Loop Invariant Code Motion (LICM) optimization increases register pressure, leading to worse performance, and notes that the register allocator struggles to fix these issues.

The author concludes that while LLVM is indispensable, its design and maintenance model create a high burden on its users.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely validates the article's points, with many commenters sharing their own frustrations and experiences with LLVM. The conversation centers on a few key themes:

*   **Compilation Speed:** This is a major point of contention. Several users express shock that LLVM, which was once touted as faster than GCC, now has severe compile-time issues, especially for Rust. The discussion highlights alternative backends like Cranelift and a proposed faster O0 backend for LLVM as potential solutions.
*   **Trust and Safety:** Some commenters question how Rust can be considered a "safe" language when its entire foundation rests on the massive, complex, and unauditable LLVM compiler. Others counter that Rust's own rigorous testing helps find and fix bugs in LLVM, benefiting the entire ecosystem.
*   **Technical Challenges:** Experienced developers dive into the technical weeds, confirming the article's claims. They discuss the difficulty of working with undocumented backend components, the subpar performance of the register allocator, and the fact that even the "stable" C API has unstable parts.
*   **Maintenance Burden:** The difficulty of maintaining projects built on LLVM is a recurring sentiment. One user notes that while rebasing can be manageable, the constant churn and lack of clear semantics create a high maintenance cost for front-end developers.

Overall, the discussion paints a picture of a powerful but flawed tool that the software industry has become dangerously dependent on. There is a shared sense of frustration with its complexity, instability, and the high cost of working with it.
