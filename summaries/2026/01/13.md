## [LLVM: The bad parts](https://www.npopov.com/2026/01/11/LLVM-The-bad-parts.html)
**Score:** 298 | **Comments:** 57 | **ID:** 46588837

> **Article:** The article "LLVM: The bad parts" by Nikita Popov critiques the LLVM compiler infrastructure, arguing that while it is a powerful and widely-used tool, it has significant flaws. The author identifies several key issues:
*   **Compilation Time:** LLVM has become progressively slower over the years, with large projects like Rust and Chromium suffering from long build times. This is attributed to the sheer number of optimization passes and the cost of the middle-end.
*   **Stability and Maintenance:** The internal C++ APIs are highly unstable, making it difficult for frontends (like Clang or Rust) to keep up with LLVM updates. This forces frontends to use the more limited C API, which has its own stability issues with certain components like the Orc JIT library.
*   **Optimization Bugs:** The optimizer is complex and prone to subtle bugs that can introduce security vulnerabilities. The author notes that code review is often insufficient for catching these issues.
*   **Poor Diagnostics:** When optimizations fail or produce suboptimal code, it is extremely difficult for developers to understand why, as LLVM provides little insight into its decision-making process.
*   **Backend Complexity:** The backend (SelectionDAG/GlobalISel) is poorly documented and complex, making it hard for new targets to implement.

The article concludes that while LLVM is an incredible piece of engineering, its complexity, performance, and maintenance challenges are significant and growing problems for its users.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion largely validates the article's claims, with many commenters sharing their own experiences with LLVM's shortcomings. A central theme is the trade-off between LLVM's powerful optimizations and its high costs. Compilation time is a frequent complaint, with some noting that Rust and C++ projects suffer immensely, while others point to alternatives like Cranelift or a proposed faster O0 backend as potential solutions. The discussion also highlights the difficulty of maintaining frontends due to LLVM's unstable internal APIs, contradicting the article's suggestion that the C API provides a stable shield.

There is a debate on the auditability and safety of compilers. While one commenter expresses alarm that critical software relies on an unauditable compiler, others counter that Rust's own testing and tools like Alive2 help mitigate these risks. The complexity of the backend is another key point, with developers confirming that the SelectionDAG/GlobalISel components are poorly documented and that the register allocator struggles to fix bad decisions made by earlier optimization passes. Finally, a practical problem of community contribution is raised, where the lack of "credit" for code reviews disincentivizes thorough review processes, a problem that persists even within companies.
