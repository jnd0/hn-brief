## [What came first: the CNAME or the A record?](https://blog.cloudflare.com/cname-a-record-order-dns-standards/)
**Score:** 409 | **Comments:** 145 | **ID:** 46681611

> **Article:** The Cloudflare blog post details an incident where a seemingly minor change to their authoritative DNS servers caused widespread outages. The change involved altering the order of DNS records in responses: specifically, placing CNAME records after the A records they point to, rather than before. While Cloudflare interpreted RFC 1034 as ambiguous regarding this ordering, they discovered that several critical DNS clients—including the widely used glibc `getaddrinfo` function and Cisco switch firmware—strictly required CNAMEs to appear first. This dependency on a specific, non-standardized behavior caused these clients to fail or, in the case of some Cisco switches, enter reboot loops. Cloudflare reverted the change and is now proposing a new RFC to explicitly standardize the "CNAMEs first" ordering to prevent similar issues in the future.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion focused on the nature of the failure, the interpretation of the RFC, and the broader implications for software development and standards.

A central theme was the tension between adhering to standards and accommodating real-world implementations. Many commenters argued that the RFC's language—stating that an answer is "preface[d] by one or more CNAME RRs"—is actually quite clear and requires CNAMEs to come first. They felt Cloudflare's claim of ambiguity was a misreading and that the incident was primarily a failure to test against major, real-world clients like glibc. The fact that glibc is ubiquitous, not an obscure edge case, was highlighted as a significant oversight in Cloudflare's testing process.

The discussion also invoked software engineering principles. Hyrum's Law was cited to explain how any observable system behavior becomes a dependency for users, while the validity of Postel's Law ("be liberal in what you accept") was debated, with some arguing it's now considered harmful. Commenters expressed surprise that a company like Cloudflare would deploy such a change without a comprehensive test suite covering common DNS clients.

Finally, the conversation touched on the path forward. There was a general consensus that Cloudflare's decision to conform to the de-facto standard (CNAMEs first) is the correct and pragmatic choice, even if their interpretation of the RFC was different. There was also a discussion about the IETF process, with commenters preferring updates to existing RFCs over creating entirely new standards to ensure visibility and easier adoption.

The core mechanism is not superior forecasting by makers, but rather their strategy of passively selling "Yes" contracts to optimistic bettors.
>
