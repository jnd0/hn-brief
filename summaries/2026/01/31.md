The goal is to make websites resilient to DDoS attacks and censorship, as the hosting load is distributed among the peers viewing the site.
>

A significant portion of the conversation centers on the challenges and stagnation of WebTorrent. Several users express that WebTorrent has failed to gain widespread adoption, describing it as "stagnant" and plagued by long-standing issues. The consensus is that WebTorrent doesn't work as effectively as traditional BitTorrent clients, primarily due to limitations within browsers. Key technical hurdles identified include the inability to perform true peer-to-peer discovery without relying on initial trackers, and restrictions on WebRTC connections between browsers, which prevent the kind of direct, unordered connections that a real torrent client would use. One user notes that if browsers had "real torrent clients," the conversation would be entirely different.

The project's architecture and value proposition are also scrutinized. A primary concern raised by user `elbci` is that Peerweb, as demonstrated, still relies on a central website (`peerweb.lol`) for uploading files and linking to content, making it a "single point of failure" and not truly decentralized in its current form. This leads to a broader debate about the unsolved problems in the decentralized web. While peer-to-peer storage (via torrents or IPFS) is considered a "solved" problem, commenters argue that the real missing piece is a decentralized system for addressing, searching, and discovering content without relying on centralized DNS. The discussion explores potential solutions, such as using a blockchain for DNS records or creating a distributed hash table (DHT) that can bridge WebRTC and traditional HTTP/WebSocket clients.

Finally, the discussion touches on the project's presentation and practical use cases. Some users are critical of the website's design, identifying its color palette and use of emojis as common tells of AI-generated ("vibe-coded") content, which erodes their trust. On the other hand, potential applications are discussed, such as hosting video to create DDOS-proof sites (though moderation is a major concern) or creating simple, resilient websites like wikis. One user confirms they successfully loaded an existing static site (MDwiki) using the tool, suggesting it works for certain use cases. However, skepticism remains about its usability and reliability, with one user stating the demo didn't work for them and questioning its value over using a standard torrent client.
