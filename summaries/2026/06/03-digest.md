The story of the day isn’t just about spam emails—it’s a indictment of how technology and economics conspire to exploit the desperate. The article “Please don’t spam people looking for employment. It’s just cruel” has 880 points and 249 comments, which tells you it’s a topic that’s eaten at people’s nerves. The core argument is that spam emails targeting job seekers aren’t just annoying; they’re predatory. The author frames it as a moral failure, a violation of the vulnerable. But the discussion on Hacker News reveals a more nuanced (and arguably more cynical) layer. Commenters aren’t just angry at spam—they’re arguing about whether recruiters are inherently bad or if job seekers are just poor at filtering signals. One user noted that recruiters often withhold company names, which seems like a baffling move unless they’re trying to game the system. Others suggested AI agents are flooding these inboxes, which raises the question: if AI is spamming people, who’s training these models? The thread also dredges up the uncomfortable reality that “who wants to be hired” threads are often filled with people who are either genuinely desperate or just looking for any sign of interest. It’s a mess, and it’s not clear who’s at fault here. The system seems designed to let the worst of both worlds coexist: job seekers get spam, recruiters get a way to bypass proper vetting, and AI companies get a new data source.  

This isn’t just about job boards or email clients—it’s about the infrastructure of opportunity. If you’re unemployed, you’re at the mercy of algorithms and humans who don’t care about your dignity. The comment about AI agents being behind many of these emails is telling. If these models are being trained on data that includes spam emails, they’re essentially learning to perpetuate the same behavior they’re supposed to combat. It’s a feedback loop where the tools meant to streamline hiring end up making it worse. One commenter pointed out that some recruiters might be using AI to scrape job boards and then blast generic messages, which is a perverse inversion of efficiency. The thread also touched on whether job seekers who post publicly are “down-and-out” or just open to opportunities. That’s a weird framing—it assumes that being open to work is a moral failing. In reality, it’s a survival mechanism. The discussion doesn’t resolve itself; it just highlights how much of our professional lives are now managed by systems that prioritize scale over empathy.
