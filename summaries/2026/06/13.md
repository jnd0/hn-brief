## [Electric motors with no rare earths](https://www.renaultgroup.com/en/magazine/energy-and-powertrains/all-about-electric-motors-with-no-rare-earths/)
**Score:** 672 | **Comments:** 193 | **ID:** 48510010

> **Article:** Renault’s article presents rare-earth-free electric motors as a way to power EVs without relying on permanent magnets made from scarce materials. The key technology is an electrically excited synchronous motor, where the rotor’s magnetic field is produced by copper windings instead of permanent magnets. Renault claims this approach can reach about 92% efficiency while reducing exposure to supply-chain constraints tied to rare-earth mining and processing. The article frames the motor as part of a broader push toward more sustainable and geopolitically resilient EV manufacturing.
>
> **Discussion:** Commenters quickly pointed out that motors without permanent magnets are not new; wound-rotor and electrically excited synchronous machines have existed for more than a century and are common in large industrial machines. The main technical debate centered on whether Renault’s implementation uses brushed slip rings, which can wear out, or a brushless rotor with inductive coupling and rectification, which would be more maintenance-free but harder to make efficient. Several commenters noted that the 92% efficiency claim is respectable but likely below the 95%-plus efficiency often cited for permanent-magnet motors, while others argued that a few percentage points may be an acceptable tradeoff for avoiding rare-earth dependence. Some challenged concerns about brush maintenance by citing Renault Zoe experience, claiming high-mileage operation without brush replacement and arguing that copper windings tolerate heat better than neodymium magnets. The thread also branched into comparisons with BMW and Nissan, disputed claims about European and Indian EV supply chains, and briefly explored sodium batteries as a possible pairing for low-cost, rare-earth-free EVs.
