**Score:** 340 | **Comments:** 181 | **ID:** 48385965

> **Article:** The ESP32‑S31 is a new RISC‑V based SoC from Espressif that includes SIMD instructions, CAN‑FD, Motor PWM, and a CORDIC module for fixed‑point math but no hardware floating‑point unit. It offers a 12‑bit SAR ADC with conversion times in the 10 µs range, and the chip is positioned for edge AI workloads with up to 8 MB of PSRAM. Espressif emphasizes that the SDK and toolchain are unified across its product lines, making the S31 a drop‑in replacement for earlier ESP32 variants. The product page highlights the chip’s suitability for low‑power, high‑performance embedded applications such as motor control, audio, and neural‑network inference.
>
> **Discussion:** Commentators praised the move to RISC‑V and SIMD, noting how it simplifies toolchain use for Rust and other modern languages, but some raised concerns about the lack of floating‑point hardware and the relatively slow ADC conversion times for demanding motor‑control loops. A technical debate unfolded over whether 1 µs ADC samples are realistic or necessary, with experts explaining that most motor‑control schemes rely on current sensing rather than voltage sampling, and that loop bandwidth is limited by conversion and computation delays. The thread also touched on Espressif’s naming conventions, with users arguing that the proliferation of ESP32‑X variants creates confusion, while others defended the unified SDK strategy and clarified that the different lines share the same development ecosystem. Peripheral discussions veered into wireless protocols, with Z‑Wave being dismissed as proprietary and Bluetooth audio latency being critiqued as still problematic for music applications. Finally, realistic expectations for edge AI were set: while the S31 can run small CNNs with quantisation, large models like Depth‑Anything are beyond its memory capacity, and users were advised to focus on classification or wake‑word detection rather than full‑scale inference.
