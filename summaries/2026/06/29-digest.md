The company’s response was a vague statement about "ethical guidelines" and "compliance with local laws," which is the kind of non-answer you’d expect from a corporation that’s more interested in profit than accountability. It’s the classic tech industry playbook: when faced with a scandal, double down on ambiguity and hope the public doesn’t notice the blatant evasion.  

This isn’t just about AI; it’s about the normalization of bad faith in tech. The fact that this document even exists suggests that the company was aware of the risks but chose to proceed anyway. They knew that using AI to generate content without transparency could undermine public trust, but they calculated that the immediate gains—faster ad creation, lower costs, more engagement—outweighed the long-term damage. It’s a familiar pattern: tech companies treat ethical concerns as afterthoughts, not core principles. If you’ve worked in this space, you’ve seen this cycle before. A new technology emerges, it’s embraced with blind enthusiasm, and then suddenly, when the consequences become too obvious, the company pivots to damage control. The real question isn’t whether this will lead to regulation—though that’s likely—but whether the industry will ever learn to prioritize integrity over convenience.  

The document also revealed that the AI was trained on a dataset that included non-consensual content, some of which was scraped from social media without user knowledge. This isn’t a new issue, but the scale here is staggering. The company didn’t just scrape public data; they actively curated content that aligned with their models’ training goals. This means the AI could have been inadvertently amplifying misinformation or biased narratives, depending on how it was deployed. The engineers who built this system probably knew about the dataset’s flaws but went along with it because the alternative—scraping only ethically sourced data—would have required more time and resources. It’s the same logic that led to Facebook’s data scandals or Google’s ad-targeting controversies. Tech companies have a habit of rationalizing unethical practices as necessary trade-offs.
