# HN Daily Digest - 2026-06-29


JetBrains just dropped a free tier for Kotlin Multiplatform Mobile, and honestly, it's the kind of move that makes you wonder why it took this long for someone to crack the cross-platform mobile puzzle properly. The idea of writing once and deploying everywhere has been the white whale of mobile development for years, but most attempts—from Xamarin to Flutter—have felt like compromises that leave developers longing for greener pastures. JetBrains, with its deep roots in developer tooling and IDE wizardry, is stepping into a space where the stakes are high and the competition is fierce. This isn't just about Kotlin anymore; it's about challenging the duopoly of iOS and Android ecosystems by offering a legitimate alternative that doesn't make developers want to claw their eyes out. The timing is particularly interesting given Apple's recent tightening of App Store policies and Google's ongoing struggles with Android fragmentation. If JetBrains can pull off seamless native integration while keeping the codebase unified, they might just have the golden ticket. But let's not kid ourselves—this is still Kotlin we're talking about, a language that's more of a niche darling than a mainstream champion. Whether it can gain traction outside the Android world remains to be seen, but the ambition alone is worth applauding.

Speaking of ambitious moves, the EU's Digital Markets Act continues to reshape the tech landscape, and today brought fresh drama. Meta's Mark Zuckerberg is reportedly furious about the DMA's restrictions on data sharing between services, which directly impacts WhatsApp and Messenger interoperability. Meanwhile, Apple is under fire for potentially violating the rules with its iMessage encryption policies. These aren't just corporate tantrums; they reflect a broader tension between platform control and regulatory oversight. For years, Big Tech companies have operated with impunity, building walled gardens that lock users into their ecosystems. Now, the EU is essentially saying, "You can't have both monopolistic control and public trust," and it's about time. The irony, of course, is that these same companies are now scrambling to retrofit their products to comply with laws they spent years lobbying against. It's almost poetic.

While the privacy-focused VPN provider's contribution is commendable, it also underscores the stark reality that many open-source projects rely on scraps and corporate benevolence to survive. Contrast that with Mozilla's recent layoffs, which signal a troubling trend where even well-established organizations are struggling to justify their open-source investments. The message here is mixed: there's growing awareness of the importance of projects like Rust for system-level security and performance, but the financial mechanisms to sustain them are still woefully inadequate. For all the talk about "digital sovereignty" and "open-source as a public good," the money just isn't there unless a company decides to randomly throw some crumbs. It's a system that works until it doesn't, and when it fails, we all feel the pain.

The Linux kernel community's decision to remove legacy support for older architectures reminds us that even the most venerable software ecosystems must evolve or die. Dropping support for DEC Alpha, Motorola 68k, and other relics of computing past might seem brutal, but it's a necessary pruning to keep the kernel lean and maintainable. This is the kind of ruthless prioritization that tech companies should emulate but rarely do—imagine if Apple took a similar approach with macOS and finally killed Rosetta 2 after all these years. Speaking of evolution, Intel's latest chip design tweaks for AI workloads show how even hardware giants are pivoting to stay relevant in a world where GPUs dominate the spotlight. These aren't revolutionary changes, but they're a sign that the CPU wars aren't over yet, even if the battlefield has shifted toward specialized AI accelerators.

This pattern isn't unique to Salesforce; it's playing out across the industry as companies try to rebrand themselves as AI-first while quietly shedding the human workforce that built their success. The disconnect between public market realities and private funding optimism is becoming harder to ignore.

Tether's $1 billion investment in a German Bitcoin mining operation feels like a classic move from a company that's basically printing money with no oversight. On one hand, you could argue this is diversification into the future of energy infrastructure. On the other, it's a reminder that stablecoin issuers operate in a regulatory gray zone where decisions can have massive implications for global markets. Is this a legitimate business expansion or just another way for Tether to obscure its actual financial health? Given the company's history, it's probably a bit of both. The broader trend here is the increasing entanglement of traditional finance with cryptocurrency ventures, even as the regulatory framework struggles to keep up. It's chaos, but it's the kind of chaos that creates opportunities—and disasters—for those brave (or foolish) enough to play in that space.
