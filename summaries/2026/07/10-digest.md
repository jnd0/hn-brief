The European Commission has dropped a blunt accusation on Meta, declaring that the addictive design mechanisms woven into Instagram and Facebook violate the EU’s Digital Services Act, a move that frames endless scrolling not as a quirky feature but as a regulatory breach. The press release leans on research that ties engagement‑maximizing algorithms to measurable harm, especially among minors, and signals that the Commission will treat those recommendation engines as core obligations under the DSA rather than optional niceties. In the commentariat the reaction splits along familiar fault lines: one side sees the action as a long‑overdue consumer‑protection win, arguing that network effects and social‑pressure make opting out a hollow choice for most users; the other warns that the move smacks of authoritarian overreach, likening it to the Commission’s controversial chat‑control proposals and warning that moral panics around Big Tech can later be turned into broader surveillance powers. Technically, several users point out that the chronological feed of old—limited to accounts you actually followed—was far less compulsive than today’s AI‑curated stream, and they share practical work‑arounds such as Instagram’s algorithm reset or browser extensions that hide reels. The thread ultimately reveals a deeper tension: whether we trust democratic institutions to curb exploitative design or fear that any concession hands the state a lever to shape information flows at will.

Commenters dive into the weeds, with some proposing back‑of‑the‑envelope math that suggests the true per‑ride expense could be far lower once utilization and overhead are accounted for, while others counter that hidden line items like liability insurance, 24/7 staffing, specialized equipment, and the sheer price of the ambulantes themselves keep the figure realistic. The conversation often drifts to international comparisons, noting that many countries fund EMS through taxation rather than patient billing, and volunteer fire‑EMS models are floated as a possible antidote to the surprise‑bill epidemic that plagues the U.S. system.

Bringing the focus back to the present, the study on parental device use and adolescent attachment introduces the Device Interference for Attachment Security (DAIS) scale, attempting to quantify how caregivers’ phone‑related distraction correlates with teenage feelings of insecurity. The findings show a robust link between reported caregiver distraction and higher attachment‑insecurity scores, though the authors prudently label the relationship correlational and caution against reading causality into a cross‑sectional snapshot. The discussion zeroes in on methodological limits: self‑report instruments administered at a single time point invite shared method variance, and a lurking third variable—such as pre‑existing relationship quality—could easily explain both heightened device‑noticing and attachment anxiety. Nevertheless, many commenters share personal tactics for mitigating phone intrusion, from device‑free dinner tables to deleting algorithmic feeds that pull attention away from face‑to‑face interaction. Moving from software to hardware, the QuadRF review introduces a phased‑array software‑defined radio that passively sniffs RF emissions in the 4.9–6.0 GHz C‑band, allowing it to locate drones transmitting on 5.8 GHz and even visualize Wi‑Fi signals through walls. The device comprises four tiles that can be daisy‑chained, each performing its own phase‑shift calculations after the developers reverse‑engineered the Raspberry Pi 5’s MIPI interface via the RP1 chip. On drone detection, veterans point out that major airports already layer radar, optical, acoustic, and AI classification, yet false positives from birds and balloons persist; Remote ID mandates help but remain spoofable. The QuadRF’s narrow 5.8 GHz coverage leaves it blind to 900 MHz, cellular, and the increasingly common fiber‑tethered drones used in Ukraine to evade jamming, while its limited field of view complicates wide‑area sky scans. Export‑control anxieties surface repeatedly, with speculation that U.S. Munitions List Category XI language could sweep in passive‑radar code, prompting projects like KrakenSDR to excise such features despite legal ambiguity, and many developers self‑censor to stay clear of potential violations.
