Apple’s latest announcement this morning sent shockwaves through the developer community: the company is quietly deprecating Objective-C in favor of Swift for all future iOS, macOS, and watchOS development. While the writing was on the wall, the formal deprecation—set to land in iOS 18—has sparked a mix of resignation and nostalgia among developers who’ve spent decades wrestling with Objective-C’s quirks. The shift is more than symbolic; it marks the final nail in the coffin for a language that once defined a platform but was increasingly seen as a relic. Swift’s rise has been relentless, and Apple’s decision to sunset Objective-C is a clear signal that the company is betting its future on a language that’s faster, safer, and more modern. For those who remember the chaos of managing memory manually and parsing legacy codebases littered with arcs and delegates, this feels less like a loss and more like a relief. But the transition won’t be painless—Apple is reportedly offering tools to auto-convert Objective-C code to Swift, though many developers are skeptical about how robust those tools will be. The timing feels deliberate: with iOS 18 just months away, Apple is setting the stage for a smooth migration while distancing itself from a language that’s become synonymous with older iOS versions. The irony isn’t lost on insiders: Objective-C was once the gatekeeper of iOS development, but now it’s the language that coders are scrambling to leave behind. This isn’t just about code—it’s about control. By forcing developers to adopt Swift, Apple ensures a more homogeneous ecosystem, which makes debugging, optimization, and future innovation far easier. Still, some see a double-edged sword. For open-source projects and legacy systems, the deprecation could create fragmentation, as companies scramble to update dependencies or risk losing access to critical features. The move also raises questions about Apple’s long-term commitment to backward compatibility, a hallmark of macOS development that’s now being quietly rewritten. For now, the message is clear: Swift isn’t just the future—it’s the only future.

This isn’t just a technical achievement; it’s a power play. The tension between innovation and accountability is palpable, and the lack of transparency only deepens the divide between corporate AI labs and the broader research community.

But sources close to the deal claim the transaction was less about technological synergy and more about eliminating a potential rival. CoreWeave’s ability to offer affordable, on-demand GPU instances directly challenged Microsoft’s pricing model, which has long relied on bundling hardware and software. Now that CoreWeave’s assets are being absorbed, the company’s user base is reportedly facing sudden service disruptions and pricing hikes. This isn’t the first time Microsoft has leveraged acquisitions to stifle competition, but the scale here is unprecedented. The move has reignited antitrust concerns, particularly as Microsoft continues to dominate both cloud and AI markets. Regulators in the EU and U.S. are now investigating whether the acquisition violates competition laws, though insiders doubt any meaningful action will follow. The broader implication is clear: consolidation in tech is accelerating, and smaller players are increasingly treated as stepping stones rather than collaborators. For developers and startups reliant on affordable GPU access, the acquisition is a stark reminder that the rules of the game are being rewritten in favor of the already powerful.

This isn’t just a technical problem—it’s a systemic failure rooted in the lack of accountability and funding for open-source maintainers. Many projects rely on volunteer developers who juggle unpaid work with corporate jobs, leading to burnout and inconsistent updates. The Linux Foundation’s proposal to establish a mandatory vulnerability disclosure framework has been met with pushback from the open-source community, with critics arguing that such measures would stifle innovation. The debate underscores a fundamental tension: open-source thrives on openness, but that very openness makes it vulnerable to exploitation. Worse still, companies like Microsoft and Google are quietly funding open-source projects to shape their development, creating a conflict of interest that undermines the neutrality of the ecosystem. The report’s timing couldn’t be worse, as the software supply chain faces increasing scrutiny in the wake of high-profile breaches. For developers, the message is clear: the code we rely on is fragile, and the systems that keep it secure are collapsing under their own weight.  

In a separate development, the EU’s Digital Markets Act (DMA) has forced Apple to open its iOS ecosystem in ways that could fundamentally alter the mobile landscape. Starting next month, Apple will be required to allow third-party app stores and payment systems on iOS devices, dismantling its decades-old walled garden. While the move is framed as a victory for consumer choice, its long-term impact is far more complex. For years, Apple’s tight control over iOS has been a double-edged sword: it ensured security and consistency but also stifled competition and innovation. The DMA’s intervention could disrupt Apple’s revenue model, which heavily relies on the 30% commission charged on in-app purchases. Developers, who have long criticized the company’s fees, are cautiously optimistic, though many remain skeptical about the practical implications. Critics argue that opening iOS will fragment the user experience, leading to a fragmented app ecosystem that’s harder to navigate. Meanwhile, the requirement to allow alternative app stores raises concerns about security, as third-party platforms may not enforce the same rigorous review standards as Apple’s App Store. This is a high-stakes gamble for Apple, which has built its brand on tight integration and control. The DMA’s influence could ripple beyond iOS, forcing other tech giants to rethink their own closed systems—a development that could reshape the entire tech industry.

Meanwhile, the tech industry is quietly bracing for a surge in litigation and cybersecurity measures, as companies rush to patch vulnerabilities and mitigate risks.
