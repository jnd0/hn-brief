# HN Daily Digest - 2026-03-15

Montana's 2025 Right to Compute Act has passed, establishing computation as a fundamental human right and positioning the state as a potential AI/data center hub. The law limits government regulation of computational resources unless demonstrably necessary for public safety or health, mandates risk management protocols for AI-controlled critical infrastructure, and removes explicit shutdown requirements from earlier drafts. Proponents argue it prevents NIMBYism from blocking development, while critics question whether it serves corporate interests more than individual rights. The discussion online reveals deep skepticism about the law's practical effects and whether it addresses any real harms or simply creates new ones. Some users point to the absence of historical context for why computation needs this protection, contrasting it with civil rights legislation that emerged from documented injustices. Others highlight potential environmental impacts, noting data centers' strain on local resources and the tendency for abandoned infrastructure to create long-term problems. The law's vague risk management requirements and potential for regulatory capture by large tech firms remain significant concerns, especially given the lack of community input in its drafting.

The California Age-Appropriate Design Code Act is forcing operating systems to implement age verification mechanisms, sparking debate about government overreach versus reasonable protection of children online. Some argue age verification is a reasonable compromise compared to more invasive methods, while others see it as government intervention into open source software development. The tension extends beyond California, touching on broader questions of parental responsibility in an era when schools and tech companies create environments where traditional control is insufficient. One thread notes that schools often issue devices to students, creating situations where even engaged parents struggle to monitor their children's online activities. The discussion reveals deep divisions about the roles of parents, schools, tech companies, and government in protecting children online, with some arguing for complete parental control while others point out the practical limitations of that approach in a connected world.

AI usage promotions are becoming increasingly sophisticated, with Anthropic offering a March 2026 promotion that doubles Claude usage during off-peak hours. The incentive targets 5AM to 11PM ET outside of US business hours, aims to spread out server load, and avoids counting against weekly usage limits. This follows a December promotion that reportedly encouraged users to upgrade to higher-tier plans. The psychology behind these promotions is fascinating - they create an "abundance mindset" that makes users reluctant to return to limited usage, effectively turning them into more active customers. Time zone confusion emerged as a major theme, with users criticizing the use of ET for a global service and calculating how the promotion would work in different regions. Some predict these off-peak usage incentives will become more common as companies balance server loads, potentially leading to more expensive peak tokens in the future. The broader pattern suggests a shift toward dynamic pricing models in AI services, where usage costs fluctuate based on demand patterns rather than offering flat-rate pricing.

Carr's statement suggests that broadcasters providing "unpatriotic" coverage could face consequences, revealing deep partisan divides over free speech principles. Many commenters note the hypocrisy of "free speech absolutists" who remain silent when the Trump administration threatens media outlets, while others point out that the right's commitment to free speech was always conditional, disappearing when they gained power. The conversation also touches on broader concerns about the erosion of democratic norms, with some comparing current events to pre-authoritarian patterns in other countries. Technical discussions about FCC powers and the distinction between broadcast and internet media emerged, though the dominant theme was political critique of the administration's actions and the apparent abandonment of previously stated principles by conservative voices. The pattern is increasingly clear - government officials leveraging regulatory power to punish dissent, a move that would have been unthinkable in previous administrations and suggests a dangerous trajectory for press freedom.

The debate over non-English programming languages continues with Han, a Korean programming language implemented in Rust that translates standard programming keywords into Korean words. The project aims to make code more readable for Korean speakers by using actual Korean vocabulary rather than transliterations. The creator explored whether Korean's dense Hangul script could make code more compact and efficient for AI processing, though testing showed Korean keywords actually use more tokens due to tokenizer training biases. Several commenters noted that for experienced programmers, learning a handful of English keywords is trivial compared to mastering programming concepts and navigating the English-dominated ecosystem of documentation, libraries, and Stack Overflow. One user shared their experience learning to code before knowing English, finding keywords were never the barrier. Others pointed to existing Korean languages like Nuri and Yaksok that incorporate Korean grammar more deeply, and discussed how Japanese input systems could theoretically support symbol-heavy languages through full-width characters and easy access to mathematical symbols. The consensus seems to be that while such projects are interesting and culturally valuable, the surrounding ecosystem remains the bigger challenge for non-English programming, with English keywords being a minor hurdle compared to the vast infrastructure built around English-language development.

This practice of silently changing application functionality without user knowledge raises concerns about reliability and professional tool usage. The Hacker News debate centered on the ethics and implications of A/B testing in software, especially with LLMs where unexpected behavior changes can be psychologically jarring. While some argued A/B testing is standard for improving products, others emphasized users should be informed and given opt-out options. A key distinction emerged between testing UI elements versus testing actual tool output, with many expressing concern that LLMs already struggle with reproducibility. Technical insights revealed how Claude's plan mode works with multiple explore subagents, and neurodivergent users highlighted potential psychological impacts from unexpected behavior changes. The discussion also touched on whether open source solutions could avoid these issues while achieving comparable performance to proprietary tools, suggesting a potential market for AI tools that prioritize consistency over optimization.

His passing prompts reflection on whether the traditions he represented - rational public discourse, democratic deliberation, and the public sphere - can survive in our current fragmented media landscape.
