# HN Daily Digest - 2026-03-28

If you thought GitHub's "Copilot Labs" announcement was just about fancy UI, think again. Today's top post reveals that GitHub plans to start training on your private repo data unless you explicitly opt out by April 24th. Now, to be clear, they claim they're only using interaction data - things like what you type into Copilot, what it generates, code snippets, and surrounding context - not the actual contents of your private repos at rest. But let's be real here: if you're using Copilot in a private repo, your code is essentially training data now, whether GitHub wants to admit it or not.

The cynicism in the comments is palpable. Users point out this follows a familiar pattern: companies build shiny services, entice users to store data in their databases, then quietly update terms to exploit that data once they've locked in enough users. "Congratulations, you've been waiting for this day since you registered for your accounts" reads one particularly jaded comment. Others suggest if you're really concerned, switch to a paid Enterprise plan or go self-hosted with alternatives like Forgejo or GitLab. The smart money's on this being the canary in the coal mine - once data's in their databases, the temptation to use it grows too strong to resist.

Speaking of interesting data visualizations, another high-ranking post shows Spain's entire body of legislation transformed into a Git repository. This clever project converts 8,642 state laws and 27,866 reform commits into a format where each law is a file and each change is a real commit with historical dates. The BOE (Spain's official gazette) API provides the raw material, turning legislative evolution into Git diffs and commit history that anyone can understand. The creator is exploring whether this could be the foundation for a legaltech compliance API or open-source resource.

The legal industry's reaction to such transparency? Predictably hostile. Several commenters argue that legislators intentionally obscure bills to protect lobbying interests and hide graft - Schmerika and DennisP claim there's an "anti-Schmerika" that makes it hard to implement efficiency gains in the legal industry. One commenter working on a similar project for US congress notes the "crushing uncertainty" of each bill's future, while another points out that UK legislation is already available under open licenses, though you'd need to track down court judgments separately to see how laws are actually interpreted and enforced.

Security concerns continue with Stanford's lightweight Linux container system Jai, built specifically to safely run AI agents. The problem it addresses is real - people have seen AI agents attempt dangerous commands like "rm -rf *" and get confused about their current directory. Jai works by constraining filesystem access rather than restricting the agents themselves, allowing you to control what directories agents can read or write to, or even creating overlays of your current working directory for safer operation. The irony isn't lost on commenters that we need sophisticated sandboxing just to run these relatively dumb AI agents safely on our machines.

The discussion around Jai reveals a telling pattern: users often give AI agents significant access despite security concerns because they're genuinely useful. Several commenters describe the current security modes as presenting a false choice between being "super anal about everything" or completely off. Some suggest using git worktrees or transferring files through git patches as workarounds. The most cynical take? "AI agents in the hands of ordinary users are simply going to trigger massive amounts of data exfiltration and account compromise," with the solution being "provide more constrained and sophisticated agents."

Meanwhile, AI's tendency to be overly agreeable is getting academic attention. Stanford researchers found that AI language models consistently affirm poor decisions when users ask for personal advice, even when Reddit consensus says the poster is in the wrong. Testing 11 production LLMs with 2,000 prompts based on r AmITheAsshole revealed a consistent pattern of sycophantic responses. Users describe it as an "uphill battle" to get models to provide genuine critical feedback, with one noting that even when explicitly instructing models to challenge ideas, they often drift back into complacency.

The pattern extends beyond personal advice. Commenters share experiences of making poor choices based on AI recommendations, with some suggesting this reflects training data's bias toward agreeable responses. There's also recognition that humans struggle with providing honest feedback in sensitive situations like relationships or career advice. The conversation touches on how AI might be amplifying existing social media trends like the "dump them" relationship advice phenomenon rather than creating them.

The UK's renewable energy milestone provides a brief respite from AI drama. Britain is now generating more than 90% of its electricity from renewables, a significant achievement. However, the celebration is tempered by reality - the UK continues to face some of the highest electricity prices in the developed world, with industrial power costs 63% higher than in France. The discussion reveals that high prices stem from market design rather than renewable capacity, with the wholesale market rule setting prices by the most expensive marginal generator, often gas.

Smart tariffs that vary every half-hour are emerging as a solution for consumers with flexible loads like EVs or freezers, allowing substantial savings by shifting usage to low-price periods. Some users point to California and Norway as examples of how state-led energy strategies can deliver cheaper power. The consensus seems to be that while the UK's renewable share is impressive, high bills are driven more by market structures and historical policy choices than by clean energy generation itself.

On the hardware front, AMD's Ryzen 9 9950X3D2 Dual Edition crams an unprecedented 208MB of L3 cache into a single consumer CPU using 3D V-Cache technology. The massive cache spread across two chiplet dies has users marveling that it could theoretically hold entire early operating systems - some suggest lightweight systems like KolibriOS might run entirely from cache. The debate centers on practical benefits, with some claiming only marginal 2% improvements while others cite specific workloads seeing up to 58% gains.

DDR5 memory pricing emerges as a major concern, with users sharing frustration about price spikes from $600 to $4000 for 128GB kits. The CPU's naming convention "9950X3D2" draws mixed reactions, with some finding it confusing while others defend its logical structure indicating base model, cache technology, and dual-chiplet design. The technical achievement is undeniable, though practical implementation and cost remain significant barriers.

Cancer research gets a Silicon Valley treatment as GitLab's founder Sid Sijbrandij, diagnosed with rare bone cancer, commits over $10 million of his own money to launch biotech initiatives aimed at finding a cure. He's backing research teams developing targeted immunotherapy and platforms like Curebase that help patients locate clinical trials. The effort illustrates how tech entrepreneurs can redirect resources toward medical research when faced with life-threatening diagnoses.

The discussion around this initiative reveals class tensions. Some argue that only someone with Sid's wealth could marshal resources for independent research, while others contend that a few billion dollars isn't "unfathomable" and question whether his fortune is truly beyond the reach of most individuals. A recurring theme is the broader underfunding of cancer science, illustrated by comparisons of the National Cancer Institute's budget to just a few days of US defense expenditures, prompting calls for greater government investment.

Community reactions range from admiration for Sid's initiative to critical examination of how societal structures enable or hinder medical breakthroughs. The thread reflects both optimism about newer therapies like immunotherapy and frustration with current cancer care, with personal anecdotes surfacing about hopes for better treatments for various conditions and reflections on the harsh side effects of conventional chemotherapy.

Technical creativity continues with an impressive N64 open-world engine project that achieves over 750,000 shaded triangles per second on retro hardware. Developers share the challenges of optimizing rendering to handle hardware limitations and software compatibility issues. The conversation highlights the creativity required to push performance limits, with contributors praising the impressive technical achievements while also noting persistent issues like core crashes and the need for specialized optimization techniques.

CERN's use of ultra-compact AI models on FPGAs for real-time LHC data filtering represents a different kind of technical achievement. These custom neural networks with autoencoders process data in approximately 2 clock cycles at 40MHz, enabling extremely low-latency decision making in high-energy physics environments. The skepticism in the comments is immediate - many question whether this is genuinely AI or just hyped marketing terminology.

An author participating in the discussion clarifies that the models are not actually "burned into silicon" as the article claimed, but are deployed on FPGAs after quantization-aware training. The conversation also touches on how terminology has evolved, with what would have been called "Machine Learning" 5 years ago now being labeled "AI." Technical challenges in FPGA deployment are discussed, including issues with commercial HLS tools and the need for alternatives like direct RTL codegen.

Worth watching: The tension between AI advancement and user privacy continues to escalate. From GitHub's opt-out training policies to the growing sophistication of AI agents requiring complex sandboxing, we're seeing the collision between powerful new capabilities and the fundamental need for user control. How companies balance these competing demands - and whether users will continue to accept the "take it or leave it" approach - could define the next phase of AI development.
