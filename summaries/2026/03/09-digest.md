# HN Daily Digest - 2026-03-09

Agent Safehouse is causing quite a stir in the macOS security space, with 725 points and 166 comments discussing this native sandboxing tool for local AI agents. The tool generates policies for the `sandbox-exec` utility, allowing you to run AI agents securely without resorting to containers or remote servers. What's interesting is the online policy builder that lets you create security profiles without even installing anything locally - a smart move considering Siwatanejo's valid concern about running remote shell scripts from the internet.

The technical community is having a field day with this one. Atombender pointed out the fundamental limitations: agents can only see files you explicitly give them, and they can't launch other processes. xyzzy_plugh wants to see advanced features like copy-on-write semantics, while kstenerud suggests a commit-based approach using Docker containers. The debate about whether filesystem sandboxing alone is sufficient feels familiar - credentials and network access remain the real vulnerabilities, as ptak_dev noted. tcbrah and andybak are already worrying about long-term capability versus safety tensions, while tl2do observes that improved agent reliability might be reducing immediate risks.

Speaking of security debates, Ireland just shut down its last coal plant at Moneypoint, becoming the 15th European country to end coal power generation. The 915MW facility began operations in 1985, and now Ireland joins Belgium, Sweden, Austria, Portugal, and the UK in this milestone. But the Hacker News discussion reveals the complexity behind these numbers - several Irish commenters argue energy prices have skyrocketed as Ireland transitioned from energy provider to importer, hitting lower-income citizens hardest. The fungible nature of electricity grids means Ireland's grid still depends on energy from neighboring countries that use coal, making the "coal-free" designation feel somewhat performative to those actually paying the bills.

The technical crowd is already debating alternatives - nuclear power, battery storage, and pumped hydro all came up. One commenter pointed out that Europe's high electricity costs are driving manufacturing to Asia, while another noted that China's role in renewable energy is complicated: they're both building coal plants and dominating solar panel production. The mention of Ireland's planned import of nuclear-generated electricity from France through the Celtic Interconnector suggests the country's energy strategy is evolving beyond just eliminating coal.

In more troubling legal news, a US Court of Appeals ruled that companies can update their Terms of Service via email and imply user consent through continued service use. The court found that sending an email notification with a link to updated terms constitutes sufficient notice. The Hacker News community's reaction was predictably cynical - many argued that only basic terms like no hacking, rate limits, and legal compliance should be enforceable. Some users are simply opting out entirely, canceling streaming services, using open-source software, or pirating content to avoid TOS altogether. The contrast with Sweden's legal system, where contracts must have definite provisions that can't be changed, highlights how different legal frameworks handle these issues.

The AI timeline discussion is getting heated as the goalposts for AGI keep moving. OpenAI's charter defines AGI as systems outperforming humans in economically valuable work, but critics argue current LLMs lack true memory and reinforcement learning. The resignation of Kirubakaran from OpenAI over ethical concerns about AI's role in national security - including surveillance and lethal autonomy - has added fuel to these debates. Technical insights highlight LLMs' reliance on statistical prediction rather than genuine reasoning, leading to errors like deleting files or following malicious instructions. The community remains divided between technical realism about current limitations and optimism about future progress.

LibreOffice 26.2's new Markdown support has generated excitement in the document processing world. This update allows local conversion between MS Office documents and Markdown format without uploading sensitive content to third-party websites - addressing a long-requested capability. The philosophical discussion touches on whether LibreOffice missed opportunities by not embracing web-based document formats earlier. There's also debate about how Markdown would function within a WYSIWYG environment, highlighting the tension between traditional document editing paradigms and modern markup approaches.

The homelab community is thriving with BryanAntonio's setup featuring a transition to Linux-based TrueNAS after finding the FreeBSD version lacking. His multi-machine approach - separate hardware for NAS, Proxmox server, and OPNsense routing - reflects the cost-effectiveness of modern hardware and the reduced marginal costs when expanding infrastructure. The discussion centers on managing multiple services on a single machine, with users debating the practicality of mixing desktop and server roles. Key technical insights include using reverse proxies like Nginx or Caddy for port abstraction, DNS configurations for subdomain management, and Tailscale for secure remote access without port forwarding.

There's a significant disagreement between those who run robust setups mixing Plex and security cameras on workstations and those who warn against this approach due to reliability concerns during reboots. Other users share solutions for password manager issues and off-prem backups via friends' NASes, while praising the community's collaborative problem-solving.

The OSINT dashboard project ShadowBroker sparked an interesting discussion about LLM-generated projects on Hacker News. The real-time dashboard aggregates 15 live global feeds, displaying aircraft and maritime traffic on an interactive map using Next.js and FastAPI. However, users quickly encountered a common issue: the dashboard appeared blank due to missing API keys. The author acknowledged poor error handling and promised improvements, but the thread also touched on security after a leaked .env file was discovered in an early commit.

Technical suggestions emerged to improve performance using vector tile libraries like PMTiles and Martin, while the project sparked broader debate about the influx of LLM-generated submissions on HN. Some dismiss these efforts as "plastic cutlery" - low quality but functional - while others defend their practical value. Comparisons to large-scale military OSINT systems highlighted the gap between hobbyist dashboards and enterprise-grade solutions. The author engaged constructively with feedback throughout, showing that even if the code was AI-generated, the development process remained authentically human.

There's also the tension between centralized control and decentralization, visible in both the legal discussion about TOS and the energy debate about Ireland's grid dependencies. The AI timeline discussions reflect broader societal questions about what constitutes genuine intelligence versus clever statistical modeling, while the OSINT dashboard conversation highlights how AI is changing both development processes and community expectations.
