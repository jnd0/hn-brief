In Europe, a significant shift in the digital payments landscape is underway. Some 130 million Europeans are transitioning to Wero, a new EU-wide sovereign payment system designed to reduce dependence on Visa and Mastercard. Wero, essentially an EU-wide version of the successful Dutch iDeal system, will focus initially on peer-to-peer transfers with commerce payments targeted for 2027. The initiative involves banks from Spain, Italy, and Portugal joining what was originally France's Paylib replacement, leveraging SEPA instant transactions to enable cross-border payments as simple as domestic ones. While the technical details suggest this might be merely a thin wrapper around existing bank APIs, improved ergonomics alone could make it valuable.

The discussion around payment systems reveals some interesting tensions. Commenters debate the merits of various approaches, with some praising iDeal as the gold standard for internet payments due to its bank-redirect security model that avoids sharing card details with merchants. However, others note that iDeal lacks features like pre-authorization and has limitations with recurring payments through SEPA Direct Debit. Brazilian PIX receives praise for being even better with more functionality including split payments and financing, though critics point out it lacks the fraud protection and chargeback mechanisms that Visa and Mastercard provide. The geopolitical dimension shouldn't be underestimated either—some view this EU initiative as a response to US payment dominance and concerns about foreign access to transaction data, particularly referencing past incidents where US authorities threatened to use credit card data for border enforcement actions.

While the full details aren't available, this incident highlights the ongoing security challenges in the development ecosystem, particularly with extension marketplaces that struggle to vet code effectively. The attack vector—through an extension rather than a direct repository compromise—demonstrates how sophisticated actors are becoming at targeting developer tools to gain access to valuable code and infrastructure. For organizations, this serves as yet another reminder that security must extend beyond just repository permissions to include the entire toolchain developers rely on.

In typography news, Donald Knuth's 1980 article "The Letter S" explores the mathematical challenges of designing the letter S in typography, devoting nine pages of mathematical formulas to this single character. The article explains that S is uniquely difficult among uppercase letters because it lacks the structural constraints of other characters, having continuously changing curvature with no stabilizing axis or primitive shape. This work was part of Knuth's broader digital typography project that led to the creation of TeX and Metafont, systems he developed to ensure consistent typography for his books. The discussion reveals that S is the only uppercase letter not based on a primary ellipse or combined with straight lines, resulting in complex mathematical definitions—a reminder of how deeply computation has permeated even the most artistic aspects of technology.

Worth watching: The Dutch iDeal payment system continues to serve as an interesting case study in how banking infrastructure can be designed with both security and usability in mind. Unlike credit card systems that expose sensitive information to merchants, iDeal redirects users to their bank's environment for authentication—a model that could inspire future payment innovations. As Europe pushes toward Wero and other alternatives to Visa and Mastercard, the underlying principles behind iDeal's success merit attention for anyone working on financial technology or digital identity systems.
