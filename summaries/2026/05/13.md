## [Deterministic Fully-Static Whole-Binary Translation Without Heuristics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08419)
**Score:** 218 | **Comments:** 52 | **ID:** 48117810

> **Article:** Elevator is a deterministic, fully static whole‑binary translator that rewrites x86‑64 executables into native code without using heuristics or JIT. It sidesteps the code‑versus‑data ambiguity by simultaneously interpreting every byte as both data and potential instruction, building a superset control‑flow graph and resolving indirect jumps via a runtime lookup table. The authors claim the resulting binaries achieve performance on par with or better than QEMU’s user‑mode JIT, and the method works for cooperative binaries while acknowledging limitations such as no support for self‑modifying or JIT‑compiled code. The paper also notes that the approach can be applied to safety‑critical domains where a signable, static translation is required.
>
> **Discussion:** The thread quickly gravitated toward the practical limits of static translation, with several commenters pointing out that Elevator’s superset disassembly still cannot guarantee safety for adversarial binaries and that Rice’s theorem makes a fully general solution impossible. Others highlighted the certification angle, noting that a deterministic, signable binary is valuable for regulated industries that cannot rely on JIT, while some questioned the relevance of such work for mainstream AI disruption. A recurring debate centered on whether the technique could ever handle self‑modifying or JIT‑generated code, with some arguing that modern OS memory protections already sidestep those cases and others insisting that hand‑rolled assembly remains problematic. The performance discussion compared Elevator’s speed to QEMU’s user‑mode JIT, with some users claiming on‑par results for specialized paths but others warning that a 50× expansion in decoded possibilities would be a cache disaster if fully materialized. Finally, the community weighed in on architecture choices, noting that focusing on x86‑64 may be less useful than targeting older 32‑bit workloads, and that the method’s reliance on single‑threaded, exception‑free binaries restricts its immediate applicability.
