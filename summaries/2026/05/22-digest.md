# HN Daily Digest - 2026-05-22

Google's decision to pivot its Antigravity coding tool from an integrated development environment to a command-line interface has left many developers feeling betrayed, and rightfully so. The company's so-called "bait and switch" has replaced a functional IDE with a CLI-focused product that lacks Windows WSL support, fails to persist credentials, and consumes an absurd 4GB of disk space after just four chat sessions. This isn't just a poorly executed product update; it represents a disturbing pattern from Google of abandoning users who've invested time in their ecosystem. The move signals Google's apparent shift away from integrated development environments toward standalone agentic tools, a strategy that disrupts established workflows without providing clear benefits. As one commenter noted, this scattered product strategy reflects deeper organizational issues at Google, where different teams seem to operate with no unified vision, leaving developers to navigate the wreckage.

The conversation ultimately reflected a mixture of technical skepticism about near-term breakthroughs and cautious optimism about eventual safety gains—a tension that seems to define much of the current tech landscape.
