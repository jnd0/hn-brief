# HN Daily Digest - 2026-05-11

The most provocative thing on Hacker News today is someone declaring they're going back to writing code by hand, and the comment section has somehow collapsed into one of the most honest debates about AI coding we've seen in months. The core argument is simple: AI-generated code is only trustworthy if you never read it, and if you do read it, you'll find invariants violated, subtle bugs hidden in plain sight, and a codebase that quietly turns into spaghetti the moment you let your guard down. The author cites a concrete case where 75,000 of 140,000 lines were deleted after the AI finished its job, which is less a productivity win and more a testimony to how cheaply we produce complexity now. What's striking is that the commenters split almost exactly down the middle between the "prompt harder" camp, who argue that pure functions, strict module boundaries, and rapid unit tests can pull high-quality output out of any model, and the "you're fooling yourself" camp, who point out that the moment you spend as much time reviewing AI output as you would have spent writing it yourself, the entire productivity calculus collapses. One commenter puts it bluntly: the AI doesn't understand what it's doing, it just generates plausible code that eventually violates the constraints you explicitly told it to respect. The cognitive debt is real. People are talking about review overhead, invariants that get silently broken, blocking calls dropped on the critical path, and testing requirements cheerfully ignored. The takeaway feels less like a manifesto and more like a warning that we're all about to spend a lot more time debugging hallucinations.

This thread ties neatly into a second wave of stories about AI's actual limitations in production. The piece about running local models on an M4 with 24GB of RAM reveals the same tension from a different angle. People are excited about offline inference, about not depending on cloud APIs, about having your LLM sit on your desk. But the numbers are sobering: Gemma 4.31B shows promise in benchmarks and falls apart on real-world tasks, and the gap between what consumer hardware can run and what frontier models deliver is still enormous. Several commenters argue that the entire local LLM movement is chasing a fantasy until someone ships a model that can reliably handle complex reasoning without a GPU cluster in the back room. Others note that the gap may close, but it's closing slowly, and by the time it does, cloud models will have moved further ahead. There's a recurring theme here that deserves more attention: we keep measuring progress in benchmarks and specs while the actual gap is in reliability, invariants, and edge cases, the same things the hand-coding article was complaining about.

Meanwhile, the Obsidian plugin RAT story is a perfect illustration of why the trust problem extends beyond code generation. Someone built a malicious plugin, users clicked through multiple safety warnings, and a remote access trojan called Phantom Pulse ended up on their machines. The Obsidian CEO's response that it was "social engineering" feels like the software equivalent of saying "the bike wasn't locked, so it's not our fault." Commenters rightly ask why the plugin system doesn't enforce granular permissions, why a plugin can execute arbitrary code without being sandboxed more aggressively, and why the default model is still "trust the user." The conversation drifts into a broader plugin-security debate, with people comparing Obsidian's model to VS Code extensions, Homebrew formulae, and npm packages, all of which have had similar problems. The unspoken consensus is that we've built a world where every application is an extensible platform, and every extensibility point is an attack surface, and we're all pretending that's fine.

The Gmail registration story is its own little dystopia. Google now requires you to scan a QR code and send an SMS just to create a new Gmail account. The discussion splits between people who understand why Google does this, given that it runs free email infrastructure for billions of people under constant spam assault, and people who see it as a deliberate escalation toward mandated digital identity. One commenter notes that the QR code just opens a pre-filled SMS, so it's not automating anything, but that's cold comfort when SIM-swapping exists. The deeper anxiety is about what comes next: if email requires biometric verification, if every account needs to be linked to a phone number that can be cloned, what exactly are we securing? Some users mention switching to Proton or Microsoft as a result, which tells you everything about how much trust remains in Google's ecosystem. The thread also surfaces old wounds about Workspace upgrades that erased storage and locked people out, and about phishing emails that bypass Google's filters entirely, making the whole verification theater feel performative rather than protective.

There's a strong undercurrent of surveillance and power dynamics running through several stories today. The Maryland story about a $2 billion grid upgrade funded by out-of-state AI data centers reads like a textbook case of corporate capture: big money overrides local regulation, residents get stuck with the bill, and the benefits flow elsewhere. Commenters debate whether the upgrades are necessary infrastructure or a subsidy scheme for hyperscalers, with technical arguments about solar pricing, water usage, and the environmental footprint of data centers. But the political dimension is what really stings. One commenter puts it plainly: large corporations shape policy to serve their interests, and the public gets the bill. That sentiment echoes in the Palantir story, where European investors are piling into the company despite its well-documented ties to U.S. intelligence. The discussion frames this as complicity, comparing it to Meta's inclusion in pension funds and questioning whether ethical investment can coexist with surveillance infrastructure. The line between investment and endorsement has never been thinner, and the answer apparently depends on which side of the Atlantic you're standing on.

The James Burke piece about the greatest shot in television is a welcome palate cleanser, though the comments immediately devolve into an argument about whether it's actually one continuous shot or clever editing, which is the kind of pedantry that ruins everything. The broader conversation about whether 1970s documentaries represented a golden age of educational television versus modern YouTube channels is more interesting. Some argue that Burke, Sagan, and Bronowski had something we've lost: a willingness to trust the audience with complexity, to let ideas breathe without algorithmic optimization. Others counter that YouTube democratized access and that nostalgia is doing heavy lifting in these comparisons. The technical detour into rocket launch details, fuel systems, and aspect ratios is exactly the kind of tangent HN does best.

The AI note-taking story about lawyers worrying over privilege waiver is particularly sharp. When an AI transcribes a meeting and turns every offhand comment into a searchable, permanent record, you've effectively weaponized every casual remark. The example of Gemini claiming a payment rollout was for Russia when the conversation was about France is the kind of hallucination that gets dangerous in a courtroom. Commenters mention practical workflows like running transcripts through Claude to flag low-confidence passages, but the core issue remains: the productivity gain of automatic notes is being weighed against the legal risk of discovery. Some suggest this will push companies toward ever-stricter recording policies and self-censorship, which is a cost nobody talks about when evangelizing AI adoption.

The career anxiety stories are hard to ignore. Sean Goedecke's piece arguing that software engineering may no longer be a lifetime career generated over 400 comments, which tells you something about how raw the nerve is. The debate circles around whether the 2-5% of a developer's time spent actually writing code is the valuable part, or whether it's the understanding, the problem formulation, the domain knowledge that makes someone irreplaceable. One commenter warns that "the AI is coming for that too," which lands harder than the author probably intended. The petroleum engineering angle, pairing deep domain expertise with coding skills, feels like the only honest career advice left, though it's also a bit bleak for someone twenty-five years into a pure software career. The PS3 emulator devs politely asking people to stop flooding their repo with AI PRs is the day's most relatable moment: someone built something, and the internet decided it needed GPT-generated patches whether it wanted them or not.

The CUDA-oxide announcement, the cloudflare-canonical drama, and the Hollywood writer training AI piece all share a common thread about who actually controls the tools and the narrative. European money flowing into Palantir, Microsoft Israel's chief leaving amid ethical controversy, and students booing an AI commencement speaker all point to a moment where the hype cycle is meeting something it didn't expect: resistance. Not technical resistance, but moral and political resistance, the kind that doesn't show up in benchmarks or token counts.

Worth watching: the hand-coding debate and the local LLM performance piece together paint a picture of where AI tooling actually stands in 2025, and the Obsidian RAT story is a reminder that the security model for every extensible application is one social engineering click away from disaster.
