## [Disagreement among frontier LLMs on real-world fact-checks](https://lenz.io/research/llm-disagreement)
**Score:** 464 | **Comments:** 321 | **ID:** 48307887

> **Article:** The paper evaluatesfrontier large language models such as GPT‑5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3 Pro, Gemini 3 Pro+Search and Sonar Pro on a four‑label fact‑checking prompt that requires labeling claims as True, Mostly True, Misleading, or False. Across 1,000 recent real‑world user claims, 67 % (with a 95 % confidence interval of 64–70 %) showed at least one model that disagreed with the panel majority, yielding a modest ordinal Krippendorff α of 0.639. The authors note that the dataset consists of claims from the past 180 days with knowledge cutoffs in 2023‑2024, so many statements are beyond the models’ training data and cannot be verified without external search. Critiques focus on the IID assumption, the suitability of ordinal‑α for a four‑class ordered scale, and the forced‑choice design that omits an abstain option.
>
> **Discussion:** Participants debate whether the four‑label rubric captures nuanced disagreement, arguing that a claim can be both true and misleading or mostly false without fitting neatly into a single bucket. Several commenters point out that the lack of explicit definitions for True, Mostly True, Misleading and False makes the evaluation more about prompt interpretation than factual accuracy. Technical critiques highlight concerns about the IID assumption, the use of ordinal‑α on a categorical scale, and the impact of non‑deterministic model outputs when only a single prompt is run per claim. Community members also question the relevance of testing frontier models on recent events that exceed their cutoffs and suggest repeating prompts or adding a consensus baseline to gauge individual variance. Overall the discussion underscores that the study measures panel‑level label inconsistency rather than which model is factually correct, and that broader validation against human references is still pending.
