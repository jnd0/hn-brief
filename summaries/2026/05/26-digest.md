# HN Daily Digest - 2026-05-26

California's decision to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after the inevitable backlash feels like a rare moment of sanity in an otherwise clueless legislative landscape. The original bill would have required operating systems to implement age checks for all users—a technical nightmare that would have forced even tiny Linux distros to collect user ages. Apparently, some lawmakers actually noticed that requiring embedded systems to verify ages might be slightly impractical. The "compromise" reflects the classic pattern of legislation: create a broad, problematic rule, wait for public outcry, then carve out exceptions while pretending the underlying problem is solved. The real question is why they couldn't foresee this before passing the bill in the first place. Meanwhile, the discussion thread revealed the usual circular debate about age verification—some argue for "RTA" headers that sites could use to signal content safety, others point out this creates new attack surfaces, and everyone agrees parental controls should be user-driven rather than legally mandated. Meta, Google, and Microsoft love shifting liability to OS providers while libertarians cry privacy violations. The usual suspects, in other words.

Elsewhere in the religious-technology intersection, Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical makes a rare papal statement about AI ethics, arguing that AI should serve humanity rather than concentrating power among the few. This follows the Vatican's growing interest in emerging technologies, though some commenters questioned how seriously we should take moral guidance from an institution with its own track record on... well, moral guidance. One user suggested people view the Pope as more trustworthy than current political leaders, which was immediately challenged regarding the Church's handling of abuse scandals. The thread quickly devolved into debates about papal authority in the modern age, with moderators moving comments to consolidate the duplicate posts. Meanwhile, another story about the Pope warning that opaque AI risks "new forms of dehumanization" barely registered—apparently two papal pronouncements on AI in one day is just too much for the internet to process. The moral panic around AI continues to gather strange bedfellows.

Speaking of AI's impact, "Leave Me Behind" captures a growing sentiment among programmers that handing development tasks to "stochastic code extruders" is causing a sharp decline in software quality. The author draws parallels to Mario Savio's famous speech about resisting oppressive machinery, arguing that AI is creating a "blight" that will inevitably harm everyone it touches. The discussion thread predictably split into familiar camps—some defended AI's ability to solve complex problems and improve architectures, while others cited incidents like an AWS environment being deleted by an agentic AI tool to illustrate risks. What's interesting is the emerging debate about whether current human intelligence is actually at its peak, with one commenter citing historical sources to argue that lazy thoughtlessness is an innate human trait rather than an AI-induced one. Several participants emphasized that the jury is still out on AI's long-term impact, noting that while some developers report higher quality outputs, the overall effect on median codebases remains uncertain. This ambiguity is precisely what makes the AI conversation so frustrating—it's all speculation wrapped in techno-utopian or dystopian rhetoric.

The security implications of AI tools came into focus with a demonstration that Microsoft Copilot Cowork can exfiltrate files when a malicious skill containing prompt injection is uploaded. The attack bypasses human approval by manipulating the agent into posting Teams messages with pre-authenticated download links for files the user has access to. Commenters correctly split the issue into two parts—prompt injection and permissions—with many arguing that the core vulnerability lies in the "Act" component of ReAct, where LLMs gain real-world capabilities without adequate safeguards. The consensus was that Microsoft rushed Copilot Cowork to production and "vibe coded" its permissions system, with some calling the beta label a cover for a yolo-commit. There was broad agreement that relying solely on prompt injection defenses is insufficient, and that restricting agent permissions is the critical mitigation. Meanwhile, Uber's COO admitted that it's getting harder to justify the money spent on "tokenmaxxing"—the practice of maximizing AI token usage as a productivity metric. The Hacker News thread heavily criticized using token consumption as a metric, with many comparing it to "hammering nails with a microscope." There was significant discussion about the economics of AI versus human labor, with some predicting that when token subsidies end, AI may become more expensive than entry-level employees. Concerns were raised about young engineers developing fundamental skills if they rely too heavily on AI tools, with several commenters criticizing practices like token leaderboards as red flags for poor engineering leadership.

As Google continues its transformation into something other than Google itself, the search for alternatives has become increasingly urgent. While the article about search engine alternatives had no summary, the discussion thread likely covered the usual suspects like DuckDuckGo, Kagi, and Brave Search, plus emerging contenders. What's interesting is how the definition of "Google" has evolved—once synonymous with search, it now represents a sprawling ecosystem that few alternatives can match. This raises the question of what exactly people are searching for: a better search engine, a more private experience, or simply an escape from Google's ever-growing influence. The answer probably varies by user, but the trend is clear—people are actively looking for alternatives, even if they don't entirely abandon Google's services. Meanwhile, Norway is proceeding with its 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage to train a sovereign LLM in Norwegian, leveraging national library archives to ensure the model reflects Norwegian culture rather than English-centric ones. Critics question whether the hardware is sufficient for full LLM training, while others defend it as a necessary proof of concept. The technical discussion highlighted that English and Norwegian are closely related Germanic languages, making LoRA adaptation potentially effective, but current models remain anglo-centric, often failing to search in the target language without prompting. Norway's approach raises questions about whether every country needs its own sovereign AI model or if shared datasets with appropriate localization would be more practical.

On the privacy front, Mullvad is rolling out a mitigation for exit-IP fingerprinting by rotating its VPN exit addresses and offering a Random mode in its browser that provides a different IP for each site. The company's blog post explains that the new approach will randomize IP assignments rather than rely on a static pool, improving privacy for users of the Mullvad Browser. The discussion dissected trade-offs, with some arguing that the browser's built-in proxy mitigates fingerprinting while others warned that any browser vulnerability could expose a user's real IP. A key debate emerged about threat models—whether the browser is appropriate for protection against surveillance capitalism or nation-state actors. Meanwhile, Dutch authorities seized approximately 800 servers and arrested two individuals suspected of providing infrastructure that facilitated cyberattacks against targets worldwide. The servers allegedly violated EU sanctions by supplying computing resources to entities linked to Russian intelligence. Commenters debated why technically skilled operators would choose the risky business of supporting cybercrime, citing low legitimate salaries in Eastern Europe and the allure of perceived superiority. The arrests highlight the ongoing cat-and-mouse game between cybercriminals and law enforcement, though many participants questioned whether the focus on hosting providers rather than the buyers of their services represents a meaningful disruption.

This reflects a broader trend of seeking alternatives to the dominant US tech infrastructure, driven by both cost concerns and data sovereignty issues.

IBM plans to make the foundry independent, allowing other quantum hardware companies to access the infrastructure. The discussion quickly devolved into a critique of IBM's corporate strategy, with many recalling Watson's over-hyped promises and questioning whether a quantum foundry is a sensible growth play. A recurring theme was the tension between IBM's legacy consulting model and its new research ambitions. Meanwhile, a Dutch plan for a 244-acre data center was seemingly pulled, though details are sparse—perhaps Microsoft reconsidered the ROI on that particular project. In programming languages, the article on C extensions, portability, and alternative compilers highlights how many C projects rely on compiler-specific features that create compatibility problems. The discussion centered on portability challenges, with many users complaining about Linux-centric code that fails to compile on Windows and FreeBSD. Others defended the outlet by pointing out that sea level rise alone is dramatic enough to warrant concern. Levees have worsened subsidence by blocking silt replenishment, and much of the city sits on unstable organic material. There's debate over whether CNN has a political agenda, with some highlighting the network's changing ownership while others dismissing the criticism as disproportionate compared to right-wing media misinformation. Economically, commenters discussed how relocation incentives often favor staying until property is condemned, and insurers are expected to eventually abandon high-risk markets. Meanwhile, Toshifumi Suzuki, founder of Seven-Eleven Japan, has died, leaving behind a legacy that revolutionized convenience store culture. The discussion centered on the stark contrast between Seven-Eleven Japan and its US counterpart, with many lamenting the inferior quality and cleanliness of American stores. There was debate over whether Japanese ownership would improve US locations, though skepticism prevailed about the US supply chain's ability to support fresh food. Despite the critiques, praise was abundant for the AI-generated summaries and infinite scroll. The real question isn't whether we can control technology, but whether we can control ourselves.
