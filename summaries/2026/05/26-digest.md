# HN Daily Digest - 2026-05-26


The Eternal Sloptember. George Hotz, ever the provocateur, has coined a term that’s sticking like chewing gum on a hot sidewalk. In his latest essay, he argues that today’s large language models, for all their syntactic fireworks, are fundamentally incapable of genuine software engineering. They don’t *code*; they extrude statistically probable token sequences that happen to compile, producing what Hotz dubs “sloptember”—a perpetual September of sloppy, conceptually bankrupt code. The models are, in his view, hitting the wrong target perfectly: they mimic the surface form of programming without understanding the underlying problem. The discussion that followed was a familiar tech-culture war, with the usual binary trenches. On one side, the “AI pilled” insist that LLMs already complete 80-95% of tasks effectively, pointing to incremental productivity gains. On the other, skeptics like Hotz warn that we’re building a generation of systems on a foundation of plausible-looking gibberish, a critique sharpened by historical context—the original Luddites, they note, weren’t anti-technology but against the fraudulent manufacture of inferior goods and the bypassing of labor standards. The core technical insight, however, cuts through the hype: AI-generated code is often syntactically pristine but conceptually misaligned, its quality utterly dependent on the human guiding the prompt. The debate frequently devolves into comparing this moment to the crypto craze, with some seeing similar patterns of speculative fervor, while others insist the utility is fundamentally different. Yet the most telling comment might be the one calling for concrete examples of failure rather than general criticism, because without those, “sloptember” remains a potent meme but a hard case to prove.

If Hotz’s thesis is that AI is flooding the world with subtly broken code, a parallel narrative emerged from “Leave Me Behind.” This piece argues that the generative AI revolution in software development is actively harmful, creating user-hostile products and declining quality that “surpasses even pre-existing issues like human error and perverse financial incentives.” The author’s contempt for “stochastic code extruders” is palpable, reducing them to mere “next-token predictors” devoid of intelligence or genuine creation. The comment thread was a deeper, more philosophical trench fight. Some, like aspenmartin, pushed back hard, citing AI’s ability to solve complex mathematical conjectures (Erdős problems) and arguing that the median human programmer often produces worse code. Others, like hansmayer, fired back with real-world catastrophes like the AWS incident as proof that “agentic code” is inherently inferior and actively damaging. A fascinating secondary thread questioned the very premise of human intellectual supremacy, invoking the Flynn effect and contrasting ancient philosophers with modern “streamies” to argue that collective laziness and thoughtlessness are timeless human traits, not a new AI-driven phenomenon. The fundamental disagreement hinges on whether AI is a tool for elevation or an “inevitable” blight that demotivates innovation and degrades the craft for everyone. It’s a microcosm of the industry’s anxiety: are we being replaced by a superior force, or are we being encouraged to lower our standards to match a mediocre automation?

The ethical dimension of this automation wasn’t lost on the Vatican. Pope Leo XIV and subsequent statements from Pope Leo (perhaps a different iteration or a title) warned that opaque AI run by a few powerful firms risks creating “new forms of dehumanization.” It’s a moral framing that contrasts sharply with Silicon Valley’s usual “move fast and break things” ethos. While the tech industry often pays lip service to “AI alignment,” the Pope’s concern is less about paperclip maximizers and more about the concentration of power and the erosion of human dignity through algorithmic management and surveillance. It’s a critique that resonates beyond religious circles, touching on the very real societal risks of unaccountable systems making life-altering decisions. Whether this perspective gains traction among the engineering priesthood remains to be seen, but it provides a useful counterweight to the relentless techno-optimism.

From abstract philosophical risks to the concrete, soul-crushing reality of enterprise software: Jira is Turing-complete. An article proved it by implementing a Turing machine within Jira’s workflow engine, using issue transitions, custom fields, and validators to simulate a tape and state machine. It’s a brilliant piece of hacker ingenuity that simultaneously serves as a devastating indictment of Jira’s complexity. The discussion confirmed every developer’s darkest suspicions: every Jira instance is a unique “fractal shit snowflake” of historical customizations, making automation a nightmare of undocumented edge cases. The API is powerful, but the perceived organizational inertia means initiative is often punished, not rewarded. Stories abounded of developers writing scripts to make Jira work for them, sometimes even to cover their own mistakes. The thread inevitably veered into comparisons with alternatives like Phabricator/Phorge and Bugzilla, and a lengthy, universal complaint about the awfulness of Microsoft’s Outlook and Teams—a reminder that the tools of knowledge work are, for the most part, uniformly terrible. The perverse genius is that a system designed for project tracking has become so flexible it can perform any computation; the tragedy is that it’s often used to track the progress of those very computations.

Speaking of large, complex systems, Microsoft has pulled the plug on a planned 244-acre data center in Caledonia, Wisconsin. The project faced fierce local opposition, with over 2,000 residents signing a petition. The proposed site, on farmland near homes and a power plant, became a flashpoint for tensions between tech-driven economic development and community character. While Microsoft vaguely noted it remains interested in Southeast Wisconsin, villagers complained about a lack of formal notification and hoped for earlier engagement in the future. Residents expressed relief, wanting to collaborate on a more suitable site. The discussion around this story branched into several insightful tangents. One thread analyzed the procedural hurdles for such developments, noting that lengthy permitting disadvantages small businesses while favoring large corporations that can self-finance land holdings. Another debated Cloudflare’s blocking practices, with arguments over whether it protects small sites from DDoS or breaks the web for legitimate users. A technical point clarified that 244 acres is enormous for a single data center (typical facilities are 5-10 acres), with the land encompassing support infrastructure. Finally, a debate on environmental justice and hypocrisy emerged: some saw wealthy residents using environmental language to preserve cheap coal power for themselves, while others argued data centers provide substantial tax revenue and their water usage from Lake Michigan is negligible relative to the lake’s volume and regulated withdrawal limits. It’s a classic “not in my backyard” (NIMBY) scenario, but with the added complexity of 21st-century digital infrastructure.

Privacy and security stories this cycle revolve around the cat-and-mouse game between anonymity providers and de-anonymization efforts. Mullvad, the Swedish VPN provider, announced a mitigation rollout to shut down all publicly listed exit-IP servers, replacing them with unlisted IPs to prevent exit-node fingerprinting. The move, tied to a broader industry concern, aims to stop third parties from linking users to the service via static exit addresses. The discussion debated whether this was a response to a recent U.S. congressional warning (though Mullvad is Swedish) or just a necessary technical fix. Arguments surfaced about how VPNs source exit IPs—most use grey-market residential IPs rather than retail ISP links—and the relative safety of Mullvad’s browser with built-in proxy versus a separate OS-level VPN. Meanwhile, in the Netherlands, authorities seized 800 servers and arrested two individuals for providing infrastructure that enabled cyberattacks on EU-sanctioned entities. The operation targeted a hosting provider that had inherited equipment from a sanctioned company, highlighting enforcement of sanctions law. Commenters quickly turned this into a broader debate about why skilled individuals choose cybercrime over legitimate work, citing financial incentives, low penalties in some regions, and the psychological thrill of outwitting law enforcement. Skepticism emerged about the legitimacy of the hosting companies involved, with one user insisting they were fronts for Russian intelligence, challenging others to provide evidence. There was also frustration about the lack of transparency in prosecutions and whether the seizure truly disrupted operations or just displaced them. These stories underscore a fundamental tension: the infrastructure of the internet is both a tool for liberation and a vector for control, and the line between the two is increasingly blurred.

Regulatory pressures are mounting on multiple fronts. California moved to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after significant backlash, a small but symbolic win for open-source pragmatism over broad-brush legislation. Meanwhile, the bootstrapper’s guide to an EU-based tech stack under €10 per month highlighted the growing desire for GDPR-compliant, jurisdictionally safer alternatives to U.S. cloud giants. The list—Hetzner, Scaleway, Brevo, Mollie, UpDown.io—paints a picture of a fragmented but viable European ecosystem, though some commenters noted the irony that many of these services still rely on U.S. hyperscalers underneath. The thread debated the practicality and the underlying sentiment: is this about privacy, or about a burgeoning European techno-nationalism? On the compliance front, the 2026 HIPAA security rule update sparked criticism, particularly around outdated encryption requirements and the general burden of compliance, which many see as a game of catch-up with evolving threats. These stories collectively show a tech landscape increasingly shaped by legal and regulatory guardrails, a far cry from the early internet’s Wild West.

In the realm of speculative but high-stakes tech, IBM is spinning off its quantum chip foundry as a standalone entity with a $2 billion investment from the CHIPS Act. The move aims to provide shared infrastructure for other quantum hardware companies, producing 300mm superconducting silicon chips and developing custom ASICS. The timeline suggests convergence around 2029, with systems consuming up to 3 megawatts of power. The discussion was heavily skeptical, colored by IBM’s history with Watson’s overhyped failures and its consultative business model. Commenters pointed out that Shor’s algorithm requires thousands of entangled qubits, making current chips unlikely to threaten cryptography anytime soon. There was debate over whether there’s even agreement on what constitutes a quantum chip or real applications warranting such foundry volume. Some praised the pragmatic use of existing semiconductor processes, while others noted European competitors like QuantWare raising funds for industrial-scale quantum processors. The thread, while including off-topic humor, underscored deep doubts about quantum computing’s near-term utility and concerns about public funds supporting speculative research. It’s a familiar pattern: a big, ambitious announcement met with a healthy dose of engineer cynicism.

Norway, however, is betting big on a different kind of sovereign tech project. The National Library plans to train a large language model using 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage and an HPE Cray Supercomputing EX system, boasting 448 GPUs and 64,512 CPU cores. The goal is to create an AI that reflects Norwegian language, culture, and history, addressing the perceived inadequacy of globally trained, English-centric models. Critics question the feasibility given the limited hardware and suggest that curating and sharing high-quality Norwegian datasets with foreign model builders would be more cost-effective. The linguistic note that English and Norwegian are closely related Germanic languages, facilitating cross-linguistic transfer via methods like LoRA, adds a technical nuance to the debate. There’s also broader skepticism about whether most countries can afford or politically justify sovereign LLMs, though Norway’s vast sovereign wealth fund gives it a unique advantage if AI proves transformative. This story highlights the geopolitical dimension of AI development, with nations seeking to avoid cultural and linguistic homogenization by Silicon Valley giants.

Climate change made an appearance not as a tech solution but as a human crisis. An article argued that rising seas will swallow New Orleans and that people need to start relocating now. The discussion was predictably bleak, touching on the logistical, financial, and emotional nightmare of climate-induced migration, and drawing parallels to other vulnerable global cities. It’s a stark reminder that while we debate the ethics of AI and the efficiency of our code, physical infrastructure and entire communities face existential threats from a warming planet.

The tech world also paused to note the passing of Toshifumi Suzuki, founder of Seven-Eleven Japan. Suzuki revolutionized Japanese retail with franchising, just-in-time inventory, and centralized POS systems, turning 7-Eleven into a cultural institution famed for high-quality prepared foods and community space—a stark contrast to its American counterpart. The discussion that followed delved into the vast operational differences between 7-Eleven Japan and the U.S., the acquisition history, and the decline of the Speedway brand under Japanese management. Side threads touched on Japan’s payment evolution from cash to PayPay and the technical hurdles for foreign phones using Felica-based Suica payments. It was a moment of reflection on how business models and cultural context shape technology’s adoption and impact.

Uber’s COO stated it’s getting harder to justify money spent on “tokenmaxxing”—a jab at the crypto and Web3 hype that once gripped the tech industry. The comment sparked debate over the tension between centralized control and creative autonomy, with references to Meta’s layoffs. While some defended crypto’s innovative potential, the overall sentiment aligned with a post-hype skepticism, viewing much of the token-based innovation as a costly distraction from core business.

On a more technical note, an article criticized the pervasive reliance on compiler-specific extensions in C projects, which breaks portability with alternative compilers like Clang or TinyCC. The piece argued for checking for attribute functionality rather than compiler macros and cited projects like slimcc that implement full GCC-compatible headers. The discussion resonated with developers who encounter code that compiles only on Linux due to GCC extensions, leaving Windows and FreeBSD users stranded. It highlighted the ongoing challenge of maintaining portable code in a world where Linux is often the default development environment, and the need for explicit compiler detection and testing across OSes.

So, what patterns emerge from this mosaic? A deep, simmering skepticism toward grand technological narratives—whether it’s AI’s coding prowess, quantum computing’s timeline, or crypto’s utility—is the dominant theme. This isn’t Luddism; it’s the hard-nosed pragmatism of engineers who have seen hype cycles come and go, leaving technical debt and broken promises in their wake. We’re also seeing the collision of digital innovation with regulatory, legal, and physical-world constraints: age-verification laws, data center siting, HIPAA updates, and climate migration. The tools we build and the infrastructure we deploy are no longer just technical problems; they are social, political, and environmental ones. The insider conversation has shifted from “can we build it?” to “should we build it, and what are the consequences?” That’s a healthy, if uncomfortable, evolution.

Worth watching: the interplay between AI’s rapid advancement and the growing body of critical literature like Hotz’s “sloptember” essay. Will the industry heed these warnings and prioritize robustness and alignment, or will the pressure for productivity gains override caution? The answer will shape software quality for a generation. Also, monitor the sovereign tech movements in Europe and places like Norway—they could Balkanize the internet further or create viable alternatives to U.S. hegemony. Finally, keep an eye on quantum computing’s so-called “NISQ” era; if IBM’s foundry spin-off yields tangible progress, the hype might finally meet reality, but if not, it risks becoming another shelved mega-project. The common thread is that the stakes are getting higher, and the margin for error is shrinking. The code we write, the laws we pass, and the infrastructure we build today will have lasting, irreversible impacts. That’s a lot of responsibility for a profession that still can’t standardize on a decent issue-tracking system.
