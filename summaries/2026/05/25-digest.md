If you thought AI agents were ready to take over your backend, a new arXiv paper titled "Constraint Decay: The Fragility of LLM Agents in Back End Code Generation" might sober you up. The authors put frontier models—including a GPT‑5.2—through their paces on backend code generation tasks that include explicit architectural constraints. The result? A "constraint decay" effect where success rates plummet as more non‑functional rules (think design patterns, style guides, security policies) are added. Even the most advanced models barely qualify as production‑ready for agentic programming. The discussion thread echoed these findings, with many commenters noting that LLMs excel at "copy‑cat" generation but fall apart when forced to juggle complex, multi‑objective specifications. Some pinned the decay on prompt length and context‑window limits, making it hard for agents to keep all the rules in mind over long sessions. There was a split between optimists who believe stronger models and higher reasoning budgets will erase these weaknesses, and skeptics who've seen these issues persist in real‑world projects. The consensus? A robust harness or tool‑calling loop, plus concrete code examples, can mitigate but not eliminate the problem. So, for now, treat these agents as enthusiastic interns—great for prototyping, but don't trust them with your mission‑critical codebase just yet.
