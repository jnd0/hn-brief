> **Article:** The article describes an issue where Claude Code, Anthropic's AI coding assistant, confuses who said what in conversations, leading to potentially dangerous situations. The author reports instances where Claude incorrectly attributes its own statements to the user, such as claiming the user said "deploy" when Claude itself made that suggestion, or attributing a "tear down" command to the user. This problem appears to stem from Claude Code's approach of leaving it up to the LLM to determine message sources rather than maintaining a deterministic boundary between data and control paths.
>
> **Discussion:** The Hacker News discussion reveals this is a fundamental limitation of LLMs rather than a bug specific to Claude Code. Commenters explain that LLMs treat all tokens probabilistically without architectural boundaries between data and control paths, making speaker attribution unreliable as context windows grow. Some compare this to SQL injection vulnerabilities, while others note it's more like social engineering - an inherent feature rather than a bug. The community debates whether colored tokens or separate modalities could solve this, but most agree the probabilistic nature of LLMs means such issues can't be fully eliminated. Several commenters share similar experiences with other AI systems like ChatGPT and Gemini, noting that negative instructions (like "don't deploy") are particularly problematic. The consensus is that developers must treat LLM outputs as untrusted and implement safeguards rather than relying on the model's accuracy.
