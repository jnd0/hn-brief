# HN Daily Digest - 2026-04-11

NASA's Artemis II mission successfully splashed down in the Pacific Ocean after a 24-hour lunar fly-by, marking a significant milestone for the Orion capsule and Space Launch System. The CBS News article reporting the splashdown couldn't help but note that NASA's Office of Inspector General has set an acceptable crew mortality rate of 1 in 30 for Artemis missions - roughly three times the risk tolerance of the Space Shuttle era. Because nothing says "progress" like making space travel three times deadlier than a vehicle that already had a 1.5% failure rate.

The discussion that erupted was predictably divided between people who think this is an acceptable risk for pushing humanity forward and those who wonder why NASA seems to be regressing on safety. Some pointed out that Apollo had its own share of deadly incidents during training, while others noted that the Space Shuttle was a known death trap that kept flying anyway. The technical explanation for the water landing - that Orion's 25,000 mph reentry velocity makes a winged landing impractical - was met with the expected mix of awe and "why didn't they just design it differently in the first place?"

Meanwhile, in the world of AI security, Anthropic's Mythos model managed to find a critical vulnerability in OpenBSD after burning through $20,000 in computational costs. The system automated vulnerability detection by iterating through codebases, though critics were quick to point out that this is only impressive if you ignore the false positive rate and the fact that a skilled human could probably have found the same vulnerability while Mythos was still warming up its GPUs.

The conversation around Mythos was particularly entertaining because it perfectly encapsulated the current state of AI hype. Some people were practically genuflecting before the altar of automation, while others were asking the obvious question: how many false positives did it generate for every real vulnerability? The answer, predictably, was "not disclosed" - because nothing says "rigorous security research" like hiding your error rates. The real takeaway seems to be that the "harness" or system design matters more than the model size itself, which is a polite way of saying that good engineering beats bigger models every time.

The paper on exploiting AI agent benchmarks dropped with the subtlety of a nuclear bomb, demonstrating how these supposedly rigorous evaluation tools can be gamed to achieve near-perfect scores without actually solving anything. Methods included sending empty inputs and trojanizing binaries - because apparently the people designing these benchmarks didn't consider that an AI might try to cheat. The paper specifically called out benchmarks like Terminal-Bench and SWE-bench as particularly vulnerable to this kind of manipulation, which is both hilarious and deeply concerning if you care about actually measuring AI progress rather than just generating impressive-looking graphs.

The Hacker News discussion around this paper was a master class in tech industry cognitive dissonance. On one hand, you had people who were genuinely shocked that benchmarks could be manipulated - as if anyone who's ever worked in tech hasn't seen this movie before. On the other hand, you had OpenAI representatives defending their existing measures like blocklists and manual reviews as if those weren't just additional attack surfaces waiting to be exploited. The underlying theme was that benchmarks are often more about marketing than actual measurement, which will surprise absolutely no one who's been in the industry for more than five minutes.

France decided to ditch Windows for Linux, citing US tech as a "strategic risk." The article itself is behind a paywall, but the discussion was predictably entertaining. The usual suspects argued about whether Linux on the desktop is ready for prime time, while others pointed out that France has been trying this since the 90s with roughly the same level of success as every other government IT modernization project. The strategic risk argument is probably the most compelling angle - when your national security depends on software from a company that might be subject to US government pressure, having a local alternative starts to look pretty attractive.

The Presidential Pardon Database project went live, offering searchable records of US pardons going back through multiple administrations. The timing was impeccable, given the recent batch of controversial pardons that had everyone from legal scholars to political pundits arguing about the limits of executive power. The database allows users to query preemptive pardons and compare them to historic examples like Nixon's blanket pardon, which is particularly relevant given the current administration's fondness for issuing pardons that seem designed to benefit specific individuals rather than serve any broader justice purpose.

The discussion around the pardon database quickly devolved into the expected political trench warfare, with some participants arguing that preemptive pardons should be prohibited entirely while others defended them as necessary tools for protecting people from politically motivated prosecutions. The Karen Read case came up as an example of how prosecutorial overreach can lead to situations where pardons might be justified, though others countered that this was exactly the kind of argument that gets used to justify pardons for people who are clearly guilty. The technical aspects of the site - like its ability to analyze patterns in pardon issuance across administrations - were largely overshadowed by the political implications.

South Korea introduced a universal basic mobile data access plan providing over seven million subscribers with unlimited downloads at 400 kbps after their data allowances expire. The scheme aims to ensure internet access for all citizens, recognizing the essential nature of connectivity in daily life. This is either a brilliant social policy or a cynical way to ensure everyone has a smartphone and can be tracked and monetized, depending on your level of optimism.

The Hacker News community was largely positive about South Korea's initiative, with many noting how essential connectivity has become for daily life. However, several commenters raised the obvious point that 400 kbps is barely enough for anything beyond basic web browsing and text-based applications. Some pointed out that while this might be sufficient for reading documents or doing basic AI interactions, it's completely inadequate for downloading government forms or uploading signed documents - which are exactly the kind of tasks you'd expect people to need to do when accessing government services online.

The future of everything is apparently lies, at least according to one particularly pessimistic article that argued AI is amplifying manipulation and inequality while prioritizing profit over trust and transparency. The piece reflected the growing divide between those who see AI as a potential utopian force and those who warn of its darker implications. Technical developments like faster inference and new model versions were discussed as both promising and unsettling, with the overall message being that we need more thoughtful governance of AI development.

The conversation around this article perfectly captured the current state of AI discourse - a mix of legitimate concerns about manipulation and inequality, technical optimism about what the technology can achieve, and a healthy dose of skepticism about whether any of the current solutions will actually address the fundamental problems. Some participants called for regulation, while others argued that community trust could replace institutional safeguards. The technical advancements were noted, but concerns about access and fairness persisted. It was the kind of discussion that makes you want to both build amazing things with AI and also move to a cabin in the woods with no internet connection.

Cirrus Labs announced it's joining OpenAI in what appears to be a talent acquisition rather than a product purchase. As part of the transition, the company said its Cirrus CI service will shut down effective June 1, 2026, while its SaaS runners will wind down over time. In the near term, Cirrus Labs will relicense its source-available tools - Tart, Vetu, and Orchard - under a more permissive license and stop charging licensing fees for them.

The discussion around this acquisition was particularly interesting because it highlighted the ongoing tension between using third-party services and maintaining control over your infrastructure. Some users expressed frustration over the short notice given to customers, questioning the responsibility of service providers to give ample migration time. Others pointed out that the Cirrus CLI makes moving task definitions to other CI systems relatively easy and that many users had already migrated to GitHub Actions. The decision to relicense the tools under a more permissive license was widely praised, with some hoping it will spur community development. But the underlying theme was the same one that comes up every time a popular service gets acquired and shut down: if you're not paying for the product, you are the product, and products get discontinued.

Advanced Mac Substitute, an API-level reimplementation of 1980s-era Mac OS, hit a snag when it failed to build due to an unimplemented OpenDF system call. The project aims to recreate classic Mac toolbox calls at the trap level without emulating the original hardware, which is simultaneously brilliant and completely insane. The build failure was traced to a missing implementation of a System 7 API that many legacy programs expected to be present.

The conversation around this project was a delightful mix of technical nostalgia and practical concerns. Commenters quickly identified the build failure and suggested solutions, while also reminiscing about the historical reliance on Pascal calling conventions and how early Mac toolbox functions were accessed via trap instructions. Some participants drew parallels to other reimplementation projects like ARDI Executor, while others debated the performance implications of API-level reimplementations versus full emulation. The discussion touched on everything from floppy-disk swapping and hardware timing to the broader lesson that binary compatibility often hinges on subtle implementation details rather than documented specifications. It was the kind of deeply technical conversation that makes you appreciate just how much complexity lurks beneath even the simplest-seeming pieces of software.

Fluorographane storage claims to achieve 447 TB/cm² density with zero retention energy using a reversible C-F pyramidal inversion barrier verified by computational chemistry methods. The paper proposes a tiered architecture where atomic-force-microscopy probes handle single-atom reads and writes while a speculative MEMS array uses femtosecond IR pulses for sub-nanometer addressing. The author, a self-affiliated researcher with three PhDs, argues that the medium could reach read/write speeds of 25 PB/s using chemical vapor deposition fabrication.

The response to this paper was about what you'd expect when someone claims to have solved storage density and energy efficiency simultaneously while also proposing read speeds that would make current SSDs look like floppy disks. Critics immediately questioned the feasibility of the Tier 2 readout, calling it "physically impossible" and pointing out the lack of concrete experimental validation beyond the AFM proof-of-concept. Supporters defended the caching claim as standard practice in scanning probe systems and emphasized that the core contribution is the computed transition state rather than the speculative architecture. The debate highlighted a broader tension in the community between enthusiasm for exotic high-density storage and skepticism about practical read/write speeds, manufacturing challenges, and energy costs. Despite the polarized responses, a few participants acknowledged that the underlying chemistry could be a genuine physical effect worth exploring further, even if the proposed memory system remains speculative.

Twenty years of AWS experience apparently teaches you that no matter how long you've been around, you're still just another cog in Amazon's machine. Colin Percival, creator of Tarsnap and long-time FreeBSD EC2 maintainer, reflected on receiving sponsorship from Amazon via GitHub Sponsors for 10 hours per week for a year in 2024 after asking an Amazonian for funding support to continue his FreeBSD EC2 work. The post covered his long relationship with AWS, including the early days when Jeff Barr hosted AWS meetups in Second Life.

The discussion around this post centered on the asymmetric relationship between open source developers and hyperscalers, with many criticizing the way companies like Amazon benefit from free R&D while offering minimal support to the maintainers who make their platforms viable. Some noted they withhold private research to avoid providing free R&D to Amazon, while others debated the merits of Business Source Licenses as a protective measure. The technical subthread about IAM roles for EC2 and IMDS security was particularly interesting, with Percival clarifying that he suggested using XenStore to pass credentials to the kernel rather than the HTTP endpoint. The conversation also touched on engineering compensation, with some noting that $120/hour gets excellent developers in German-speaking EU versus much higher US rates, though others argued that top-tier talent like Percival commands premium salaries comparable to professional athletes.

Dark Castle, the 1986 Macintosh platform game created by Jonathan Gay (later the founder of FutureSplash/Flash), is now re-released on Steam as Return to Dark Castle with expanded levels. The original download links are dead, but archived versions can be accessed via the Internet Archive and played through emulators such as Mini vMac or in-browser via ClassicReload. Community members discussed the game's legacy, copyright status, and technical hurdles, including a claim of a 40th-anniversary edition in development.

The conversation around Dark Castle was pure nostalgia fuel, with commenters reminiscing about the game's iconic "Yeah!" catchphrase and the sound of monsters. Several participants clarified that the original Dark Castle was developed by Jonathan Gay, who later created FutureSplash and Flash, and that a modern remake titled Return to Dark Castle now exists on Steam. The conversation turned technical as users shared solutions such as Mini vMac, archived disk images, and browser ports on ClassicReload, while noting that input sometimes fails on those emulators. A recurring theme was frustration over dead download links and the need for proper copyright clarification, with one user pointing out that the original creator's identity and ownership remain contested. The thread sparked broader nostalgia for early Macintosh titles, with users praising the game's artistry and questioning the feasibility of ports to retro handhelds or other platforms.

The Orange Pi 6 Plus review highlighted a single-board computer built around a 12-core RK3588 ARM processor, featuring dual 5 GbE ports, Wi-Fi 6, USB-C 3.2 Gen2, and optional UEFI support via TFA. It packs impressive hardware specs, including up to 32 GB RAM and a PCIe-based network subsystem, but the SoC still relies on a vendor-specific kernel for full functionality. The author compared it to the Raspberry Pi 5, emphasizing the Orange Pi's superior raw performance while acknowledging the software ecosystem lag.

The discussion around this board perfectly captured the eternal struggle of single-board computer enthusiasts: amazing hardware held back by terrible software support. Commenters quickly zeroed in on the chronic software-support gap, with many arguing that despite the Orange Pi's powerful hardware, the need for custom kernels makes it "largely useless" for most users, pushing them back to Raspberry Pi or x86 mini-PCs. A side debate emerged over the viability of SBCs for general-purpose computing, where some questioned whether anyone actually used them that way, while others lamented the missed opportunity for democratized compute if mainline support improved. Technical contributors discussed possible workarounds, mentioning UEFI ports and the challenges of upstreaming drivers for the RK3588's PCIe-based peripherals. The conversation also drifted into related topics like NPU usefulness, power-bank reliability, and the broader trend of SBCs becoming niche development tools rather than desktop replacements. Overall, the thread reflected a split between hardware enthusiasm and pragmatic concerns over software maturity.

Worth watching: the ongoing tension between AI capability and practical utility, as demonstrated by both the benchmark exploitation paper and the Mythos vulnerability discovery. When your evaluation methods can be gamed and your automated security tools have undisclosed error rates, it's hard to know whether we're making progress or just getting better at optimizing for the wrong metrics.
