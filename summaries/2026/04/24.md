## [Show HN: Honker – Postgres NOTIFY/LISTEN Semantics for SQLite](https://github.com/russellromney/honker)
**Score:** 222 | **Comments:** 51 | **ID:** 47874647

> **Project:** Honker is a self-contained extension that brings Postgres-style NOTIFY/LISTEN semantics to SQLite by watching the WAL file with stat(2) polling, enabling push-style, cross-process event delivery with single-digit millisecond latency and no external daemon. It bundles ephemeral pub/sub, durable work queues with retries and dead-letter handling, and event streams with per-consumer offsets, all stored as rows in the same .db file so writes and notifications commit atomically and roll back together. The author chose stat over platform-specific file watchers to preserve cross-platform reliability, noting that high-frequency syscalls remain affordable and that Litestream compatibility is preserved. Named as a playful nod to queue-worker nomenclature, the project targets the “SQLite + Litestream on a VPS” pattern and explicitly warns that it is alpha software not suited to thousands of concurrent listeners.
>
> **Discussion:** A central theme is whether cross-process notification is mostly valuable in languages with process-based concurrency, where threads cannot easily coordinate a single SQLite writer, versus environments that can manage notification semantics in-application with concurrent queues. Technical debate focused on the choice of stat(2) over alternatives such as PRAGMA data_version, io_uring, or filesystem watchers like inotify and FSEvents, with commenters highlighting hidden costs of repeated syscalls and platform-specific pitfalls such as macOS silently dropping same-process notifications. Atomicity of notifications with SQLite transactions emerged as a decisive advantage over external IPC or brokers, since rollbacks cleanly drop queued events and avoid the “sent but rolled back” problem. Community reaction skews positive among maintainers of proliferating small SQLite-backed apps, who appreciate a queue and pub/sub story that avoids installing Postgres, while caveats center on scale limits, thundering-herd effects at thousands of listeners, and the single-machine design. Pragmatic notes on real-world usage include confirmation that Litestream coexists peacefully and that integration with common ORMs remains a practical next step.
