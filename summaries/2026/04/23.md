## [XOR'ing a register with itself is the idiom for zeroing it out. Why not sub?](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/20260421-00/?p=112247)
**Score:** 207 | **Comments:** 203 | **ID:** 47859861

> **Article:** Raymond Chen explains why the classic assembly idiom “xor reg, reg” is used to zero a register instead of “sub reg, reg”. He notes that XOR clears a register without affecting flags and historically required fewer transistors and less time because subtraction needs carry propagation. The article also mentions that many modern CPUs treat both instructions as zero‑cost operations, and that some architectures provide a dedicated zero register, making the trick unnecessary.
>
> **Discussion:** Commenters largely agreed that XOR was historically faster due to the lack of carry logic, citing simple gate‑level explanations and examples like the Cray‑1 where ADD/SUB took more cycles than XOR. Others pointed out that on many 8‑bit CPUs such as the Z80 and 6502 the two instructions have identical latency, and on modern x86 both compile to the same micro‑ops with one‑cycle latency, making the performance difference negligible. The thread also explored why no “mov reg, 0” existed early on, the presence of zero registers in RISC designs (MIPS, RISC‑V, ARM64), and special cases like Itanium’s NaT bit. A side debate touched on energy consumption versus instruction‑decode overhead, while a few participants veered into unrelated tangents about chirality and CPU architecture history. Overall, the consensus was that the XOR idiom persisted for historical reasons, even though contemporary hardware no longer gains a measurable speed advantage.
