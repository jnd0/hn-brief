# HN Daily Digest - 2026-02-28

We Will Not Be Divided - The Atlantic
The recent controversy around OpenAI and Anthropic's contracts with the Department of War has spiraled into something much larger. A group of AI researchers and developers has launched a manifesto at notdivided.org, calling for a global movement toward fully transparent AI labs. The site outlines a collective stance against the DoW's request to embed AI models in classified networks, arguing such cooperation would compromise ethical guardrails and enable mass surveillance. It cites Sam Altman's tweet confirming OpenAI's willingness to work under a contract permitting "all lawful uses" of its models, while Anthropic reportedly rejected similar terms.

The piece calls for open-sourcing of code, weights, and financial records to prevent corporate or governmental monopolies on AI. This dovetails with the broader debate sparked by recent events: OpenAI's agreement to deploy models on classified Pentagon networks, Anthropic's designation as a "supply chain risk," and President Trump's ban on Anthropic's use in government systems. The manifesto also references 5 Eyes surveillance agreements as a loophole that renders domestic privacy laws ineffective, suggesting the entire framework of "lawful use" is fundamentally compromised.

The ethical dimension is stark: engineers are being asked to build tools that could enable autonomous weapons or domestic surveillance. One commenter shared the story of Ukrainian colleagues who declined to work on autonomous kill bots, calling it "a straight up murder" they couldn't participate in. The Defense Production Act looms as a tool to compel cooperation, raising fears of talent exodus and the loss of ethical safeguards. Some engineers argue for cutting ties with America entirely, while others insist that board-level signatories are needed to enforce policy. The tension is real: these are precisely the people you need on your team, but they're the ones most likely to leave when asked to compromise their principles.

The conversation has expanded beyond individual companies to question the entire structure of AI development. Should weights be open-sourced to democratize access? Can we prevent monopolistic control while maintaining safety? One perspective suggests building "Copilot for code/HLM for documents" rather than full AGI, arguing that aligned narrow AI could still meaningfully boost productivity without creating existential risks. But others counter that even with alignment, widespread AGI capability would accelerate bioweapon development and destabilize societies.

The pattern that emerges is familiar: when companies talk about being "woke," it often means they refuse to break laws or commit war crimes. The political polarization around these decisions - with terms like "woke" and "radical left" being deployed as weapons - obscures the fundamental question: should AI companies be allowed to refuse military applications, or should they be compelled to serve state interests? The debate reveals a community torn between pragmatic security needs and principled resistance to government overreach. Some argue that companies shouldn't make these decisions at all, that engineers shouldn't be the ones determining military policy. Others maintain that the only way to ensure responsible AI is to build it transparently, with the community able to verify that it won't be misused.

The controversy has also exposed the technical infrastructure underlying these decisions. AWS and GCP will need to revoke Claude access for any entity with DoD/DOE/NSF contracts, including major universities. This creates a cascade effect through the entire tech ecosystem. The geopolitical dimension adds another layer: Europe could emerge as an alternative hub for AI development, attracting talent and companies unwilling to comply with US military demands.

ChatGPT Subscription Cancellations and Local AI Alternatives
As the political battles rage in corporate boardrooms, individual users are voting with their wallets. Multiple threads detail the process of canceling ChatGPT subscriptions, with many users citing ethical concerns about OpenAI's government contracts as their motivation. The frustration is palpable: some users report hitting "too many failed attempts" errors and disabled delete buttons when trying to close accounts, underscoring how difficult it is to actually leave the platform.

This exodus has accelerated interest in local AI alternatives. The thread on canceling ChatGPT subscriptions quickly pivots to recommendations for running models locally using Qwen3.5 and Mistral models. Cost-effective hardware options like Mac Mini or newer GPUs dominate the conversation. Users share personal experiences with hardware cooling challenges and the financial implications of AI usage. There's a strong emphasis on personal responsibility and principles in making tech decisions, especially in light of high costs and environmental concerns.

The technical barriers are real but surmountable. One user notes that with a modest M1 Mac Mini, they can run a capable 7B model for coding assistance. Another describes using Ollama for a polished local experience that outperforms some commercial offerings. The financial math becomes compelling: instead of paying recurring subscription fees, users can invest in hardware once and run models indefinitely. The discussion reflects a growing sentiment that AI should be accessible without surveillance or corporate control, with some users even exploring crowd-funded open-source training runs.

The parallel between these individual choices and the corporate ethics debates is striking. Just as engineers wrestle with whether to build weapons systems, consumers are deciding whether to support companies that provide those systems. The thread reveals a tension between convenience and principle, with some users acknowledging they'd return to ChatGPT if local alternatives couldn't match its capabilities. But others argue that once you experience truly private, self-hosted AI, there's no going back to the corporate version.

Artemis Program Overhaul and SpaceX Competition
NASA's Artemis program, which has consumed $92 billion over 14 years and produced exactly one launch, is undergoing a major restructuring amid safety concerns and delays. The overhaul aims to accelerate development while addressing technical and budgetary challenges, though the specific changes remain under wraps. The stark contrast between NASA's current approach and SpaceX's rapid, iterative testing model looms large over the conversation.

Commenters are sharply divided on whether NASA should emulate SpaceX's fast-failure, low-cost philosophy or stick with the more cautious, quality-focused Space Launch System path. Several voices point to the $92 billion price tag and single launch as evidence of inefficiency, while others argue that Starship remains far from flight-ready and that SLS's recent Moon orbit proves NASA's approach can work. The debate touches on broader concerns about bureaucracy, political pressure to meet electoral timelines, and the loss of the "quantity-driven" engineering culture that characterized Apollo.

Some invoke the ceramics classroom story - where students graded on quantity produced higher quality work than those graded on quality - to illustrate how iterative production can yield better results. The discussion reflects a community split between admiration for Apollo's achievements, skepticism of current NASA management, and optimism that commercial practices could revitalize deep-space exploration. The underlying question persists: is space exploration better served by cautious, government-led programs or by aggressive private sector innovation?

The parallel with AI development is hard to ignore. Just as with space exploration, we have a choice between slow, centralized progress and fast, distributed innovation. The Artemis debate reveals similar tensions about risk tolerance, cost efficiency, and the role of government versus private enterprise. In both domains, the question isn't just about technology - it's about values, priorities, and who gets to make the big decisions.

AI Agents and Sandbox Security
The surge in AI coding agents like OpenClaw has sparked intense debate about security and trust. Users question whether such tools can be trusted despite their impressive line-of-code metrics, with concerns about code review, potential misuse, and the risks of allowing AI to modify critical systems. The discussion reveals a fundamental tension: AI agents promise enormous productivity gains but introduce new vulnerabilities.

Technical concerns abound. How do you audit what an AI agent does? What happens when it makes a subtle, dangerous change that's hard to detect? Some argue that trust should be placed in AI agents versus human oversight, emphasizing the need for transparency, accountability, and robust safeguards. Skills, custom code, and audit trails are raised as critical requirements, with some advocating for cautious integration and others stressing the importance of human-in-the-loop processes.

The sandboxing approaches discussed range from containers and "YOLO" (just hoping agents behave) to more sophisticated isolation techniques. One commenter mentions Sandvault, a macOS-only tool that uses Unix user accounts with sudo and SSH for isolation. Others point out WebAssembly's limitations for running arbitrary code in different languages, though Python and other interpreters can already run in WASM environments. QubesOS emerges as a strong recommendation for comprehensive sandboxing, praised for its ability to isolate different environments and prevent dependency conflicts.

The conversation underscores the need for balanced approaches that leverage AI without compromising safety or transparency. Some users share their own open-source projects for sandboxing, while others discuss the performance trade-offs of different approaches. The underlying anxiety is clear: as AI agents become more capable, the potential for both benefit and harm grows exponentially.

Passkeys and the Future of Authentication
The push toward passkeys as a replacement for passwords has hit significant resistance. An article titled "Don't use passkeys for encrypting user data" has sparked debate over usability issues like accidental creation, confusion over storage locations, and security risks. The piece highlights HealthEquity's mandatory passkey requirement for HSA/RAs as of October 2025, which caused login failures on Linux with Firefox, forcing reliance on Chrome/Edge.

The discussion centers on passkeys' usability flaws, security trade-offs, and vendor lock-in risks. Users criticize passkeys for accidental creation and device dependency, while defenders argue they're recoverable credentials. Technical debates arise over attestation: some claim it enables OS takeover, while others argue it's unrelated to browsers. The HealthEquity example sparks concern about forced adoption, with some noting that some users avoid passkeys entirely.

Disagreements persist about whether passkeys qualify as 2FA and their role in end-to-end encryption. The thread underscores tensions between convenience, security, and user education. The broader pattern is familiar: new authentication methods promise security improvements but often create new problems around accessibility and control.

This mirrors the AI ethics debates in interesting ways. Just as with AI development, we're seeing a push toward centralized, corporate-controlled solutions (passkeys managed by big tech) versus distributed, user-controlled alternatives (traditional passwords or other methods). The resistance to passkeys reflects a broader skepticism toward technologies that limit user autonomy or create dependencies on specific platforms.

Some argue that the US must match adversaries' AI capabilities, framing militarization as a necessary counterbalance. Others maintain that ethical AI development requires refusing military applications entirely. The debate reveals fundamental disagreements about whether technology companies should serve state interests or maintain independence.

Europe's potential emergence as an alternative hub for AI development could reshape the landscape. If the US continues down the path of military integration, talent and innovation may flow to jurisdictions with different priorities. The question becomes whether we can have both ethical AI and national security, or whether those goals are fundamentally incompatible.
