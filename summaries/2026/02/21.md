## [EU mandates replaceable batteries by 2027 (2023)](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/news/new-law-more-sustainable-circular-and-safe-batteries-enters-force-2023-08-17_en)

> **Article:** The EU’s new Battery Regulation, which entered force on 17 August 2023, obliges all portable batteries sold in the bloc to be removable by the end‑user with commercially available tools, to carry a Battery Passport that records composition and carbon footprint, and to meet stricter recycling and safety standards. The law targets smartphones, laptops, tablets, and other consumer electronics, aiming to reduce planned obsolescence, cut mountain‑sized e‑waste streams, and promote a circular economy by 2027. It also exempts devices that are waterproof and intended for regular immersion, such as some fitness trackers, while requiring manufacturers to provide clear disassembly instructions.
>
> **Discussion:** Commenters praised the EU’s move as a rare example of political leadership that aligns consumer rights with environmental goals, citing the moral failure of exporting toxic e‑waste to Africa and Asia. Skeptics argued that removable batteries would not necessarily extend device lifetimes, noting that most users rarely replace a phone’s battery and that sleek designs already favor sealed units; they also questioned the practicality of the Battery Passport, which they said ties a device to its cell and blocks DIY repairs. Technical insights highlighted that modern batteries last longer and fast‑charge in minutes, reducing the need for spare packs, while others pointed out that heavy users—delivery drivers, teenagers, and budget‑conscious consumers—still cycle batteries twice daily and would benefit from easy swaps. Some participants raised broader concerns about software lock‑in and surveillance, suggesting that true ownership would require open‑source firmware and user‑controlled keys, while others warned that the regulation’s waterproof exemption could leave many phones still non‑removable. The thread also touched on the unintended consequences of similar bans, such as the UK’s disposable‑vape ban that led vendors to add charging ports and refill kits, ultimately creating a new waste stream.
