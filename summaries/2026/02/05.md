## [When internal hostnames are leaked to the clown](https://rachelbythebay.com/w/2026/02/03/badnas/)
**Score:** 366 | **Comments:** 192 | **ID:** 46895972

> **Article:** Rachel by the Bay reports that her Synology NAS’s web UI uses Sentry for error monitoring, and that Sentry transmits client‑side traces which include the device’s internal hostname (e.g., nas.corp‑and‑other‑corp‑merger.example.com) to a Google‑hosted “clown” endpoint. The exposure reveals internal naming conventions that could hint at corporate mergers or other sensitive information, even though the NAS sits behind a private network. She argues that this telemetry is unnecessary and suggests replacing the NAS OS with a trusted open‑source alternative or blocking Sentry calls via DNS filtering. The post serves as a warning about hidden data collection in consumer‑grade network appliances.
>
> **Discussion:** Commenters quickly clarified that “clown” is Rachel’s sarcastic nickname for big‑tech cloud services, especially GCP, and traced the term back to her time at Facebook. The technical debate centered on whether the leak stemmed from Sentry’s client‑side reporting versus Certificate Transparency logs, with several users noting that a wildcard certificate would not expose the “nas” sub‑domain and that Sentry was indeed sending the hostname in its payloads. Many participants expressed frustration with proprietary NAS firmware, recommending open‑source alternatives like TrueNAS, HexOS, or running custom containers, and suggested network‑level blocks such as Pi‑hole to stop telemetry. Others argued that internal hostnames are inherently public once they appear in DNS or URLs, emphasizing that privacy expectations should focus on data rather than naming, while still acknowledging the annoyance of unsolicited tracking. The thread also touched on broader concerns about Let’s Encrypt and CT logs exposing domains to automated scanning and attacks.

Technical debate surfaced around retrieval‑augmented generation (RAG); some, such as baby and m00dy, claimed RAG breaks with large data sets, while simonw and thunky pointed out that modern LLMs with huge context windows and integrated search make traditional vector‑based RAG less necessary.
